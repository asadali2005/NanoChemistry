1. Overview of Characterization Techniques

Nanoparticles (1–100 nm range) possess fundamentally different properties from their bulk counterparts — including higher surface area, quantum effects, and altered optical/magnetic behavior. To understand these materials, we use characterization techniques that probe different dimensions of a nanoparticle's identity.

🔷 Definition Characterization of nanoparticles refers to the use of analytical techniques to determine the structural, morphological, chemical, and physical properties of nanoscale materials. Each technique reveals a unique, complementary dimension.

📡 IR Spectroscopy

Uses infrared light to detect vibrations of chemical bonds. Identifies functional groups, metal–oxygen bonds, surface molecules, and residual ligands. Confirms the chemical identity of the nanoparticle and the success of synthesis.

🔭 TEM (Transmission Electron Microscopy)

Transmits a beam of electrons through an ultra-thin sample. Provides atomic-level imaging, direct size measurement, morphology, and crystal lattice information. Highest resolution technique.

🖥️ SEM (Scanning Electron Microscopy)

Scans a focused electron beam across the specimen surface. Reveals surface topography, morphology, aggregation behavior, and (with EDS) elemental composition.

📊 XRD (X-Ray Diffraction)

Diffracts X-rays through crystalline materials. Determines phase purity, crystal structure, crystallite size (via Scherrer equation), and lattice parameters.

Technique What It Probes Key Output Unique Strength
IR / FT-IR Chemical bonds, functional groups Absorption peaks (cm⁻¹) Identifies surface chemistry & ligands
TEM Interior structure, lattice planes Atomic-resolution images, SAED patterns True size + crystal lattice at atomic scale
SEM Surface topography, morphology Greyscale surface images Aggregation behavior, surface texture
XRD Crystal planes, long-range order 2θ diffraction pattern, d-spacings Phase ID + crystallite size (non-destructive)

Section Summary

  • Each technique is complementary — no single technique provides complete information.
  • IR → chemical identity; TEM → atomic-scale imaging; SEM → surface morphology; XRD → crystal structure.
  • Together, these four techniques give a complete picture of nanoparticle identity, structure, and properties.

2. Synthesis of Co₃O₄ Nanoparticles

🔷 Background Co₃O₄ (cobalt(II,III) oxide) is a mixed-valence transition metal oxide with a cubic spinel structure. It is used in photocatalysis, energy storage, gas sensing, and magnetic applications. Its nanoparticle form shows enhanced catalytic activity due to high surface area.

Synthesis Method: Thermal Decomposition

The nanoparticles in the referenced study were prepared by the thermal decomposition of the precursor complex [Co(NH₃)₅(H₂O)](NO₃)₃.

Precursor Complex Formation

The cobalt complex Co(NH₃)₅(H₂O)₃ is synthesized. This complex contains Co²⁺/Co³⁺ coordinated to five ammonia (NH₃) ligands and one water (H₂O) ligand, with nitrate (NO₃⁻) as the counter anion.

Thermal Decomposition

The complex is heated to 150°C and 175°C. Heat drives off all coordinated ligands (NH₃, H₂O) and the nitrate anions, leaving behind cobalt and oxygen atoms that reorganize into the Co₃O₄ spinel lattice.

Reaction (Simplified) [Co(NH₃)₅(H₂O)](NO₃)₃ → Co₃O₄ + NH₃↑ + H₂O↑ + NO₂↑ + O₂↑

Nanoparticle Formation

The rapid decomposition of the precursor limits crystal growth, yielding nanoparticles in the 5–30 nm range. The porous morphology arises from gas evolution during thermal decomposition.

📘 Why Thermal Decomposition? Thermal decomposition is simple, scalable, and does not require complex reducing agents or solvents. The precursor complex acts as a "pre-organized" source of cobalt, enabling controlled nanoparticle formation.

Precursor: Co(NH₃)₅(H₂O)₃

  • Contains NH₃ ligands (confirmed by IR)
  • Contains NO₃⁻ counter anions
  • Contains coordinated H₂O
  • All confirmed by FT-IR before heating

Product: Co₃O₄

  • Cubic spinel crystal structure
  • Co²⁺ in tetrahedral sites, Co³⁺ in octahedral sites
  • No residual ligands (confirmed by IR)
  • Average size ≈ 17.5 nm (confirmed by TEM)

Section Summary

  • Co₃O₄ is synthesized by thermal decomposition of [Co(NH₃)₅(H₂O)](NO₃)₃ complex.
  • Heating at 150°C and 175°C removes all ligands, forming pure Co₃O₄ nanoparticles.
  • The characterization techniques (IR, TEM, SEM, XRD) are then used to confirm and study the product.

3. IR Spectroscopy — Principles & Theory

🔷 Definition Infrared (IR) Spectroscopy is an analytical technique that measures the absorption of infrared light by a sample. When IR radiation interacts with a molecule, it causes bonds to vibrate (stretch or bend) at specific frequencies. The resulting absorption spectrum is a unique molecular "fingerprint."

The Electromagnetic Spectrum & IR Region

IR radiation occupies the region between visible light and microwaves:

IR RegionWavenumber Range (cm⁻¹)Use
Near-IR (NIR)14,000 – 4,000Overtones, combination bands
Mid-IR (MIR)4,000 – 400Main functional group analysis — most used
Far-IR (FIR)400 – 10Metal–oxygen bonds, lattice vibrations
📘 Key Point For metal oxide nanoparticles, the most important peaks are in the 400–700 cm⁻¹ region (Far/Low Mid-IR), where metal–oxygen stretching vibrations occur. These peaks directly confirm nanoparticle formation.

How IR Works — The Fundamental Principle

Molecular Vibrations

All chemical bonds vibrate continuously. The frequency of vibration depends on two factors: (1) the masses of the atoms and (2) the strength (force constant) of the bond. This is described by:

Hooke's Law (Bond Vibration)
ν̃ = (1/2πc) √(k/μ)

where ν̃ = wavenumber (cm⁻¹), c = speed of light, k = force constant (bond strength), μ = reduced mass of the two bonded atoms.

IR Absorption Condition

For a bond to absorb IR radiation, the vibration must cause a change in the dipole moment of the molecule. Symmetric vibrations that do not change the dipole are IR-inactive.

Resonance Absorption

When the frequency of IR light matches the natural vibration frequency of a bond, the bond absorbs that energy, causing an increased amplitude of vibration. This absorption is recorded as a dip in the transmittance spectrum (or a peak in the absorbance spectrum).

Types of Bond Vibrations

Stretching Vibrations

Symmetric stretch: Both atoms move away from/toward each other simultaneously along the bond axis.

Asymmetric stretch: One atom moves away while the other moves toward the center.

Bending Vibrations

In-plane bending: Scissoring, rocking — movement within the plane of the molecule.

Out-of-plane bending: Wagging, twisting — movement perpendicular to the molecular plane.

FT-IR vs Dispersive IR

Modern instruments use Fourier Transform IR (FT-IR) rather than the older dispersive (grating) spectrometers:

FeatureFT-IRDispersive IR
SpeedSeconds (entire spectrum at once)Minutes (scanned wavelength by wavelength)
SensitivityMuch higher (Fellgett advantage)Lower
ResolutionHigh, adjustableLimited
Signal averagingEasy — multiple scansSlow
Use in nanoparticlesStandard for all modern studiesObsolete

Section Summary

  • IR spectroscopy measures bond vibration frequencies by recording which wavelengths of IR light are absorbed.
  • Absorption requires a change in dipole moment (IR-active vibrations).
  • Wavenumber (cm⁻¹) = reciprocal of wavelength — higher wavenumber = higher frequency = stronger/lighter bond.
  • FT-IR is the modern standard — simultaneous full spectrum, fast, high sensitivity.
  • Metal–oxygen bonds appear at 400–700 cm⁻¹ in the mid/far-IR.

4. IR Spectroscopy — What It Identifies in Nanoparticles

Key Information from IR of Metal Oxide Nanoparticles

1. Metal–Oxygen Bonds

Peaks in the 400–700 cm⁻¹ region confirm the formation of metal–oxygen bonds. This is the definitive confirmation that a metal oxide nanoparticle has formed. Different metal oxides show slightly different positions in this range.

2. Surface –OH Groups & H₂O

Broad absorption around 3200–3500 cm⁻¹ (O–H stretch) and ~1630 cm⁻¹ (H–O–H bend) indicates surface hydroxyl groups or adsorbed water. Nanoparticles have high surface area → more OH groups than bulk.

3. Organic Ligands & Residues

Peaks from synthesis ligands (NH₃: ~3300 cm⁻¹, ~1600 cm⁻¹; NO₃⁻: ~1380 cm⁻¹, ~820 cm⁻¹) indicate residual organics from synthesis. Their disappearance after heating confirms clean product.

4. Peak Shifts Due to Size

In nanoparticles, metal–oxygen bond peaks can shift slightly compared to bulk due to phonon confinement effects and increased surface strain. This confirms nanoscale nature.

5. Broad Peaks = Hydrogen Bonding / Disorder

Broad, diffuse peaks indicate hydrogen bonding networks or structural disorder. Sharp peaks indicate well-ordered crystalline material.

6. Impurity Detection

Any unexpected absorption peaks reveal unwanted chemicals or by-products from synthesis — critical for quality control.

⚠️ Common Mistake Students often confuse "peaks" in transmittance spectra (which are actually dips/minima) with absorbance peaks. In transmittance mode, a downward dip = absorption. In absorbance mode (inverted), peaks go upward. Always note which mode the spectrum is displayed in.

5. FT-IR Results: Co₃O₄ Nanoparticles

Three Spectra Compared (Figure 2 from Reference)

The study recorded FT-IR spectra at three stages: (a) original complex, (b) decomposition at 150°C, (c) decomposition at 175°C.

🔴 Before Heating — Spectrum (a)

The precursor complex [Co(NH₃)₅(H₂O)](NO₃)₃ shows multiple peaks:

  • NH₃ + H₂O peaks: broad absorptions at ~3200–3400 cm⁻¹ (N–H and O–H stretches)
  • NO₃⁻ peaks: characteristic nitrate peaks at ~1380 cm⁻¹ and ~820 cm⁻¹
  • NH₃ + NO₃⁻ combination bands visible
  • Multiple overlapping peaks from ligands
  • No Co–O peaks yet (nanoparticles not yet formed)

🟢 After Heating — Spectra (b) & (c)

After thermal decomposition at 150°C and 175°C:

  • ALL ligand peaks disappear — NH₃, H₂O, NO₃⁻ all removed by heating
  • Co–O peaks appear at 560–660 cm⁻¹ — two characteristic bands of Co₃O₄
  • Spectrum (c) at 175°C shows sharper, more intense Co–O peaks
  • Confirms successful formation of Co₃O₄ spinel oxide
  • No residual ligands = pure product
✅ Key Interpretation The disappearance of ligand peaks + appearance of Co–O bond peaks at 560–660 cm⁻¹ is the definitive FT-IR evidence of successful thermal decomposition of the precursor into Co₃O₄ nanoparticles. This is the "graph tells us" takeaway.

Why Two Co–O Peaks?

Co₃O₄ has a spinel structure (AB₂O₄) where Co²⁺ occupies tetrahedral (A) sites and Co³⁺ occupies octahedral (B) sites. These two different coordination environments produce two distinct Co–O stretching bands:

Peak ~660 cm⁻¹

Corresponds to Co³⁺–O stretching in the octahedral (B-site) coordination environment.

Peak ~560 cm⁻¹

Corresponds to Co²⁺–O stretching in the tetrahedral (A-site) coordination environment.

Section Summary

  • Before heating: Multiple ligand peaks (NH₃, H₂O, NO₃⁻) confirm the precursor complex structure.
  • After heating: All ligand peaks vanish; Co–O peaks at 560–660 cm⁻¹ appear.
  • Two Co–O peaks arise from the two coordination environments in the spinel structure (Co²⁺ tetrahedral + Co³⁺ octahedral).
  • FT-IR is the primary tool for confirming synthesis success and phase purity by chemical bond identification.

7. IR Spectroscopy — Applications in Nanoparticle Research

💊 Medicine — Drug Delivery

IR confirms surface functionalization of nanoparticles for targeted drug delivery. When a drug or targeting ligand is attached to a nanoparticle surface, new IR peaks appear. This verifies successful conjugation without chemically destroying the sample.

⚡ Energy — Solar Cells & Batteries

IR verifies metal oxide composition in electrode materials. For example, in lithium-ion batteries, metal oxide electrode materials must have specific bonding confirmed by IR. Solar cell photoanodes (e.g., TiO₂, ZnO) are characterized by IR to confirm phase and surface groups.

🌿 Environment — Pollution Control

IR identifies surface functional groups responsible for catalytic degradation of pollutants. Metal oxide nanoparticles used as photocatalysts are characterized by IR before and after degradation experiments to understand reaction mechanisms.

🔍 Sensors — Gas Detection

IR confirms the surface chemistry of metal oxide gas sensors. Selective gas adsorption depends on surface –OH groups and metal–O bonds. IR characterizes these before and after gas exposure to understand sensing mechanisms.

✅ Synthesis Confirmation

The most direct application — IR confirms that synthesis has proceeded correctly by showing the appearance of M–O peaks and disappearance of precursor ligand peaks. Acts as a quick quality control tool.

🔬 Studying Chemical Behavior

In situ IR can monitor chemical reactions in real time as they happen on nanoparticle surfaces, revealing intermediates and reaction mechanisms in catalytic processes.

Section Summary — IR Spectroscopy Complete

  • IR spectroscopy is versatile: confirms synthesis, identifies surface chemistry, detects impurities, and studies reactions.
  • Applications span medicine, energy, environment, and sensors.
  • FT-IR is the standard modern implementation — fast, sensitive, non-destructive.
  • For Co₃O₄: peaks at 560–660 cm⁻¹ are the definitive signature.

8. TEM — Principles & Background

🔷 Definition Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM) is a microscopy technique in which a beam of high-energy electrons is transmitted through an ultra-thin specimen (typically <100 nm thick). The interaction of electrons with the sample produces an image at atomic-level resolution — far beyond the capability of light microscopes.

Why Electrons, Not Light?

The resolution of any microscope is fundamentally limited by the wavelength of the radiation used (Rayleigh criterion: resolution ≈ λ/2). Visible light has wavelengths of 400–700 nm, limiting resolution to ~200 nm. Electrons, when accelerated to high voltages, have much shorter de Broglie wavelengths:

de Broglie Wavelength of Electrons
λ = h / (mv) = h / √(2meV)

At 100 kV accelerating voltage, λ ≈ 0.0037 nm — ~100,000× shorter than visible light!

📘 Why TEM Needs Ultra-Thin Samples Electrons are easily stopped by matter (unlike X-rays). The sample must be thin enough (<100 nm, ideally <50 nm) for electrons to actually pass through and reach the detector. Thick samples cause too much scattering and produce no usable image.

Key Operating Parameters

ParameterTypical ValueEffect
Accelerating Voltage80–300 kVHigher V → shorter λ → better resolution, but more beam damage
Sample Thickness<100 nmMust be thin enough for electron transmission
Resolution0.1–0.2 nm (HRTEM)Atomic columns visible
Vacuum~10⁻⁷ TorrPrevents electron scattering by air molecules

9. TEM — Working Principle & Image Formation

Step-by-Step Process Flow

1. Electron Gun
Produces high-voltage electrons (thermionic or field emission)
↓
2. Anode
Accelerates electrons to high voltage (80–300 kV)
↓
3. Condenser Lenses
Focus and collimate the electron beam; condenser aperture limits beam
↓
4. Ultra-Thin Sample (on Grid)
Electrons pass through; some are scattered, some transmitted
↓
5. Objective Lens
Forms the first magnified image; objective aperture controls contrast
↓
6. Selected Area Aperture
Selects specific area for diffraction (SAED)
↓
7. Projector Lenses
Further magnify the image
↓
8. Detectors / Viewing Screen / CCD Camera
BF, ADF, HAADF detectors; EELS spectrometer; CCD camera records image

Mechanism of Image Formation — Contrast

The contrast in a TEM image comes from differential scattering of electrons by the sample. Regions with more electrons (heavier atoms, thicker regions) scatter more electrons away from the beam, appearing dark (in bright-field mode). Regions with fewer electrons appear bright.

Elastic Scattering
Main imaging mechanism

Electrons change direction without energy loss. Creates image contrast. Forms the basis of bright-field and dark-field imaging.

Inelastic Scattering
Diffraction & spectroscopy

Electrons lose energy to the sample (phonons, plasmons). Contributes to diffraction patterns (SAED) and EELS (Electron Energy Loss Spectroscopy).

Bright-Field (BF) Imaging
Standard imaging

Uses the direct transmitted beam. Dense/thick areas appear dark; empty/thin areas appear bright. Most common TEM mode.

Dark-Field (DF) Imaging
Crystal features

Uses scattered/diffracted electrons. Crystalline regions satisfying diffraction conditions appear bright. Highlights crystal grains and defects.

10. Types of TEM Techniques

CTEM — Conventional TEM

The standard TEM mode. Provides bright-field images of nanoparticle morphology, size, and distribution. Resolution typically 0.3–1 nm. Used for routine nanoparticle sizing and shape analysis.

HRTEM — High-Resolution TEM

Achieves sub-Ångström resolution. Can directly image atomic columns and crystal lattice planes. Phase contrast imaging. Essential for confirming crystal structure, measuring d-spacings, and studying defects at atomic scale. Requires aberration-corrected instruments for best results.

SAED — Selected Area Electron Diffraction

A diffraction pattern is obtained from a selected region of the sample. Produces rings (polycrystalline) or spots (single crystal). Ring patterns can be used to determine crystal structure and d-spacings — complementary to XRD but from nanoscale regions.

STEM — Scanning Transmission EM

A focused electron probe is scanned point by point across the sample (like SEM, but in transmission). STEM with HAADF (High-Angle Annular Dark-Field) detector gives Z-contrast imaging (heavier atoms appear brighter). Combined with EDS and EELS for elemental mapping at atomic scale.

📘 TEM vs SAED vs XRD SAED gives crystal structure information from a nanoscale region (same particle you are imaging), while XRD gives average structure from millions of particles in bulk powder. SAED is essential when you want to know the crystal structure of a specific individual nanoparticle.

TEM vs XRD: Particle Size vs Crystallite Size

📘 Important Distinction

TEM particle size = actual physical dimension of the whole nanoparticle as seen in the image.

XRD crystallite size (Scherrer) = size of a single coherently diffracting crystal domain within the particle.

A nanoparticle may be a single crystal (TEM size ≈ XRD size) or may consist of multiple crystallite domains (TEM size > XRD size). Comparing both gives insight into internal crystal structure.

Section Summary

  • TEM confirms spherical morphology of Co₃O₄ nanoparticles with size range 5–30 nm, average ~17.5 nm.
  • TEM provides direct visual evidence of individual nanoparticles — highest resolution of all four techniques.
  • TEM size and XRD crystallite size can differ if particles are polycrystalline.
  • HRTEM can additionally reveal lattice planes and confirm crystal structure visually.

13. SEM — History, Principle & Signal Types

🔷 Definition Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) is a technique that uses a focused beam of electrons scanned across the surface of a sample to produce detailed images of surface topography, morphology, and (with EDS) elemental composition. Unlike TEM, electrons do not pass through the sample — they interact with the surface and near-surface regions.

Historical Milestones

1942

First SEM Prototype

Vladimir Zworykin demonstrated the first working prototype SEM. Resolution was poor by today's standards, but the fundamental principle was established.

1965

First Commercial SEM

Cambridge Instruments (UK) launched the first commercially available SEM. This made the technique accessible to research laboratories worldwide and revolutionized materials science.

1980s

Environmental SEM (ESEM)

Environmental SEM was introduced, allowing wet, non-conductive, and biological samples to be imaged without requiring the vacuum conditions of conventional SEM or metallic coating.

Today

Sub-nm Resolution & EDS/EBSD Integration

Modern FE-SEM (Field Emission SEM) achieves sub-nanometer resolution. Integration with EDS (Energy Dispersive X-ray Spectroscopy) gives elemental maps, and EBSD (Electron Backscatter Diffraction) provides crystal orientation maps — enabling full compositional and structural analysis from a single instrument.

Four Signal Types Produced by SEM

When the electron beam strikes the sample, multiple types of signals are generated simultaneously:

Secondary Electrons (SE)
KE < 50 eV

Generated near the surface (<10 nm depth). Low energy. Most common SEM signal — used for topography imaging. High resolution. Emitted from regions directly under the beam.

Backscattered Electrons (BSE)
KE > 50 eV

Elastically reflected from deeper in the sample. Z-contrast: heavier elements → more BSE → appear brighter. Used for compositional contrast imaging. Lower resolution than SE.

Auger Electrons
Surface chemistry

Emitted from the outermost surface (1–3 nm). Element-specific energies. Combined with Auger Electron Spectroscopy (AES) for surface chemical analysis. Very surface-sensitive.

Cathodoluminescence (CL)
Optical emission

Visible/UV photons emitted when excited electrons relax. Reveals band gaps, defects, and dopant distributions in semiconductors and minerals. Optical emission mapped spatially.

📘 Also: X-Ray Signals (EDS/EDX) When the electron beam ionizes inner-shell electrons, characteristic X-rays are emitted as outer electrons fill the vacancies. These X-rays are characteristic of each element and are detected by EDS (Energy Dispersive X-ray Spectroscopy) to perform elemental analysis and mapping. This is an extremely important SEM add-on capability.
⚙️

14. SEM — Working Mechanism & Process Flow

Step-by-Step SEM Process

Sample Preparation

Clean, dry specimen is required. Non-conductive samples (like metal oxide nanoparticle powders) must be coated with a thin conductive layer (Au/Pd, ~10 nm) via sputter coating. This prevents charge accumulation that would distort the image. (Environmental SEM avoids this requirement.)

Electron Generation

Thermionic emission (heated tungsten filament or LaB₆ crystal) or field emission (FE-SEM — sharp tip, no heating). Electrons are accelerated by a high-voltage anode (typically 1–30 kV for SEM).

Beam Focusing

Condenser lenses reduce (demagnify) the beam cross-section. Objective lens focuses the beam to a fine spot (1–20 nm diameter) on the sample surface. Condenser aperture controls beam current and coherence.

Raster Scanning

X-Y scan coils (electromagnetic deflection coils) move the focused beam point by point across the sample surface in a raster (grid) pattern. The beam dwells at each point for a fraction of a millisecond.

Signal Detection

SE signals are collected by an Everhart-Thornley (ET) detector (scintillator + photomultiplier). BSE signals by a solid-state detector above the sample. X-rays by an EDS detector. All signals are processed simultaneously.

Image Construction

The intensity of the detected signal at each scan point is mapped to a pixel brightness value, constructing a greyscale SEM image. Higher signal intensity → brighter pixel → protruding surface features appear bright.

⚠️ Common Mistake SEM images are NOT photographs. They are maps of electron signal intensity. The "3D" appearance comes from the angular dependence of SE emission — surfaces facing the detector emit more SE and appear brighter. The contrast is topographic, not photographic.

17. XRD — Principles, Background & History

🔷 Definition X-Ray Diffraction (XRD) is a non-destructive analytical technique that determines the crystallographic structure, chemical composition, and physical properties of solid crystalline materials by measuring the diffraction of X-rays by crystal planes. It provides a unique "fingerprint" pattern for each crystalline phase.

Historical Discovery

1912

Max von Laue — First XRD Observation

Max von Laue first observed X-ray diffraction from a crystal of copper sulfate. He proposed that crystals, with their regular atomic arrangement, could act as a diffraction grating for X-rays. This earned him the Nobel Prize in Physics (1914).

1913

W.H. Bragg & W.L. Bragg — Bragg's Law

Father and son William Henry and William Lawrence Bragg formulated Bragg's Law (nλ = 2d sinθ), providing a simple mathematical description of the conditions for constructive interference of diffracted X-rays. They shared the Nobel Prize in Physics (1915) — William Lawrence Bragg remains the youngest Nobel laureate in Physics at age 25.

Why X-Rays for Crystals?

📘 Key Physical Insight X-rays have wavelengths in the range 0.01–10 Å (0.001–1 nm). The interatomic spacings in crystals are typically 1–5 Å. Because the wavelength of X-rays is comparable to the spacing between atomic planes, crystals act as 3D diffraction gratings for X-rays — producing measurable diffraction patterns from which structure can be determined.

How Crystals Diffract X-Rays

A crystalline material consists of atoms arranged in a periodic, repeating 3D lattice. X-rays scattered by atoms in adjacent planes can interfere constructively or destructively:

X-Rays Hit Crystal

An incident X-ray beam strikes the crystal surface at angle θ (Bragg angle). Parallel rays hit successive parallel atomic planes separated by distance d (d-spacing).

Scattering from Planes

Each atomic plane reflects a fraction of the X-ray beam. The beam reflected from the lower plane travels an extra path length of 2d sinθ compared to the beam reflected from the upper plane.

Constructive Interference → Diffraction Peak

When the extra path length equals a whole number of wavelengths (nλ), the two reflected beams are in phase → constructive interference → a diffraction peak is recorded at angle 2θ. At all other angles, destructive interference occurs and no peak is observed.

📘 Non-Destructive Advantage XRD is completely non-destructive — the X-rays scatter from the sample without altering it chemically or structurally. The same sample can be used for multiple measurements and other techniques.
📐

18. Bragg's Law — Deep Dive

Bragg's Law
nλ = 2d sinθ

Variable-by-Variable Explanation

Symbol Name Units Physical Meaning
n Order of diffraction Integer (1, 2, 3...) Number of wavelengths in the path difference. Usually n=1 (first order) in practice.
λ X-ray wavelength Å or nm Fixed by the X-ray source. Cu Kα = 1.54 Å (most common). Mo Kα = 0.71 Å.
d Interplanar d-spacing Å Distance between adjacent parallel crystal planes (hkl). Unique for each crystal phase → "fingerprint".
θ Bragg angle degrees Angle between the incident X-ray beam and the crystal plane surface. (Note: the detector records at 2θ.)

Deriving Bragg's Law — Geometric Proof

Consider two parallel X-ray beams (Wave 1 and Wave 2) hitting parallel crystal planes (separated by d) at angle θ:

  1. Wave 1 reflects from the top plane. Wave 2 hits the bottom plane at point B, reflects, and returns.
  2. The extra distance traveled by Wave 2 = path ABC = BC + CB.
  3. From geometry: BC = d sinθ, so path difference = 2d sinθ.
  4. For constructive interference: path difference = whole number of wavelengths → 2d sinθ = nλ.
Therefore nλ = 2d sinθ  (Bragg's Law) — condition for diffraction peak to appear.

Practical Use: Calculating d-Spacing

From experimental XRD data (measured 2θ values and known λ), the d-spacing is calculated:

d-spacing from Bragg's Law
d = nλ / (2 sinθ)
📘 XRD Pattern = Fingerprint Each crystalline material has a unique set of d-spacings (determined by its unit cell dimensions and atomic arrangement). The XRD pattern (plot of intensity vs 2θ) is therefore a unique fingerprint for each phase. This pattern is compared against the ICDD PDF-4 database (International Centre for Diffraction Data) for phase identification.

Miller Indices (hkl) and Crystal Planes

Each set of parallel crystal planes is described by Miller indices (hkl) — three integers that specify the plane orientation relative to the unit cell axes. In XRD, peaks are labeled by their (hkl) indices (e.g., (311), (220), (400) for Co₃O₄).

📘 Co₃O₄ XRD Peaks The XRD pattern of Co₃O₄ shows characteristic peaks at 2θ positions labeled: (111), (220), (311), (222), (400), (422), (511), (440), (533). The (311) peak is the most intense — used as the primary identification peak. These match the cubic spinel structure (Fd3̄m space group).

19. The Scherrer Equation — Crystallite Size from XRD

Scherrer Equation
D = Kλ / (β cosθ)

Variable-by-Variable Explanation

Symbol Name Value/Units Physical Meaning
D Crystallite size nm The average size of coherently diffracting crystal domains. This is the XRD "crystallite size" — may differ from TEM particle size.
K Scherrer shape factor ~0.89–1.0 (dimensionless) Depends on crystal shape. K = 0.9 is standard for spherical particles with FWHM. K = 1.0 for cubic crystallites.
λ X-ray wavelength nm (or Å) Same as in Bragg's Law. Cu Kα = 0.154 nm.
β Peak broadening (FWHM) radians Full Width at Half Maximum of the diffraction peak in radians. Smaller D → broader peak → larger β.
θ Bragg angle degrees (convert to radians for β) Half the 2θ position of the peak center.

Why Does Peak Broadening Indicate Smaller Crystallites?

📘 Physical Explanation In a very large crystal, many atomic planes contribute to diffraction. Rays from each plane must cancel at angles slightly off the Bragg angle — requiring many planes for complete destructive interference. For a small crystallite, there are fewer planes, so destructive interference at off-Bragg angles is incomplete → diffraction persists over a broader angular range → broader peak (larger β).

Think of it like this: larger crystallite = sharper peak; smaller crystallite = broader peak.

Important Limitations of the Scherrer Equation

⚠️ Limitations
  • Gives crystallite size, not particle size. A particle can contain multiple crystallites.
  • Peak broadening also arises from microstrain (lattice distortions). The Scherrer equation ignores strain. The Williamson-Hall plot separates size and strain contributions.
  • Only valid for crystallite sizes roughly 2–100 nm. Above this range, peaks are too sharp for meaningful FWHM measurement.
  • Assumes no instrumental broadening (must subtract instrument contribution from β).

Example Calculation

Sample Calculation — Co₃O₄

For the (311) peak of Co₃O₄ (most intense peak in the XRD pattern):

  • λ = 0.154 nm (Cu Kα)
  • 2θ ≈ 36.8° → θ ≈ 18.4° → cosθ ≈ 0.949
  • β (FWHM) ≈ measured from the peak (example: 0.6° = 0.01047 rad)
  • K = 0.9
  • D = (0.9 × 0.154) / (0.01047 × 0.949) = 0.1386 / 0.00994 ≈ 13.9 nm

This crystallite size should be compared with the TEM average particle size (~17.5 nm) — if XRD < TEM, particles may be polycrystalline.

Section Summary

  • Scherrer equation: D = Kλ / (β cosθ) — calculates crystallite size from XRD peak broadening.
  • Smaller crystallites → broader peaks (larger β).
  • K ≈ 0.9 for spherical particles; λ = X-ray wavelength; β in radians; θ = Bragg angle.
  • Detects nanocrystalline materials in the 2–100 nm range.
  • Limitations: does not include microstrain; gives crystallite not particle size.

20. XRD — Experimental Setup

X-Ray Tube (Source)

Cu or Mo anode generates bremsstrahlung (broad continuous) + characteristic X-rays. Most common: Cu anode → Cu Kα radiation at λ = 1.54 Å (0.154 nm). The characteristic Kα line is produced when an outer electron fills the vacancy left by inner-shell (K-shell) electron ejection.

📘 Why Cu Kα? Copper is cheap, produces intense characteristic X-rays, and the 1.54 Å wavelength is ideal for most d-spacings in common materials (1–5 Å). Mo Kα (0.71 Å) is used for hard-to-penetrate materials.

Monochromator

Selects a single wavelength (Kα line) from the polychromatic X-ray beam. Uses a graphite crystal or a nickel filter. Reduces background noise and eliminates unwanted wavelengths (Kβ, bremsstrahlung). Essential for accurate peak positions and d-spacing calculations.

Sample Stage (Goniometer)

The goniometer is a precision rotation mechanism that controls the angle θ. For powder XRD: the sample is a finely ground powder pressed onto a flat holder. For single crystal: mounted on a spindle. The goniometer rotates at precise angles to systematically scan through all Bragg conditions (θ–2θ scan).

📘 Powder vs Single Crystal XRD Powder XRD (most common for nanoparticles): Randomly oriented crystallites ensure all crystal planes are simultaneously presented at their Bragg angles during the scan → produces rings in 2D, plotted as 1D pattern. Single crystal XRD: Measures specific reflections — used for full structure determination.

Detector

Scintillator counter (NaI crystal + photomultiplier) or modern CCD array / position-sensitive detector. Records 2θ vs intensity. Position-sensitive detectors collect multiple angles simultaneously, dramatically reducing scan time. Dynamic range >10⁸ (can detect both weak and strong peaks simultaneously).

The θ–2θ Scan

In a standard powder XRD scan:

  • The X-ray source is fixed (or moves) and the detector moves to twice the angle of the sample (2θ geometry).
  • As 2θ is scanned from ~10° to 80°, diffraction peaks appear at angles satisfying Bragg's Law for each family of crystal planes (hkl).
  • The result is a diffractogram (intensity vs 2θ) — the XRD pattern.

22. XRD — Applications

🔍 Phase Identification

Unique diffraction fingerprints identify crystalline phases — matched against ICDD PDF-4 database containing >900,000 reference patterns. Examples: Quartz vs Calcite vs Gypsum; Rutile vs Anatase TiO₂; α-Fe₂O₃ vs Fe₃O₄.

🔬 Crystal Structure Determination

Solve unit cell dimensions (a, b, c) and angles (α, β, γ). Rietveld refinement uses the entire diffraction pattern to determine atom positions, occupancies, and thermal parameters. Full structural analysis without single crystals.

📏 Crystallite Size & Microstrain

Scherrer equation for crystallite size. Williamson-Hall analysis separates size and microstrain contributions. Detects nanocrystalline materials (2–100 nm). Monitors grain growth during annealing.

💊 Industrial & Pharmaceutical

QC of API (Active Pharmaceutical Ingredient) polymorphs in drug manufacturing — different crystal forms have different solubility and bioavailability. Monitoring cement hydration phases. Steel heat treatment analysis and alloy QC.

🌡️ In-Situ & High-Temperature XRD

Monitor phase transformations in real time as temperature changes. Study thermal decomposition (as in this Co₃O₄ study). Observe reactions under applied pressure or gas environments.

🔋 Energy Materials

Characterize battery electrode materials, fuel cell catalysts, photovoltaic absorber layers. Monitor structural changes during charge/discharge cycles in batteries.

Section Summary — XRD Complete

  • XRD is the definitive technique for crystal structure and phase identification.
  • Bragg's Law (nλ = 2d sinθ) governs diffraction — peaks appear only at specific 2θ angles.
  • Scherrer equation (D = Kλ/β cosθ) extracts crystallite size from peak broadening.
  • XRD is non-destructive, accurate, and backed by a massive reference database (ICDD).
  • Applications span pharmaceuticals, materials science, energy, and geology.

📘 The Power of Combining Techniques
  • IR tells us WHAT it is chemically (Co–O bonds confirmed, no ligands).
  • XRD tells us its CRYSTAL STRUCTURE (cubic spinel Co₃O₄, phase pure).
  • TEM tells us the TRUE SIZE and SHAPE of individual particles.
  • SEM tells us how particles AGGREGATE and the SURFACE MORPHOLOGY.
  • Together → complete characterization of the nanomaterial.

📡 IR Spectroscopy

  • Measures bond vibration via IR absorption
  • Needs dipole change to absorb
  • Metal–O bonds: 400–700 cm⁻¹
  • OH/H₂O: ~3200–3600 cm⁻¹
  • NH₃: ~3300 cm⁻¹, 1600 cm⁻¹
  • NO₃⁻: ~1380 cm⁻¹, ~820 cm⁻¹
  • FT-IR = modern standard (fast, sensitive)
  • Confirms synthesis by ligand disappearance
