1. Background & Context

1.1 Antibiotic Pollution — Why It Matters

Antibiotics are extensively used in aquaculture, animal breeding, and medicine to treat and prevent bacterial infections. Their mass consumption means large quantities — including active parent compounds and metabolic by-products — are discharged into the environment via industrial effluents and municipal sewage.

⚡ Key Problem

Conventional wastewater treatment plants (WWTPs) are unable to completely remove antibiotics. These compounds persist in effluents, accumulate in ground water and surface water, and exert chronic toxicity on aquatic organisms while promoting antibiotic-resistant bacteria in the environment — a global public health threat.

1.2 Sulfamethoxazole (SMX) — Target Pollutant

📖 Definition

Sulfamethoxazole (SMX) is a common sulfonamide antibiotic widely used in treating bacterial infectious diseases. Chemically, it contains an aniline (–NH₂) aromatic ring connected via a sulfonamide (S–N) bond to a methyl-isoxazole group. Its molecular formula corresponds to m/z 254 in mass spectrometry.

  • Persistently detected in groundwater and surface water worldwide due to incomplete WWTP removal.
  • Raises concern about ecotoxicity and promotion of antibiotic resistance genes.
  • Chosen as the model contaminant in this study because it is a well-studied, environmentally relevant emerging pollutant.

1.3 Advanced Oxidation Processes (AOPs)

AOPs are chemical treatment methods that generate highly reactive oxidizing species (ROS) to destroy pollutants that resist conventional treatment. They can achieve near-complete mineralization of organic contaminants.

1.4 Peracetic Acid (PAA) — The Oxidant Precursor

📖 Definition

Peracetic acid (PAA), chemical formula CH₃C(O)OOH, is an organic peroxide widely used as a disinfectant in textile, medical, and food industries, as well as in wastewater treatment. It is commercially supplied as an equilibrium mixture with hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂), acetic acid, and water.

Why PAA is Attractive in AOPs

OxidantO–O Bond EnergyEase of Activation
Peracetic Acid (PAA)159 kJ mol⁻¹Easiest (lowest energy needed)
Hydrogen Peroxide (H₂O₂)213 kJ mol⁻¹Moderate
Peroxymonosulfate (PMS)317 kJ mol⁻¹Hardest

The weaker O–O bond in PAA compared to PMS and H₂O₂ means it requires less energy input to be activated, making it more cost-effective and efficient when paired with the right catalyst.

PAA Activation Methods

  • UV radiation — photolytic cleavage of the O–O bond.
  • Thermal activation — elevated temperature breaks the peroxide bond.
  • Transition metals — electron transfer from metal ions/surface species cleaves O–O; considered the most cost-effective and energy-efficient due to natural abundance and low cost.

Why Cobalt is the Best Transition Metal for PAA Activation

Among transition metals (Cu, Fe, Mn, Co), cobalt shows the highest catalytic performance for PAA activation. In homogeneous Co(II)/PAA systems, extremely low Co(II) concentrations (<1 μM) can induce 99.9% SMX removal. However, homogeneous cobalt systems release free Co²⁺ ions which are toxic to aquatic life — causing secondary pollution. This motivates the development of heterogeneous cobalt catalysts.

Limitations of Prior Cobalt-Based Heterogeneous Catalysts

The core challenge: promote catalytic efficiency + prevent cobalt ion leaching. This is where g-C₃N₄ comes in as the ideal support material.

Section 1 Summary

  • SMX is a sulfonamide antibiotic persistently detected in water bodies — a major environmental concern.
  • AOPs using reactive oxygen species are effective for SMX elimination.
  • PAA has the weakest O–O bond among common peroxides → easiest to activate.
  • Cobalt is the most efficient metal for PAA activation, but homogeneous Co²⁺ causes toxicity.
  • Heterogeneous cobalt catalysts are needed; prior ones suffer from leaching and poor efficiency.

2. Graphitic Carbon Nitride (g-C₃N₄)

📖 Definition

Graphitic carbon nitride (g-C₃N₄) is a metal-free, polymer semiconductor composed of heptazine (tri-s-triazine) rings connected by nitrogen bridges, forming a 2D layered structure analogous to graphene. Each heptazine unit contains six pyridinic nitrogen atoms bearing lone electron pairs.

2.1 Unique Properties of g-C₃N₄ Relevant to This Study

🔵 High Chemical Stability

Resistant to acid, base, and oxidants up to ~600°C in air — survives harsh reaction conditions.

⚡ Electron-Rich Structure

Six nitrogen lone pairs per heptazine ring donate electron density — excellent for binding metal cations.

🧲 Metal Ion Entrapment

Pyridinic N atoms act as Lewis base sites, chelating metal ions via strong metal–N coordination bonds.

🌱 Non-Toxic

Environmentally benign; prepared from cheap precursors (urea, melamine, dicyandiamide).

🔄 Charge Transfer

The delocalized π-system facilitates internal electron transfer between embedded metals and the framework.

🏗️ Versatile Platform

Framework immobilizes metal catalysts, preventing leaching while promoting catalytic activity.

2.2 Crystal Structure Details

The XRD pattern of pure g-C₃N₄ shows two characteristic diffraction peaks:

  • 2θ ≈ 12.9° → (100) plane: Corresponds to the in-plane ordering of tri-s-triazine (heptazine) structural units.
  • 2θ ≈ 27.2° → (002) plane: Corresponds to interlayer stacking distance (~0.326 nm), analogous to the graphite (002) reflection — hence the name "graphitic" carbon nitride.
⚡ Key Insight

g-C₃N₄ alone is ineffective at activating PAA (negligible SMX removal observed in g-C₃N₄/PAA system). Its role is purely as a structural support and anchor for the active cobalt species — not as an activator itself.

2.3 Synthesis of Pure g-C₃N₄

Pure g-C₃N₄ is synthesized by thermal polycondensation (calcination) of nitrogen-rich precursors such as urea. Urea decomposes and self-polymerizes upon heating to form the heptazine-based polymer network. The absence of Co(NO₃)₂·6H₂O during calcination yields pure g-C₃N₄.

3. Co–CN: Synthesis & Structure

3.1 What is Co–CN?

📖 Definition

Co–CN (Cobalt-doped graphitic carbon nitride) is a heterogeneous catalyst where cobalt atoms are atomically incorporated into the heptazine framework of g-C₃N₄ via Co–N chemical bonds — not merely mixed physically or coated onto the surface.

3.2 Synthesis Protocol

Co–CN is synthesized via a facile one-step calcination method:

  1. 1
    Mix precursors: Urea (nitrogen/carbon source) + Co(NO₃)₂·6H₂O (cobalt source) in appropriate mass ratio.
  2. 2
    Calcination: Heat the mixture to high temperature in a muffle furnace. Urea undergoes thermal polycondensation to form g-C₃N₄ while Co(NO₃)₂·6H₂O simultaneously decomposes, releasing Co²⁺/Co³⁺ ions that coordinate with the pyridinic N atoms of the forming g-C₃N₄ framework.
  3. 3
    Result: Cobalt becomes chemically bonded within the g-C₃N₄ matrix (Co–N bond), uniformly distributed throughout the framework as confirmed by EDS element mapping.
⚠️ Important Distinction

Co–CN is NOT a simple physical mixture of Co₃O₄ and g-C₃N₄. XRD shows no Co₃O₄ peaks and SEM shows no Co₃O₄ particle morphology in fresh Co–CN. Cobalt is incorporated atomically into the g-C₃N₄ framework.

3.3 Cobalt Doping Content Variants

4. Catalyst Characterization

Fresh Co–CN and used Co–CN (after reaction) were characterized by XRD, XPS, SEM+EDS, and BET to confirm structure, composition, and stability.

4.1 X-Ray Diffraction (XRD)

📖 What XRD Tells Us

XRD identifies crystalline phases by their characteristic 2θ diffraction angles. It confirms what compounds are present, their crystal structure, and changes in interlayer spacing.

Cobalt Oxide Reference

Peaks at 2θ = 19.1°, 31.4°, 36.9°, 38.7°, 44.9°, 55.8°, 59.5°, 65.4° → all match Co₃O₄ (PDF#74-1656). This confirms the cobalt oxide reference sample is Co₃O₄.

Pure g-C₃N₄

Peaks at 12.9° (100) and 27.2° (002) — characteristic tri-s-triazine units and aromatic stacking.

Fresh Co–CN

Both g-C₃N₄ peaks are present but shifted to higher 2θ and show reduced intensity → indicates decreased interlayer stacking distance (lattice compression) caused by Co incorporation. No Co₃O₄ peaks → cobalt is not forming separate oxide nanoparticles; it is embedded atomically.

Used Co–CN

Little change compared to fresh Co–CN → crystal structure is preserved during PAA activation reaction. Confirms structural stability of Co–CN.

5. PAA Activation Mechanism

5.1 The Core Activation Equations

The surface cobalt species ≡Co(II) and ≡Co(III) in Co–CN participate in a redox cycle that cleaves the O–O bond in PAA to generate organic radicals:

🧪 Core Reactions
Eq. (2): ≡Co(II) + CH₃C(O)OOH → ≡Co(III) + CH₃C(O)O• + OH⁻

Co(II) donates an electron to PAA → O–O bond breaks → acetyloxy radical (CH₃C(O)O•) released + Co(II) oxidized to Co(III) + hydroxide ion.

Eq. (3): ≡Co(III) + CH₃C(O)OOH → ≡Co(II) + CH₃C(O)OO• + H⁺

Co(III) accepts an electron from PAA → acetylperoxy radical (CH₃C(O)OO•) released + Co(III) reduced back to Co(II).

⚡ The Redox Cycle — Why Co–CN is Stable & Efficient

Equations (2) and (3) work together: Co(II)→Co(III) in Eq. 2, Co(III)→Co(II) in Eq. 3. This creates a continuous self-regenerating redox cycle (≡Co(II) ⇌ ≡Co(III)). The catalyst is never permanently consumed — it keeps generating radicals as long as PAA is present.

5.2 Secondary Radical Reactions (Minor Pathways)

🧪 Secondary Reactions
Eq. (4): CH₃C(O)O• → •CH₃ + CO₂ (β-scission of acetyloxy radical)
Eq. (5): •CH₃ + O₂ → CH₃OO• (methyl radical reacts with dissolved O₂)

•CH₃ and CH₃OO• have weaker oxidation capacity than the primary organic radicals and their contribution to SMX removal is negligible. This was confirmed by N₂ purging experiments (removing dissolved O₂) — which had negligible impact on SMX removal, proving CH₃OO• (which requires O₂ to form) is not important.

5.3 Alkaline Decomposition Reactions (Why High pH Hurts)

🧪 PAA Decomposition at Alkaline pH
Eq. (6): CH₃C(O)OO⁻ + CH₃C(O)OOH → CH₃C(O)OH + CH₃C(O)O⁻ + O₂
Eq. (7): CH₃C(O)OO⁻ + H₂O₂ → CH₃C(O)O⁻ + H₂O + O₂

At high pH, PAA deprotonates (pKa ≈ 8.2) to form CH₃C(O)OO⁻, which is more reactive but rapidly decomposes via reactions (6) and (7), consuming PAA before it can be catalytically activated. This reduces radical generation and suppresses SMX removal.

6.1 Radical Scavenging Experiments — Methodology & Rationale

Scavenging experiments use chemical quenchers (scavengers) that react rapidly with specific reactive species. By adding a scavenger and observing the change in SMX degradation rate, we can infer which species are responsible.

⚡ Critical Conclusions from Scavenging
  • •OH is NOT generated in the Co–CN/PAA system (TBA shows no inhibition). This is consistent with prior cobalt-based PAA studies.
  • Organic radicals (R–O•) are the dominant reactive species: CH₃C(O)O• and CH₃C(O)OO• are responsible for 74.4% of SMX degradation.
  • MeOH inhibition confirms organic radicals; the remaining ~24% SMX removal even with MeOH may reflect incomplete scavenging or direct oxidation by PAA.

6.3 Why •OH is Absent

In Equation (2), the O–O bond in PAA is cleaved homolytically to produce CH₃C(O)O• and OH⁻ — the hydroxide ion, not the hydroxyl radical (•OH). This is a fundamentally different bond cleavage pattern compared to H₂O₂-based Fenton systems (which do produce •OH). The PAA O–O bond, when broken by Co(II), yields organic radicals rather than •OH.

Reactive Species Summary

  • Dominant: CH₃C(O)O• (acetyloxy) + CH₃C(O)OO• (acetylperoxy) — together responsible for most SMX degradation.
  • Absent: •OH — TBA experiment confirms this.
  • Negligible: •CH₃ and CH₃OO• — N₂ purging experiment confirms their contribution is insignificant.
  • Method: Differential scavenging (TBA vs. MeOH) to distinguish •OH from R–O•.

⚡ Leached Cobalt Ion Contribution

After reaction, the leached Co²⁺ concentration was 18 μg L⁻¹. Testing Co²⁺/PAA at 20 μg L⁻¹ gave only 49.6% SMX removal (vs. 98.5% for Co–CN/PAA). Since the actual leached concentration is lower than 18 μg L⁻¹ throughout the reaction (it accumulates over time), the contribution of leached Co²⁺ is even smaller in practice. Therefore, heterogeneous catalysis at the Co–CN surface dominates SMX removal.

⚠️ Commercial PAA Solution Note

Commercial PAA is an equilibrium mixture of PAA, H₂O₂, acetic acid, and water. The Co–CN/H₂O₂ control experiment confirms that H₂O₂ in the PAA solution does not contribute to SMX removal — only PAA itself is the active oxidant precursor.

📖 pKa of PAA

The pKa of PAA ≈ 8.2. Below this pH, PAA exists predominantly in the protonated (neutral) form CH₃C(O)OOH. Above this pH, the deprotonated (anionic) form CH₃C(O)OO⁻ dominates. The neutral form is more stable and can be catalytically activated; the anionic form rapidly self-decomposes.

⚡ Practical Implication

The optimal near-neutral pH (6.5) is highly practical for real wastewater treatment, since most effluents are near-neutral — no need for costly pH adjustment.

9. Stability & Reusability of Co–CN

CycleSMX RemovalObservation
Round 198.5%Full activity
Round 298.4%Negligible decline

Why Co–CN is So Stable

  1. 1
    Strong Co–N Chemical Bond: Cobalt is anchored to g-C₃N₄ through covalent-like coordination bonds, not physical adsorption. This prevents cobalt from leaching into solution during repeated use.
  2. 2
    Self-Regenerating Redox Cycle: The ≡Co(II)/≡Co(III) cycle (Eqs. 2 & 3) continuously regenerates both oxidation states — no permanent deactivation of active sites occurs.
  3. 3
    Structural Stability: XRD and SEM of used Co–CN show no morphological or crystallographic changes — the g-C₃N₄ framework is not degraded by PAA.

Catalyst Regeneration Protocol

After each cycle, the used Co–CN is recovered by filtration, washed with deionized water five times (to remove residual SMX and reactive species), and dried in vacuum at 60°C overnight before the next run. This simple protocol maintains catalyst performance.

⚡ Significance of Stability

High reusability dramatically reduces the cost per unit of wastewater treated, making Co–CN economically viable for practical water treatment applications.

10. SMX Degradation Pathways

Eight transformation products (TPs) were identified by mass spectrometry (LC–MS/MS). Based on these TPs, four probable degradation pathways were proposed:

Pathway 1
Hydroxylation
Reactive species attack the electron-rich aniline ring of SMX, adding –OH group. SMX (m/z 254) → m/z 270.
Pathway 2
Nitration
Amino group (–NH₂) on the aniline ring is oxidized to a nitro group (–NO₂). SMX → m/z 284; m/z 270 → m/z 300. Also m/z 284 → m/z 300 via hydroxylation.
Pathway 3
Bond Cleavage
CH₃C(O)O• and CH₃C(O)OO• attack the S–N bond in SMX, breaking it to yield m/z 99 (isoxazole-amine fragment) and m/z 158 (sulfonate fragment, not detected but inferred).
Pathway 4
Coupling Reaction
N-centered radicals from SMX (generated by H-abstraction from –NH₂) couple together to form dimeric products: m/z 503, m/z 519, m/z 348, m/z 364.

10.2 Why N-Centered Radicals Form

The aniline group (–NH₂) in SMX is an electron-rich moiety — a prime target for radical species. Organic radicals (CH₃C(O)O• and CH₃C(O)OO•) can abstract a hydrogen atom from the –NH₂ group, generating a nitrogen-centered radical on SMX. These N-radicals are highly reactive and can couple with other N-radicals to form the dimeric products observed in Pathway 4.

⚠️ Incomplete Mineralization Note

The formation of dimeric coupling products (m/z 503, 519, 348, 364) shows that SMX is not always fully mineralized — some larger intermediate molecules are formed. Further treatment (longer reaction time, higher dosages) or combination with other processes may be needed for complete mineralization.

Advantages of Co–CN over Prior Catalysts

  • Atomic Co dispersion → no agglomeration → better catalytic contact
  • Co–N bond → stronger anchoring → minimal leaching vs. Co₃O₄, CoFe₂O₄, LaCoO₃
  • Larger BET surface area than pure g-C₃N₄
  • Excellent reusability (3 cycles, minimal activity loss)
  • Works at neutral pH — practical for real wastewater

Common Exam Pitfalls

  • •OH is NOT produced in Co–CN/PAA — do not confuse with Fenton-type systems.
  • g-C₃N₄ alone does NOT activate PAA — cobalt is essential.
  • Commercial PAA contains H₂O₂ — but H₂O₂ contributes negligibly to SMX removal.
  • The ≡Co(II)/≡Co(III) ratio decreases slightly (not eliminates) after reaction — both states are regenerated.
  • PAA deprotonates at pH >8.2 — alkaline conditions hurt, not help.
  • m/z 158 was not detected but its formation cannot be excluded (it's an intermediate in Pathway 3).
