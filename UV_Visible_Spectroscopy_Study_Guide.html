Introduction to UV-Visible Spectroscopy

📖 Definition

UV-Visible Spectroscopy is an analytical technique that measures how much ultraviolet (UV) and visible light is absorbed or transmitted by a substance. The absorbed radiation stimulates molecular vibrations and electronic transitions within the molecule.

The Electromagnetic Spectrum — UV-Vis Region

UV-Vis spectroscopy operates across a wavelength range of 160 nm to 780 nm, which spans from the far-UV into the visible region. Understanding where this fits in the electromagnetic spectrum is essential:

UV (160–400 nm)
VISIBLE (400–780 nm)
160 nm300 nm400 nm500 nm600 nm700 nm780 nm
🔬

Measurement Parameter

Measures absorbance (A) or transmittance (T) of light passing through a sample.

⚗️

What it identifies

Used to identify both inorganic and organic species — from transition metal complexes to cocaine and aspirin.

🌊

Energy Interaction

UV/Vis photons have enough energy to excite valence (bonding) electrons to higher energy states.

💡 Why does cocaine absorb UV light?

Cocaine has two absorption maxima — one at approximately λmax1 ≈ 225 nm and a weaker one near λmax2 ≈ 270 nm. These arise from the π→π* transition (aromatic/ester groups) and n→π* transition (ester carbonyl C=O), respectively. This UV fingerprint is used in forensic drug analysis.

§1 Summary

  • Range: 160–780 nm (UV + visible light)
  • Measures absorbance or transmittance
  • Stimulates electronic and vibrational transitions
  • Identifies both organic and inorganic compounds

Absorption & Bonding Electrons

Why Does Absorption Occur With Bonding Electrons?

When a photon of UV or visible light strikes a molecule, its energy can be absorbed by electrons in chemical bonds. The electron uses this energy to jump from a lower-energy orbital (bonding or non-bonding) to a higher-energy orbital (anti-bonding).

⚡ Core Principle

The energy E(λ) required for a transition differs depending on the type of bonding electron involved. Different bond types (σ, π, n) require different wavelengths of light to excite their electrons.

This means UV-Vis absorption gives us functional group information — the absorption pattern reveals what types of bonds are present in the molecule.

Why Are Most Organic Spectra Complex?

Unlike atomic spectra with sharp lines, molecular UV-Vis spectra are broad and complex. Here's why:

1
Electronic + Vibrational Transitions Are Superimposed

Each electronic transition is accompanied by many vibrational sub-levels. Instead of a sharp line, you get a broad band that is the envelope of many overlapping transitions.

2
Absorption Bands Are Broad

Due to this superimposition, peaks spread over tens of nanometers, making it hard to resolve individual transitions.

3
Detailed Theoretical Analysis Not Possible

Full quantum mechanical analysis of all transitions is impractical. However, semi-quantitative or qualitative analysis of bond types is achievable.

4
Solvent & Molecular Effects Complicate Comparison

The choice of solvent can shift peak positions and broaden bands, making direct comparison between samples difficult unless conditions are identical.

⚠️ Common Mistake

UV-Vis spectra of organic molecules cannot unambiguously identify a compound by themselves. They must be used alongside other spectroscopic techniques (IR, NMR, MS). UV-Vis is most powerful for quantitative analysis and detection of specific functional groups.

§2 Summary

  • UV-Vis absorption involves bonding electrons jumping to anti-bonding orbitals
  • Different electron types (σ, π, n) need different energies (wavelengths)
  • Organic spectra are complex due to superimposed electronic + vibrational transitions
  • Semi-quantitative and qualitative bond-type analysis is possible

Types of Electrons: σ, π, and n

Three categories of electrons participate in UV-Vis transitions. Understanding each type is fundamental:

① Sigma (σ) Electrons — Single Bond Electrons

📖 Definition

Sigma (σ) electrons are the electrons that form single bonds between atoms. They occupy a σ bonding orbital — a low-energy, cylindrically symmetric orbital formed by direct (head-on) overlap of atomic orbitals.

σ orbital
Low Energy · Bonding
Symmetric, continuous e⁻ density between nuclei
σ* orbital
High Energy · Anti-bonding

② Pi (π) Electrons — Double Bond Electrons

📖 Definition

Pi (π) electrons are the electrons that form the second bond in double bonds (and third bond in triple bonds). They occupy π bonding orbitals formed by side-on (lateral) overlap of p-orbitals. There are two lobes — one above and one below the σ-bond axis.

π orbital
Low-Mid Energy · Bonding
Two lobes; above and below the molecular plane
π* orbital
High Energy · Anti-bonding
Nodal plane through bond axis; lobes are offset

③ Non-bonding (n) Electrons — Lone Pairs

📖 Definition

Non-bonding (n) electrons are lone pairs that exist on heteroatoms (O, N, S, halogens). They do not participate in any chemical bond and exist at a neutral, intermediate energy level — between bonding and anti-bonding orbitals.

🧪 Example: Formaldehyde (H₂C=O)

Formaldehyde has all three types of electrons:

  • σ electrons (•) — in the C–H and C=O single bond component
  • π electrons (×) — in the C=O double bond π component
  • n electrons (○) — lone pairs on the oxygen atom

This is why formaldehyde shows n→π* absorption — the lone pair on O can be promoted to the π* anti-bonding orbital of C=O.

§3 Summary

  • σ electrons: single-bond electrons; σ orbital (bonding) → σ* (anti-bonding)
  • π electrons: double-bond electrons; π orbital (bonding) → π* (anti-bonding)
  • n electrons: lone pairs on heteroatoms; neutral energy, can transition to σ* or π*
  • Energy order: σ < π < n < π* < σ*

Types of Electronic Transitions

Four possible transitions occur in UV-Vis spectroscopy, listed here from highest energy (shortest λ) to lowest energy (longest λ):

Electronic Transition Energy Diagram

The higher the energy gap, the shorter the wavelength (λ) needed

① σ → σ* Transition (Vacuum UV)

⚠️ Vacuum UV — Practical Problem

The σ→σ* transition requires the highest energy (shortest wavelength, ≤185 nm) of all UV-Vis transitions. This falls in the "vacuum UV" region. It's called that because:

  • Oxygen, nitrogen, and water vapor in air all absorb strongly at these wavelengths
  • Instruments must be purged with nitrogen or work in vacuum — very impractical
  • As a result, σ→σ* transitions are rarely used for analytical purposes

② n → σ* Transition

📖 Description

A lone pair (n) electron on a heteroatom is promoted to the σ* anti-bonding orbital. This requires less energy than σ→σ* but more than transitions involving π systems.

PropertyValueNotes
Wavelength (λ)~150–250 nmNear/far UV region
Molar absorptivity (ε)100–3,000Weak to moderate absorption
Compounds involvedSaturated compounds with heteroatomse.g., alcohols, ethers, amines, halides
Analytical utilityModeratePartially accessible to standard instruments

③ π → π* and n → π* Transitions (Most Important!)

⭐ Most Analytically Useful Transitions

These transitions are the workhorses of UV-Vis spectroscopy. They require unsaturated functional groups (compounds containing double bonds: C=C, C=O, C=N, aromatic rings, etc.) and fall in the ideal wavelength range of standard UV-Vis instruments.

n → π* Transition

  • A lone pair (n) electron is promoted to the π* anti-bonding orbital
  • Requires a heteroatom and an adjacent π system (e.g., C=O, C=N)
  • λ ≈ 200–700 nm, ε ≈ 10–100 (weak absorption)
  • Example: carbonyl groups (C=O) show n→π* around 270–300 nm

π → π* Transition

  • A bonding π electron is promoted to the π* anti-bonding orbital
  • Requires any π system (alkenes, aromatics, conjugated systems)
  • λ ≈ 200–700 nm, ε ≈ 1,000–10,000 (strong absorption)
  • Example: aromatic rings, conjugated dienes absorb strongly via π→π*
Transitionλ Rangeε (Intensity)RequirementRelative Strength
σ → σ*≤185 nmVery highAny single bondImpractical (vacuum UV)
n → σ*150–250 nm100–3,000Heteroatom lone pairWeak–moderate
n → π*200–700 nm10–100C=O, C=N + lone pairWeak
π → π*200–700 nm1,000–10,000Double bonds / aromaticStrong
💡 Intuition: Why is π→π* stronger than n→π*?

Molar absorptivity (ε) reflects the probability of the transition. A π→π* transition involves electrons that are delocalized in the π system — they have large spatial overlap with the π* orbital, making the transition highly probable (high ε). The n→π* transition involves a localized lone pair that has poor spatial overlap with the π* orbital — it's a "forbidden" (low probability) transition, giving weak absorption (low ε).

§4 Summary

  • σ→σ*: Vacuum UV (≤185 nm) — impractical
  • n→σ*: 150–250 nm; weak; saturated heteroatom compounds
  • n→π*: 200–700 nm; very weak (ε 10–100); needs C=O or similar
  • π→π*: 200–700 nm; strong (ε 1,000–10,000); needs unsaturation
  • n→π* and π→π* are most analytically useful (right wavelength range, accessible)

Qualitative Analysis

Limitation 1 — Few Resolved Peaks

⚠️ Key Limitation

Because UV-Vis spectra are broad (electronic + vibrational transitions superimposed), there are very few resolved (distinct) peaks. This means unambiguous identification of an unknown compound is not usually possible from UV-Vis alone. It should be combined with IR, NMR, or MS for definitive identification.

Limitation 2 — Solvent Effects on Peak Position & Shape

The choice of solvent profoundly affects a UV-Vis spectrum in two ways:

A) Peak Broadening by Polar Solvents

Polar solvents (like water and alcohols) interact with solute molecules through hydrogen bonding and dipole interactions. This blurs out fine vibrational structure, causing peaks to become broader and less distinct.

🧪 Example: Acetaldehyde Spectrum

In the gas phase, acetaldehyde shows a spectrum with distinct vibrational fine structure — multiple small peaks are visible. When dissolved in a polar solvent (water or alcohol), these fine peaks merge into one broad, smooth absorption band. This is called loss of fine structure.

🧪 Example: 1,2,4,5-Tetrazine
  • (a) Vapor phase: Highly resolved, many sharp vibrational peaks
  • (b) Hexane solution: Some broadening — moderate fine structure remains
  • (c) Aqueous solution: Completely smoothed out — single broad hump

This demonstrates that increasing solvent polarity → more broadening → loss of fine structure.

B) Peak Position Shift Due to Solvent Polarity

Solvent polarity alters the energy gap between ground and excited states by stabilizing or destabilizing one or both states through solvation interactions.

⭐ Key Concept: Why Does Solvent Shift Peaks?

Most electronic transitions produce an excited state that is more polar than the ground state (due to charge redistribution upon excitation). Polar solvents stabilize the excited state more than the ground state, which lowers the energy gap → red shift (bathochromic shift).

However, for the n→π* transition, the lone pair in the ground state interacts with polar solvent (H-bonding). This stabilizes the ground state more than the excited state, which increases the energy gap → blue shift (hypsochromic shift).

🧪 Example: Acetone n→π* Transition
  • In hexane (nonpolar): λmax = 279 nm (ε = 15)
  • In water (polar): λmax = 264.5 nm

The blue shift of ~14.5 nm in water occurs because water hydrogen-bonds to the carbonyl oxygen's lone pair in the ground state, stabilizing it more than the excited π* state — this increases ΔE, requiring shorter λ to absorb.

Limitation 3 — Solvent Itself May Absorb UV-Vis Light

Every solvent has its own UV absorption, which creates a background signal that can mask analyte peaks below the solvent's cutoff wavelength.

⚠️ Practical Warning

If your analyte absorbs at, say, 250 nm, you cannot use acetone (cutoff 330 nm) or carbon tetrachloride (cutoff 260 nm) as solvent — they will completely absorb the light at that wavelength, giving a useless spectrum. Choose a solvent with a cutoff well below your absorption wavelength of interest. Water or hexane/cyclohexane are usually the safest first choices.

Useful Functional Group Clues from UV-Vis

Despite limitations, certain spectral features do provide valuable functional group information:

🔵

Carbonyl Group (C=O)

A weak band at 280–290 nm that blue-shifts (moves to shorter λ) as solvent polarity increases = strong indicator of a carbonyl group.

🔵

Aromatic Ring

Absorption near 260 nm with fine structure (multiple small peaks) = indicates an aromatic (benzene-type) ring.

🔴

Conjugated / Fused Ring Systems

More complex aromatic systems (naphthalene, anthracene) and conjugated alkenes show red-shifted (higher λ) absorption compared to simple benzene.

💡 Why do fused rings / conjugated systems red-shift?

In conjugated and fused-ring systems, π electrons are delocalized over a larger molecular area. This delocalization lowers the energy of the π* orbital (stabilizes it), which reduces the π→π* energy gap. A smaller energy gap = longer wavelength (red shift = bathochromic shift) needed for absorption.

Rule of thumb: More conjugation → longer λmax. This is why β-carotene (11 conjugated double bonds) absorbs in the visible range (~450 nm) and appears orange.

§5 Summary

  • Few resolved peaks → unambiguous ID not usually possible
  • Polar solvents broaden peaks and eliminate fine structure
  • Solvent polarity can shift peak positions (usually blue-shifts n→π*, red-shifts π→π*)
  • Solvent itself may absorb — choose solvent with cutoff well below analyte λ
  • C=O: weak band at 280–290 nm, blue-shifts in polar solvent
  • Aromatic ring: ~260 nm with fine structure
  • More conjugation → red shift

When a spectral peak shifts or changes intensity, specific terms describe these changes. These four terms are fundamental to reading and discussing UV-Vis spectra:

  • Bathochromic: red shift (longer λ)
  • Hypsochromic: blue shift (shorter λ)
  • Hyperchromic: increase in peak intensity (ε)
  • Hypochromic: decrease in peak intensity (ε)

Quantitative Analysis — Beer's Law

The Beer-Lambert Law

📖 Beer-Lambert Law

The Beer-Lambert Law states that the absorbance of a solution is directly proportional to the concentration of the absorbing species and the path length through which light travels.

The Law

A = ε · b · c

Also written as: A = log(I₀/I) = log(1/T) where T = Transmittance = I/I₀

💡 Absorbance vs Transmittance

Transmittance (T) = fraction of light that passes through: T = I/I₀

Absorbance (A) = A = –log T = log(I₀/I)

When A = 0, T = 1 (100%) — no light absorbed. When A = 1, T = 0.1 (10%) — 90% absorbed. A is preferred over T for quantitative work because it is linearly proportional to concentration.

Why UV-Vis is Widely Used for Quantitative Analysis

🎯

Wide Range of Applications

Both organic and inorganic species can be analyzed — from metal ions to pharmaceuticals.

🔍

Detection Limit

Classical: 10⁻⁴ to 10⁻⁵ M. Modern instruments: 10⁻⁶ to 10⁻⁷ M — very sensitive.

🎛️

Selectivity

Moderate to high selectivity — by choosing the right wavelength, you can measure one analyte in the presence of others.

📊

Accuracy

Typical accuracy of 1–3%, with optimized methods reaching ~0.1%.

💰

Cost & Ease

Easy to perform and cheap — UV-Vis spectrophotometers are among the most common instruments in analytical labs.

💡 Reading the Table: Key Observations
  • Alkenes and alkynes absorb at very short wavelengths (177–178 nm) via π→π* — this is near the edge of most instruments, with very high ε values.
  • Carbonyl groups (C=O) show two absorptions: a strong one (n→σ*) at ~180–186 nm and a weak one (n→π*) at ~280–293 nm. The weak ~280 nm band is often used diagnostically.
  • Most lone-pair chromophores (azo, nitro, nitroso, nitrate) show n→π* transitions with very small ε values (5–100), confirming that n→π* is a "forbidden" (weak) transition.
  • Nitroso is unusual — it absorbs in the visible range at 665 nm (blue-green light), which means it may appear colored.

Limitations of Beer's Law

Beer's Law holds only under specific conditions. Deviations arise due to:

1
High Concentrations

At high concentrations, analyte molecules interact with each other (solute-solute interactions), altering ε. Beer's Law is only strictly valid at dilute concentrations (typically <0.01 M).

2
Non-Monochromatic Light

Beer's Law requires a single wavelength (monochromatic light). If the beam contains multiple wavelengths and ε varies across them, apparent deviations appear.

3
Chemical Equilibria

If the analyte undergoes pH-dependent dissociation or other equilibria in solution, the absorbing species may change with concentration, violating the linear A vs c relationship.

4
Stray Light

Light that bypasses the sample or reaches the detector without passing through the sample causes errors, especially at high absorbances.

§7 Summary

  • Beer-Lambert Law: A = εbc — linear relationship between absorbance, concentration, and path length
  • Detection limits: 10⁻⁴–10⁻⁵ M (classical), 10⁻⁶–10⁻⁷ M (modern)
  • Accuracy: 1–3% typical, ~0.1% with optimization
  • Easy, cheap, widely applicable — most common quantitative spectroscopic method
  • Chromophores contain the functional groups responsible for absorption
  • Beer's Law deviates at high concentrations, non-monochromatic light, or chemical equilibria
