Section 01

1Introduction & Overview

Metal nanoparticles — particularly gold (Au) and silver (Ag) — possess a striking optical property that bulk metals do not: they absorb and scatter light intensely at specific wavelengths in the visible to near-infrared (NIR) range. This phenomenon is exploited by UV-visible (UV-Vis) spectroscopy to characterize these particles.

Why UV-Vis Spectroscopy for Nanoparticles?

Plasmonic nanoparticles absorb radiation spanning the visible to near-infrared (NIR) region, with the exact wavelength depending on the size, shape, and surrounding environment of the nanoparticles. This makes UV-Vis spectroscopy an ideal, rapid, and non-destructive characterization tool.

The physical basis of this absorption is the Surface Plasmon Resonance (SPR) — specifically, the Localized SPR (LSPR) that arises when nanoparticles are small enough to confine the plasmon oscillation to the particle surface.

A dispersion of plasmonic nanoparticles gives rise to one or more distinct absorption peaks in a UV-Vis spectrum. The position, number, and width of these peaks carry rich information about:

  • Shape of the nanoparticles (spheres, rods, prisms…)
  • Size and size distribution of the nanoparticle batch
  • Surface functionalization (coatings, attached molecules)
  • Aggregation state of the particles in solution
  • Local dielectric environment (solvent, surrounding molecules)
Big Picture

UV-Vis spectroscopy is to nanoparticles what NMR is to organic molecules — a fast, non-destructive window into structure and environment. It is the first-line characterization technique for plasmonic nanoparticles in any lab.

Section 02

2Surface Plasmon Resonance (SPR) — Core Concept

2.1 What is a Plasmon?

Definition — Plasmon

A plasmon is a quantum of collective oscillation of the free (conduction) electrons in a metal. It is the quantized "ripple" of the electron cloud.

In a metal, valence electrons are delocalized and move freely through the atomic lattice (the "sea of electrons" model). When an electromagnetic wave (light) strikes a metal surface, its oscillating electric field pushes and pulls this electron sea back and forth collectively — like a wave moving through water.

This collective oscillation of conduction electrons is called a plasmon. When it is confined to the surface of a metal nanostructure, it is called a surface plasmon.

2.2 Charge Polarization in Nanoparticles

When the electron cloud of a metal nanoparticle is displaced from its equilibrium position by an external electric field (from incoming light), a charge separation arises across the nanoparticle:

  • One side becomes electron-rich (negative charge accumulates)
  • The opposite side becomes electron-deficient (positive charge of the underlying ions is exposed)

This separation distance is denoted d, and it is unique to every metal nanostructure — it depends on the composition, size, shape, and surrounding medium.

Figure 1 — Charge Polarization in a Spherical Nanoparticle
The electron cloud (purple) is displaced from the positive ion core, creating a dipole with separation distance d.

2.3 The Mass-Spring Harmonic Oscillator Model

To understand SPR intuitively, we use the classical mass-spring harmonic oscillator analogy:

In the Oscillator Model
  • Mass on spring = negative electron charges
  • Spring attachment point = positive ion charges
  • Spring constant (k) = restoring Coulomb force between + and − charges
  • Driving force = the oscillating electric field of incoming light
Physical Interpretation
  • Electrons are displaced by the light's E-field
  • Coulomb attraction pulls them back (restoring force)
  • They overshoot — oscillation begins
  • At resonance frequency, amplitude is maximum
  • Maximum energy absorption = SPR peak
Figure 2 — Mass-Spring Model of LSPR (Side View)
Each negative electron acts as a "mass" on a spring (restoring Coulomb force, constant k) anchored to the positive ion core above. LSPR occurs when λ of incident light = d (charge separation distance).

2.4 The Resonance Condition

Resonance Condition
λSPR = d LSPR occurs when the wavelength of incident light equals the charge separation distance

Where:

  • λSPR — wavelength of light at which resonance (maximum absorption) occurs
  • d — characteristic charge separation distance of the nanostructure

When the driving frequency of light exactly matches the natural oscillation frequency of the electron cloud, the system is in resonance. At resonance:

  • The amplitude of electron oscillation is maximized
  • Energy absorption from the incident light is maximized → the UV-Vis spectrum shows a peak
  • The near-field electromagnetic enhancement around the particle is enormous (basis of SERS)
Restoring Force — Two Competing Pulls
  • Displacement force: The electric field of light pushes negative charges away from positive ones.
  • Restoring force: Coulomb attraction between the separated + and − charges pulls the electron cloud back.
  • This interplay creates a damped oscillator. Maximum energy transfer (absorption peak) happens exactly at resonance.
Section 2 Summary
  • A plasmon = collective oscillation of conduction electrons in a metal.
  • Incident light displaces the electron cloud → creates charge polarization with separation d.
  • The system behaves like a mass-spring oscillator: electrons = mass; Coulomb force = spring.
  • Resonance (SPR) occurs when λ = d → maximum light absorption at that wavelength.
Section 03

3Localized Surface Plasmon Resonance (LSPR)

3.1 LSPR in Bulk Metals vs. Nanoparticles

In bulk metals (large structures), surface plasmons can propagate along the surface as traveling waves — this is called a propagating surface plasmon polariton (SPP). The plasmon is not confined; it spreads over a large area.

However, in metal nanoparticles (where the particle size is much smaller than the wavelength of light), the geometry forces the plasmon to remain localized — it cannot propagate anywhere because the particle itself is tiny. This is Localized Surface Plasmon Resonance (LSPR).

Feature Bulk Metal (SPP) Nanoparticle (LSPR)
Plasmon type Propagating Localized (standing oscillation)
Confinement Travels along surface Confined within the nanoparticle
Excitation Requires special coupling (prism, grating) Direct illumination with light
UV-Vis peak No distinct visible peak Distinct, tunable peak in UV/Vis/NIR
LSPR specific location Extended — one region of large structure Entire nanoparticle surface
Definition — LSPR

Localized Surface Plasmon Resonance (LSPR) is the resonant collective oscillation of conduction electrons in a metal nanoparticle, excited by incident electromagnetic radiation, resulting in strong absorption and scattering of light at a characteristic wavelength.

3.2 Why the UV/Visible Region?

For gold and silver nanoparticles, the charge separation distance d (and thus the resonance wavelength) falls conveniently in the ~300–1000 nm range, which spans the UV, visible, and near-IR portions of the electromagnetic spectrum. This is why UV-Vis spectrophotometers are ideally suited for their analysis.

Key Range

Metal nanoparticle LSPR wavelengths: ~300 nm (UV) → ~1200 nm (NIR), depending on composition, size, and shape.

For gold nanospheres (AuNPs): ~520–530 nm (visible green/yellow region)
For gold nanorods (AuNRs): ~520 nm (transverse) and ~800–1100 nm (longitudinal)

Why Gold?

Gold is the most widely studied plasmonic material because: (1) its LSPR falls in the visible range (easily measured), (2) it is chemically inert (doesn't oxidize in air/water), (3) it can be easily functionalized via thiol chemistry, and (4) it is biocompatible — critical for biomedical applications.

Section 3 Summary
  • LSPR = plasmon confined within the nanoparticle (not propagating).
  • In bulk metals, LSPR is localized to one specific region; in nanoparticles, the entire particle supports LSPR.
  • AuNP LSPR wavelengths fall in the UV/Vis/NIR range (~300–1000 nm), ideal for standard spectrophotometers.
Section 04

4UV-Vis Spectroscopy of Gold Nanoparticles (AuNPs)

4.1 Measurement Procedure

When conducting UV-Vis spectroscopy on AuNPs, the absorbance is measured continuously over a wavelength range of 400–800 nm. A spectrophotometer shines light through the nanoparticle dispersion (usually in water or PBS) and detects how much is absorbed at each wavelength.

Prepare the nanoparticle dispersion in a transparent solvent (water, PBS, ethanol).

Place in a cuvette; set the blank (solvent only) as the baseline reference.

Scan wavelengths from ~400 nm to ~800 nm (or broader for nanorods).

Record the absorbance spectrum — identify the LSPR peak position (λmax).

Analyze: peak position reflects morphology; peak shift indicates functionalization or aggregation.

4.2 The 525 nm Peak & the Color of AuNPs

Spherical AuNP LSPR Wavelength

Spherical gold nanoparticles (AuNPs) exhibit a single LSPR peak at approximately 525 nm.

The wavelength 525 nm corresponds to green light. Since the nanoparticles absorb green light, the complementary color is transmitted/scattered to our eyes. The complementary of green is red/purple, which is why AuNP solutions appear maroon or purple/wine-colored — not gold!

Intuitive Color Explanation

Think of it this way: white light contains all wavelengths. AuNPs absorb the ~525 nm (green) component. The remaining wavelengths that reach your eye skew toward red/purple. This is why a colloidal gold solution is a striking ruby-red or burgundy color, despite gold metal being yellow.

This property has been used since ancient times — the beautiful red glass in medieval cathedrals ("ruby glass") was colored with gold nanoparticles!

The fact that AuNPs appear maroon/purple instead of gold is one of the most striking demonstrations of the size-dependent optical properties of nanomaterials.

4.3 Factors that Shift the LSPR Peak

The LSPR peak position (λmax) is highly sensitive to several variables. Any change in these will shift the peak:

Factor Effect on LSPR Peak Direction of Shift
Increased particle diameter (size) Larger d → longer resonance wavelength 🔴 Red shift (toward higher λ)
Functionalization (polymer/protein coating) Increases effective diameter; changes dielectric environment 🔴 Red shift
Nanoparticle aggregation Particles cluster → effective size increases; coupling between particles 🔴 Red shift (+ broadening)
Change in morphology (sphere → rod) New oscillation directions appear New peaks, position changes
Change in refractive index of medium Higher dielectric constant → red shift 🔴 Red shift
Smaller particle size Smaller d → shorter resonance wavelength 🔵 Blue shift (toward lower λ)

4.4 Red Shift — Explained in Depth

Definition — Red Shift

A red shift is a shift of the LSPR absorption peak toward longer wavelengths (toward the infrared region). It is called "red" because longer visible wavelengths appear red to the human eye, and beyond visible is infrared (even longer).

The two main causes of red shift in AuNP spectroscopy are:

A) Functionalization to a Polymer or Protein

When a polymer (like PEG) or protein (like an antibody) is attached to the AuNP surface, it:

  • Increases the effective hydrodynamic diameter of the nanoparticle
  • Changes the local dielectric environment around the particle — the coating has a different refractive index than the solvent

Both effects increase the effective charge separation distance d, shifting λmax to a longer wavelength.

B) Aggregation Among Individual Nanoparticles

When nanoparticles cluster together (aggregate), their plasmons couple electromagnetically. The coupled system has a much larger effective size and a different resonance frequency. This produces a red shift and significant broadening of the peak. In extreme aggregation, the peak can shift far into the NIR and even disappear into a broad shoulder.

Figure 3 — UV-Vis Spectra: Bare AuNPs vs. Functionalized AuNPs
The functionalized (red) curve shows a red shift and broadening of the LSPR peak compared to bare AuNPs (blue/purple). Broadening indicates heterogeneity or aggregation.
Common Mistake

Students sometimes confuse a red shift (peak moves to longer λ) with a decrease in peak height. These are different things. A red shift = shift in position. The peak may also broaden (wider FWHM), which is associated with aggregation. Both can happen simultaneously.

Section 4 Summary
  • UV-Vis scans 400–800 nm for standard AuNP analysis.
  • Spherical AuNPs absorb at ~525 nm → appear maroon/purple in solution.
  • Any factor that increases effective particle size (functionalization, aggregation) causes a red shift.
  • Red shift = LSPR peak moves toward longer wavelengths (infrared direction).
Section 05

5Effect of Morphology — Nanorods vs. Nanospheres

The shape (morphology) of the nanostructure is one of the most powerful determinants of LSPR behavior. Two gold nanostructures that are chemically identical but differ in shape will show completely different UV-Vis spectra.

5.1 Gold Nanospheres (AuNPs) — Single LSPR Peak

A perfect sphere has only one axis of symmetry for electron oscillation — any direction through the center is equivalent. Therefore, when light polarizes the electron cloud, the charge separation distance d is the same regardless of the orientation of the incident light's electric field.

Key Fact — Spherical AuNPs

Spherical AuNPs exhibit only one LSPR peak at approximately 525 nm, because electrons can only be polarized in one equivalent direction (the sphere is isotropic).

5.2 Gold Nanorods (AuNRs) — Two LSPR Peaks

A nanorod (cylinder) is anisotropic — it has two distinct geometrical axes:

  • Transverse axis: across the width (short dimension)
  • Longitudinal axis: along the length (long dimension)

Electrons can be polarized along either axis, giving rise to two distinct LSPR modes and therefore two absorption peaks in the UV-Vis spectrum.

Transverse LSPR Peak Width
  • Electron oscillation across the short axis (width) of the nanorod
  • Charge separation distance = diameter of the rod (small)
  • Appears at ~520–530 nm (similar to spheres)
  • Less sensitive to aspect ratio
Longitudinal LSPR Peak Length
  • Electron oscillation along the long axis (length) of the nanorod
  • Charge separation distance = length of the rod (large)
  • Appears at ~800–1100 nm (IR region)
  • Highly sensitive to aspect ratio (length/width)
Figure 4 — UV-Vis Spectrum of Gold Nanorods (AuNRs)
AuNRs show two distinct peaks: a transverse peak (~525 nm, short axis) and a longitudinal peak (~800–1100 nm, long axis). The longitudinal peak position is tunable by changing the aspect ratio.

5.3 Transverse vs. Longitudinal LSPR — Deep Explanation

The key reason the longitudinal LSPR wavelength is longer than the transverse is directly explained by the resonance condition λ = d:

Why Longitudinal Peak is at Higher λ
dlongitudinal = length of nanorod > dtransverse = width of nanorod Therefore: λlongitudinal > λtransverse

The charge separation distance along the long axis is greater than across the short axis. By the resonance condition λ = d, the longer the axis, the longer the wavelength needed to resonate.

Figure 5 — Transverse (a) and Longitudinal (b) LSPR in Gold Nanorods
(a) Transverse: charge separation across the rod width → shorter λ. (b) Longitudinal: charge separation along the rod length → much longer λ in NIR.

5.4 Wave-Particle Duality Connection

The UV-Vis spectral representation of nanorods represents the wave-particle duality of electrons:

  • Electrons are shown as particles (point charges, + and −) to visualize charge separation
  • The oscillating electric field (sinusoidal wave in the diagram) represents the wave nature of electrons
  • The nanostructure continuously switches polarity (oscillates) — it never stays in one polarized state; the polarity flips back and forth at the LSPR frequency
Aspect Ratio Tuning

The aspect ratio (AR = length/width) of a nanorod directly controls the longitudinal LSPR wavelength. As AR increases, the longitudinal peak shifts further into the NIR. This makes gold nanorods exquisitely tunable from ~600 nm all the way to >1000 nm simply by controlling synthesis conditions — invaluable for biomedical imaging and photothermal therapy (tissue "optical window" is 700–1100 nm).

Section 5 Summary
  • Spherical AuNPs: 1 LSPR peak (~525 nm) — sphere is isotropic (one equivalent direction).
  • Gold Nanorods (AuNRs): 2 LSPR peaks — transverse (~525 nm, short axis) + longitudinal (~800–1100 nm, long axis).
  • Longitudinal λ > transverse λ because dL (rod length) > dT (rod width).
  • Electrons oscillate in both directions simultaneously, but only resonate at their respective λ = d.
  • The nanostructure continuously switches polarity — it never stays static.
Section 06

6Applications of UV-Vis Spectroscopy

UV-Vis spectroscopy of metal nanoparticles is used extensively in nanotechnology, chemistry, materials science, and biomedicine. The key applications from the course are:

6.1 Determining Particle Morphology

The number of LSPR peaks and their positions directly reveal the shape of the nanoparticles:

  • One peak ~525 nm → spherical gold nanoparticles
  • Two peaks (one ~525 nm + one 800–1100 nm) → gold nanorods
  • Triangular or star-shaped nanoparticles produce characteristic multipeak spectra

6.2 Monitoring Functionalization Reactions

This is perhaps the most practically important application in nanobiotechnology. The workflow is:

Record the UV-Vis spectrum of the bare (non-functionalized) nanoparticles. Note the λmax.

Carry out the functionalization reaction (attaching polymer, protein, DNA, drug, etc.).

Record the UV-Vis spectrum of the functionalized nanoparticles.

Compare: if a red shift occurred, this indicates successful coating/functionalization. The shift is due to the increased effective diameter and changed dielectric environment.

Red Shift as a Functionalization Indicator

A measurable red shift in λmax after a functionalization attempt is used as evidence of successful conjugation. No shift → conjugation may have failed. Large shift or peak broadening → possible aggregation (overdone reaction or instability).

6.3 Other Reported Applications

The course material also highlights UV-Vis as a tool in:

  • Catalysis studies: monitoring reaction progress, determining rate constants, studying effects of temperature/pH, calculating Arrhenius and Eyring parameters
  • Drug delivery: optically monitoring the release of drugs from nanoparticle-loaded microgels
  • pH and temperature sensing: LSPR wavelength shifts in response to environmental changes
  • Size distribution assessment: a narrow, symmetric peak indicates a monodisperse sample; a broad or asymmetric peak indicates polydispersity
Section 07

7Limitations of UV-Vis Spectroscopy

Despite its power, UV-Vis spectroscopy has important limitations that must be understood for proper interpretation of results.

Limitation 1 — Cannot Accurately Quantify Functionalization Extent

UV-Vis can indicate whether functionalization occurred (via a red shift), but it cannot quantify how many molecules are attached per nanoparticle or what fraction of particles are functionalized. For quantification, techniques like thermogravimetric analysis (TGA), fluorescence, or ICP-MS are needed.

Limitation 2 — Difficulty Identifying the Dominant Factor

Multiple factors can cause a red shift simultaneously: increased size from functionalization, aggregation, change in solvent, and presence of impurities. It is often difficult to determine which factor dominates the observed peak shift from UV-Vis alone.

Limitation 3 — Impurities in Solution

In functionalization reactions, the solution almost always contains a mixture of:

  • Fully functionalized nanoparticles (desired product)
  • Unconjugated free polymer or protein floating in solution
  • Reaction intermediates and by-products
  • Aggregates

These impurities all affect the UV-Vis spectrum, complicating interpretation. Conjugation reactions rarely yield 100% functionalized nanoparticles — there is always a mixture present.

Limitation 4 — Provides Ensemble (Average) Information

UV-Vis measures the bulk average response of millions of nanoparticles simultaneously. It cannot reveal the properties of individual particles. Single-particle techniques (e.g., dark-field microscopy, TEM) are required for particle-by-particle characterization.

✦ Master Summary & Revision Sheet

Everything you need to know — at a glance.

Core Concept Chain

Metal nanoparticle → free electrons → displaced by light's E-field → charge separation (distance d) → Coulomb restoring force → oscillation → resonance when λ = d → maximum absorption → LSPR peak in UV-Vis spectrum.

Definitions to Know

  • Plasmon: Collective oscillation of free electrons in a metal
  • SPR (Surface Plasmon Resonance): Resonant collective oscillation of surface electrons
  • LSPR: Localized SPR — confined to a metal nanoparticle, not propagating
  • d: Charge separation distance — unique to each nanostructure; determines resonance λ
  • Red shift: LSPR peak moves to longer wavelength (toward IR)
  • Transverse LSPR (AuNRs): Oscillation across the rod width → ~525 nm
  • Longitudinal LSPR (AuNRs): Oscillation along the rod length → ~800–1100 nm

Flash Card Questions

Q: What causes LSPR?
Collective oscillation of conduction electrons resonating with incident light when λ = d (charge separation distance).
Q: Why do AuNPs appear maroon/purple?
They absorb green light (~525 nm). The complementary color — red/purple — is what we see in solution.
Q: Why do AuNRs have 2 LSPR peaks?
Anisotropic shape: electrons oscillate along both the short axis (transverse ~525 nm) and long axis (longitudinal ~800–1100 nm), each with a different d.
Q: What causes a red shift?
Any increase in effective nanoparticle diameter: functionalization (polymer/protein attachment) or aggregation among particles.
Q: What does the mass represent in the spring-mass model?
The negative electron charges (electron cloud). The spring constant k = Coulomb restoring force between + and − charges.
Q: Name 2 limitations of UV-Vis for NPs.
(1) Cannot quantify extent of functionalization. (2) Cannot distinguish which factor (size, functionalization, aggregation, impurities) dominates a peak shift.
Q: Why is longitudinal λ > transverse λ in AuNRs?
Because dL (length) > dT (width). By λ = d, longer charge separation → longer resonance wavelength.
Q: How does UV-Vis detect functionalization?
Compare λmax before and after reaction. A red shift in the LSPR peak indicates successful conjugation (increased effective diameter + changed dielectric environment).

Key Numbers to Memorize

  • Spherical AuNP LSPR: ~525 nm
  • AuNR transverse LSPR: ~520–530 nm
  • AuNR longitudinal LSPR: ~800–1100 nm
  • General plasmonic NP range: ~300–1200 nm
  • Standard UV-Vis scan range for AuNPs: 400–800 nm

The "LSPR → Red Shift → Application" Logic Chain

Bare AuNPs (λmax ~525 nm) → Functionalize with polymer/protein → Effective diameter increases → d increases → λmax red-shifts to longer wavelength → UV-Vis shows peak shift → Confirm successful functionalization. If no shift: reaction failed. If massive shift + broadening: aggregation occurred (undesired).
