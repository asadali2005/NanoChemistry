01

What is Electrospinning?

📌 Definition

Electrospinning is a fabrication method that uses a high-voltage electric field to charge and eject a polymer melt or solution through a spinneret (needle), producing ultrafine fibres in the nanometer-to-micrometer diameter range, which then solidify or coagulate into filaments deposited on a grounded collector.

Why Electrospinning? — Background & Motivation

Traditional fiber manufacturing (melt-blowing, wet-spinning) produces fibers typically in the micrometer range (1–1000 µm). Electrospinning is uniquely powerful because it can routinely produce fibers in the 50 nm – 1000 nm range. This nanoscale size unlocks extraordinary properties:

PropertyWhy Nanoscale MattersApplication Benefit
Surface AreaSurface-to-volume ratio increases dramatically at nanoscaleBetter catalysis, filtration, sensing, drug loading
PorosityInterfiber spaces create a porous networkFiltration membranes, scaffolds for tissue engineering
Mechanical FlexibilityThin fibers bend without fractureWearable electronics, flexible sensors
FunctionalityEasy incorporation of additives into spinning solutionDrug delivery, nanocomposites, energy devices
✅ Key Insight

Electrospinning is a versatile, scalable, and low-cost technique suitable for a wide range of materials: natural polymers (silk, collagen), synthetic polymers (PAN, PEO, PCL), ceramics (via sol-gel precursors), and composites. This versatility makes it a cornerstone method in nanomaterial synthesis.

Section Summary

  • Electrospinning = high-voltage + polymer solution → nanometer-scale fibres
  • Fiber diameters: nanometer to micrometer scale
  • Core principle: electrostatic force overcomes surface tension to draw out ultra-fine jets
  • Key advantage: enormously high surface-area-to-volume ratio

Electrospinning Setup & Components

A standard electrospinning apparatus has three primary components. Understanding the role of each component is essential for grasping why the process works.

Figure: Schematic of a standard electrospinning setup

Component 1: High-Voltage Power Supply

Component Role

Delivers kilovolt (kV)-range DC voltage to the metallic needle. This creates a strong electric field between the needle tip and the grounded collector, providing the electrostatic driving force to form and accelerate the fiber jet.

  • Typically operates in the range of 1 kV/cm to ~30 kV total, depending on polymer and setup geometry.
  • The threshold voltage (~1 kV/cm) must be exceeded before fiber formation begins — below this, a droplet simply hangs at the needle tip.
  • Polarity can be positive or negative; most setups use positive polarity on the needle.

Component 2: Syringe with Metallic Needle (Spinneret)

Component Role

The syringe holds the polymer solution or melt and delivers it at a controlled rate through the metallic needle. The needle tip is where the charged droplet forms and eventually becomes the Taylor cone and jet.

  • The needle is metallic to conduct charge from the power supply to the solution.
  • A syringe pump typically controls the feed rate (mL/h) precisely.
  • Needle diameter influences droplet size and initial jet diameter.
  • Multi-needle and coaxial needle setups exist for core-shell or multi-material fibers (advanced configurations).

Component 3: Grounded Collector

Component Role

The collector is connected to ground (0 V), completing the electrical circuit. It attracts the charged fiber jet, causing fibers to deposit and accumulate as a mat or membrane.

  • Solid substrate collectors: flat aluminum foil, glass, or silicon wafer — produce random fiber mats.
  • Rotating drum/mandrel collectors: rotation aligns the fibers parallel to each other.
  • Parallel electrode collectors (gap collectors): two separated conductive strips create an electric field that aligns fibers across the gap.
  • Liquid media collectors: used for self-assembling structures in certain biological scaffolds.
⭐ Key Formula — Electric Field

The electric field E between needle tip and collector: E = V / d, where V = applied voltage (kV) and d = tip-to-collector distance (cm). Increasing V or decreasing d increases E, which drives stronger fiber stretching.

Section Summary

  • 3 components: High-voltage supply | Syringe + needle | Grounded collector
  • Voltage: kV range; collector can be solid substrate or liquid media
  • Collector type determines fiber orientation (random vs. aligned)
03

Mechanism of Nanofiber Fabrication via Electrospinning

The process of forming nanofibers from a polymer solution involves a carefully orchestrated sequence of events, each driven by competing physical forces. Understanding these forces is the key to understanding the entire process.

The Two Competing Forces

🔵 Surface Tension

  • Acts to minimize surface area — pulls liquid into a sphere/droplet
  • Opposes fiber formation
  • Must be overcome for electrospinning to work
  • Higher surface tension → harder to spin → may need solvent adjustment

🟣 Electrostatic (Coulombic) Repulsion

  • Like charges repel → tries to maximize surface area
  • Drives jet formation and elongation
  • Must overcome surface tension to initiate spinning
  • Source: applied high voltage charging the solution

Step-by-Step Mechanism

1
Droplet Formation

High voltage is applied to the polymer solution inside the syringe needle. The electric charge accumulates at the surface of the solution at the needle tip, forming a charged pendant droplet.

2
Taylor Cone Formation

As the electrostatic repulsion between surface charges builds up, it begins to deform the hemispherical pendant droplet. The droplet progressively elongates into a distinctive conical shape known as the Taylor cone (named after physicist G.I. Taylor, 1964). At this point, surface tension and electrostatic force are approaching equilibrium.

3
Jet Ejection

Once the electrostatic force fully overcomes surface tension, a fine, highly charged polymer jet erupts from the apex (tip) of the Taylor cone. This is the critical instability threshold. The jet is extremely thin — only micrometers wide at the point of ejection.

4
Whipping / Bending Instability

The charged jet does not travel in a straight line. As it moves toward the grounded collector, the interaction between the applied electric field and the internal repulsion of the like-charged jet causes it to undergo a violent whipping (bending instability) motion. This whipping dramatically elongates and thins the jet while simultaneously allowing the solvent to evaporate (in solution electrospinning). The whipping region is where most of the diameter reduction occurs — fibers can be stretched to 1/1000th of their initial diameter.

5
Solidification & Deposition

The continuously elongated, solvent-depleted filament solidifies and is deposited onto the grounded collector as a dry, solid nanofiber. The fibers accumulate layer by layer to form a porous nonwoven membrane or mat.

Charged Droplet
→

→

⚠️ Common Misconception

Students often assume the jet travels in a straight line from needle to collector. In reality, the whipping instability region is where nearly all the fiber elongation and thinning happens. The straight portion (near the Taylor cone) is very short. The chaotic whipping is what allows fibers to thin from micrometers to nanometers.

Section Summary

  • 5 stages: Droplet → Taylor Cone → Jet ejection → Whipping → Solidification
  • Key battle: Electrostatic repulsion vs. Surface tension
  • Whipping instability is the primary fiber-thinning mechanism
  • Solvent evaporates during the whipping stage, solidifying the fiber
04

The Taylor Cone — Deep Dive

📌 Definition

The Taylor cone is the characteristic conical shape a conducting liquid droplet forms under the influence of a strong external electric field, named after Sir Geoffrey Ingram Taylor who mathematically described it in 1964. It represents the equilibrium geometry where surface tension and electrostatic pressure are balanced.

Why a Cone? — The Physics

When a droplet is charged, two pressures act at the liquid surface:

  • Surface tension pressure (Pγ): proportional to curvature; acts inward (into the liquid), maintaining a rounded shape.
  • Electrostatic (Maxwell) pressure (PE): proportional to the square of the local electric field; acts outward, pulling the surface toward lower potential (the collector).

Significance in Electrospinning

  • A well-formed, stable Taylor cone is essential for consistent, uniform fiber production. An unstable or fluctuating cone leads to fiber diameter variations and defects (beads).
  • The jet origin point is always at the Taylor cone apex — this is why needle geometry and solution surface tension are so important.
  • Observing the Taylor cone visually (often with a camera) is a standard quality-check during electrospinning experiments.
✅ Experimental Note

In practice, the Taylor cone is rarely a perfect 49.3° cone — solvent evaporation at the needle tip, solution viscoelasticity, and feed rate fluctuations all perturb the ideal geometry. However, the concept remains the theoretical foundation for understanding jet initiation.

Section Summary

  • Taylor cone = conical liquid shape at needle tip under high electric field
  • Equilibrium between surface tension and electrostatic pressure
  • Theoretical half-angle ≈ 49.3°
  • Jet is ejected from the cone apex when electrostatic force exceeds surface tension
  • Stable Taylor cone = prerequisite for uniform nanofiber production

Orientation & Hierarchical Structures of Nanofibers

A powerful feature of electrospinning is the ability to control how fibers are arranged — their orientation and structural hierarchy. This has direct consequences for material properties and applications.

Types of Fiber Orientation

Orientation TypeDescriptionHow AchievedApplications
(a) Random Fibers deposited in all directions; isotropic in-plane Stationary flat collector (standard setup) Filtration membranes, wound dressings, tissue scaffolds
(b) Aligned at angle Fibers oriented at a specific angle to a reference direction Tilted rotating drum, or modified gap collector Anisotropic scaffolds, directional wicking textiles
(c) Highly aligned (parallel) Fibers nearly perfectly parallel; highly ordered High-speed rotating mandrel, parallel-plate gap collector Energy devices (batteries, supercapacitors), piezoelectric sensors, tendon/muscle scaffolds

Why Alignment Matters for Energy Devices

⭐ Important Point from Notes

Well-aligned and highly ordered nanofibers are often specifically required for energy devices (batteries, fuel cells, supercapacitors, solar cells). This is because:

  • Ion/electron transport: Aligned fibers provide unidirectional, low-tortuosity pathways for charge carriers, dramatically improving conductivity.
  • Mechanical anisotropy: Aligned fibers give directional strength matching loading conditions.
  • Packing density: Controlled alignment allows optimized electrode architectures.

The Role of Fiber Collectors in Achieving Alignment

✅ Key Principle

The fiber collector design is the most critical factor in achieving aligned orientation. The collector modifies the local electric field distribution, which directs where and how fibers land.

  • Rotating drum: Mechanical rotation sweeps fibers into alignment along the drum circumference. Alignment quality increases with rotation speed.
  • Gap (split electrode) collector: Two parallel conductive strips separated by a gap. Fibers align perpendicular to the gap because the electric field lines run across it. Produces highly ordered arrays (as in SEM image (c) in the notes).
  • Disk collector (sharp edge): A thin rotating disk; fibers align tangentially along the edge.
  • Auxiliary electrodes: Additional electrodes positioned around the main collector can steer and align the whipping jet.

Hierarchical Structures

Beyond simple flat mats, electrospinning can produce hierarchical architectures — structures with organized features at multiple length scales:

  • Porous fibers: Phase separation during solvent evaporation creates nanopores within individual fibers (highly relevant to the "porous nanomaterial" focus of this course).
  • Core-shell fibers: Coaxial needles enable fibers with distinct inner and outer materials (e.g., drug inside, polymer shell outside).
  • Fiber-in-fiber: Hierarchical multi-scale assemblies for complex functional behavior.
  • 3D porous mats: By controlling deposition conditions, thick 3D scaffolds can be built up layer-by-layer.

Section Summary

  • Three orientation types: random, angled, and highly aligned
  • Aligned NFs are critical for energy device applications (better ion/electron transport)
  • Collector design is the primary tool for controlling orientation
  • Hierarchical structures (porous, core-shell) extend functionality beyond simple mats

Near-Field Electrospinning (NFES)

📌 Definition

Near-Field Electrospinning (NFES) is an advanced variant of electrospinning that uses an extremely short electrode-to-collector distance (500 µm – 3 mm) to exploit the stable, straight-line region of the electrospun jet (before whipping begins), enabling direct writing of nanostructures with precise positional control.

Key Technical Features of NFES

ParameterConventional ElectrospinningNear-Field Electrospinning
Electrode-to-Collector Distance10–30 cm (far-field)500 µm – 3 mm (near-field)
Applied Voltage10–30 kV100–600 V
Jet Region UsedWhipping instability regionStable, straight jet region
Fiber PositioningRandom (controlled only by collector shape)High precision direct-write
Pattern ComplexityLimited (1D/2D mats)Complex 2D and 3D patterns
Fiber Morphology ControlGood but variableExcellent, improved control

The Two Key Advantages of NFES

🎯

Advantage 1: High-Precision Fiber Positioning

By working within the stable jet zone (before the bending instability begins), NFES can place individual nanofibers at precise, predetermined positions on a substrate. In conventional electrospinning, the whipping instability randomizes fiber landing locations.

This enables:

  • Direct-writing of specific patterns (letters, grids, spirals)
  • Fabrication of ordered nanoarrays
  • Integration of nanofibers into specific device locations
🔬

Advantage 2: Improved Fiber Morphology Control

The stable jet region produces fibers with highly uniform diameter and cross-section. This is critical for applications where the fiber's precise size and shape determine performance:

  • Photonic applications: Optical waveguiding in individual nanofibers requires exact dimensional control
  • Sensing applications: Sensitivity of a single fiber sensor depends on its exact geometry
  • Single-fiber device fabrication becomes practically feasible

Demonstrated NFES Patterns (from Notes)

NFES has successfully assembled polymers including polyethylene oxide (PEO), polycaprolactone (PCL), and composite PEO/carbon nanotube (CNT) nanofibers into complex patterns:

PatternScaleSignificance
Character "Cal" (a)~200 µm feature sizeDemonstrates fine script writing capability
Triangular pattern (b)~100 µm scaleGeometric 2D patterning
Six carbon posts connected by NFs (c)~50 µm scaleTrue 3D integration — nanofibers spanning between 3D structures
✅ Why Low Voltage in NFES?

In conventional electrospinning, the long gap requires high voltage to generate a sufficient electric field. In NFES, the gap is shortened to sub-millimeter distances, so only 100–600 V is needed to generate the same field strength. This also makes NFES safer and more compatible with sensitive substrates (e.g., biological samples, semiconductor chips).

Section Summary

  • NFES: electrode-to-collector gap = 500 µm – 3 mm; voltage = 100–600 V
  • Exploits the stable jet zone (no whipping) for precision placement
  • Advantage 1: High-precision individual fiber positioning
  • Advantage 2: Superior fiber morphology control (uniform, single-fiber devices)
  • Applications: photonic devices, sensing, complex 2D/3D nanoarrays
  • Demonstrated with PEO, PCL, PEO/CNT composites in 2D and 3D patterns

Electro-Hydrodynamic (EHD) Jet Printing

📌 Definition

Electro-hydrodynamic (EHD) jet printing is another electric-field-driven direct-write nanofabrication technique. Unlike electrospinning or NFES which produce continuous fiber jets, EHD printing operates in a pulsating (nanodripping) mode, ejecting individual nanodroplets from the apex of a liquid meniscus to build 3D structures.

Key Distinction: Pulsating Mode vs. Stable Jet

Electrospinning / NFES

  • Higher applied voltage
  • Continuous, stable jet mode
  • Produces continuous fibers
  • Solvent evaporation during flight
  • Fiber-based structures

EHD Jet Printing

  • Lower applied voltage (below NFES level)
  • Pulsating / nanodripping mode
  • Produces discrete nanodroplets
  • Droplet impact + solvent vaporization
  • 3D printed nanoparticle structures

The Nanodripping Mechanism — Step by Step

1
Meniscus Formation

A liquid meniscus forms at the nozzle tip. The solution contains nanoparticles as ink. A DC voltage is applied between nozzle and substrate.

2
Nanodroplet Ejection

Rather than a continuous jet, the electric field causes the periodic ejection of single nanodroplets from the apex of the meniscus at a homogeneous period τe (ejection period). Each droplet is composed of nanoparticles dispersed in solvent.

3
Impact and Vaporization

The droplet impacts the substrate and the solvent rapidly vaporizes (within the same period τe). This leaves behind a nanoscale deposit of uniformly distributed nanoparticles. The lateral size of the deposit equals the nanodroplet diameter.

4
Structure Growth via Electric-Field-Assisted Attraction (EnA)

After the first deposit solidifies, it becomes a sharp nanostructure protruding from the substrate. This sharp structure concentrates the electric field, which then electrostatically attracts subsequent incoming charged droplets to land precisely on the same spot (electrostatic nozzle-to-structure alignment, or EnA). This process repeats periodically to build up the 3D structure.

Voltage-Droplet Size Relationship

Key Relationship

Droplet diameter ∝ 1/Vapplied

Increasing the applied voltage decreases the droplet diameter. Higher electric field → stronger pulling force on the meniscus apex → smaller, more precisely defined droplets → finer feature sizes on the substrate.

Pulse Duration vs. Volume Control

ℹ️ Two Pulsating EHD Modes

There are actually two distinct pulsating EHD modes described in the literature:

  • Nanodripping mode (described above): steady DC voltage produces periodic droplets at period τe; droplet size controlled by voltage magnitude.
  • Pulsed EHD mode: pulsed voltage applied; the duration of the pulse determines the droplet volume and therefore the feature size. Longer pulse → more material ejected → larger feature.

Both modes allow sub-micrometer feature resolution, far beyond conventional inkjet printing.

EHD Printing vs. NFES — Comparison

FeatureNFESEHD Jet Printing
Output typeContinuous nanofibersIndividual nanodroplets → 3D structures
Jet modeStable continuous jetPulsating / nanodripping
Voltage relative to NFES100–600 VLower than NFES
Structure type2D/3D fiber patterns3D nanoparticle assemblies, pillars
Material usedPolymer solutions/meltsNanoparticle inks
Special featurePrecision fiber placementEnA self-guiding → vertical growth

Section Summary

  • EHD printing uses lower voltage than NFES → pulsating (nanodripping) mode, not stable jet
  • Ejects individual nanodroplets at period τe; droplets contain nanoparticles in solvent
  • Droplet lands, solvent vaporizes → nanoparticle deposit remains
  • Sharp deposit concentrates E-field → attracts next droplet (EnA) → 3D vertical growth
  • Higher voltage → smaller droplet diameter → finer features
  • Two pulsating modes: nanodripping (DC, voltage controls size) and pulsed (pulse duration controls size)

Critical Parameter 1: Solution Concentration

Of all the electrospinning parameters, solution concentration is arguably the most fundamental because it determines whether fibers can form at all, and their diameter if they do.

The Concept of Entanglement Concentration (Ce)

📌 Key Concept — Entanglement Concentration (Cₑ)

Entanglement concentration (Ce) is the minimum polymer concentration at which polymer chains in solution are long enough and dense enough to physically entangle with one another, forming a temporary network. This entanglement is what allows continuous fiber formation — without it, the solution simply breaks up into droplets.

Concentration Regimes and Their Effects

📊

Concentration vs. Fiber Morphology

▼▼▼ Way Below Ce: Only droplets collected — no entanglements, surface tension breaks the jet into droplets (electrospraying)
▼ Below Ce: Beaded fibers — insufficient entanglements; surface tension pinches off beads along thin connecting fibers
≈ Ce: Transition — beaded fibers begin to appear; onset of entanglement network
↑ 2–2.5× Ce: Smooth, uniform fibers — optimal regime; sufficient entanglements suppress bead formation
▲▲ Too high: Helix-shaped microribbons — extremely viscous solution; jet buckles into helical ribbon morphology instead of round fiber
Concentration-Diameter Relationship

Fiber diameter ∝ Concentrationn (where n ≈ 2–3 depending on polymer system)

Higher concentration → higher viscosity → larger fiber diameter. To minimize fiber diameter, use the lowest concentration that still gives bead-free fibers (the 2–2.5× Ce sweet spot).

Why Do Beads Form?

Bead formation is a Rayleigh instability phenomenon: when polymer entanglement is insufficient, surface tension overcomes the viscoelastic resistance of the jet, causing it to break up into droplets along the fiber. The resulting morphology is "beads on a string." Beads are generally undesirable as they create mechanical weak points and reduce uniformity.

⚠️ Warning

Simply increasing concentration beyond the optimal range is counterproductive — it increases viscosity so much that the solution may clog the needle, form ribbon/helix morphologies instead of round fibers, or make the Taylor cone unstable. There is always an optimal concentration window.

Section Summary

  • Ce (entanglement concentration) is the critical threshold for fiber formation
  • Below Ce: only droplets; at Ce: beaded fibers; at 2–2.5× Ce: smooth fibers
  • Too high concentration → helix-shaped microribbons
  • Lower concentration → smaller diameter (but beads if too low)
  • Optimal window: smooth fibers with minimum diameter

Critical Parameter 2: Feed Rate

💧

Feed Rate — Overview

The feed rate (or flow rate) is the volume of polymer solution delivered through the needle per unit time (typically mL/h). It is controlled by a syringe pump and directly affects fiber diameter, morphology, and process stability.

↑ Higher feed rate → lower charge density → fewer bending instabilities → larger fiber diameter
↓ Lower feed rate → higher charge density → stronger bending instability → smaller fiber diameter
⚠️ Too high feed rate → insufficient solvent evaporation time → beaded fibers or wet fibers on collector

The Charge Density Mechanism

Understanding the why behind feed rate effects requires understanding charge density:

Concept — Charge Density in the Jet

Charge density (charges per unit volume) of the jet determines how strongly like-charge repulsion stretches and thins the fiber. A slow feed rate means less solution volume per unit time is being stretched by the same electric field, resulting in more charge per unit volume — higher charge density.

  • High charge density → stronger mutual repulsion along the jet → more vigorous bending instability → more stretching → thinner fibers
  • Low charge density (from high feed rate) → weaker repulsion → less stretching → thicker fibers
  • Additionally, high feed rate means more solution reaching the Taylor cone than the electric field can effectively stretch → the Taylor cone becomes unstable → morphology defects
✅ Practical Note

There is a matched feed rate for every set of electrospinning conditions — a rate at which the Taylor cone is stable and the process is in equilibrium. If feed rate is too low, the Taylor cone recedes and the jet breaks up intermittently. If too high, solution accumulates and drips. Finding this optimal rate is part of process optimization.

Section Summary

  • Higher feed rate → decreased charge density → larger fiber diameter
  • Lower feed rate → increased charge density → more bending instability → smaller diameter
  • Too high feed rate → not enough solvent evaporation → beads and wet fibers
  • Optimal feed rate keeps the Taylor cone stable

Critical Parameter 3: Applied Voltage

⚡

Applied Voltage — Overview

⚠️ Threshold (~1 kV/cm): Fiber formation ONLY occurs above this threshold voltage; below it, no jet is formed
↑ Higher voltage → stronger electrostatic force on solution → more stretching of jet → smaller fiber diameter
⚠️ Voltage changes → changes shape of initial droplet → changes fiber structure and morphology
↕ Effect of voltage on diameter depends on other parameters (concentration, distance)

Threshold Voltage Explained

⭐ Threshold Voltage ≈ 1 kV/cm

The threshold voltage represents the critical point where electrostatic force first overcomes surface tension at the Taylor cone apex to initiate jet ejection. This value (~1 kV/cm) is approximate and depends on:

  • Polymer solution composition — surface tension varies with solvent and polymer
  • Needle geometry — diameter of the needle affects local field concentration
  • Tip-to-collector distance — determines field gradient

Voltage and Fiber Diameter — Nuance

The relationship between applied voltage and fiber diameter is not always monotonically simple:

  • In many systems: ↑V → ↑ stretching force → ↓ diameter (this is the primary effect described in the notes)
  • However, very high voltage can destabilize the Taylor cone — if too much solution is drawn too fast, the Taylor cone retreats into the needle, altering the electrostatic geometry and potentially increasing diameter or causing multi-jet formation
  • The effect of voltage is also modulated by solution concentration: higher concentration solutions are more resistant to stretching, so voltage has a weaker effect on them

Voltage and Fiber Morphology

✅ Morphology Effects

Changing voltage changes the shape of the pendant droplet at the needle tip, which in turn affects:

  • The geometry of the Taylor cone (angle, position relative to needle)
  • The acceleration profile of the jet
  • The degree of whipping instability
  • Whether fibers are round, ribbon-like, or have surface texture (wrinkles, grooves)

Section Summary

  • Fiber formation requires exceeding the threshold voltage (~1 kV/cm)
  • Higher voltage → stronger stretching force → generally smaller fiber diameter
  • Voltage also changes droplet/Taylor cone shape → affects fiber morphology and structure
  • Voltage effect on diameter is modulated by concentration and tip-to-collector distance

Critical Parameter 4: Tip-to-Collector Distance

📏

Tip-to-Collector Distance — Overview

The distance between the needle tip and the collector affects both fiber diameter and morphology, though less dramatically than concentration, voltage, or feed rate.

↑ Longer distance → more time for solvent evaporation + more stretching of jet → thinner (smaller diameter) fibers
⚠️ Too short distance → insufficient solvent evaporation → wet, fused fibers or beads
⚠️ Too long distance → reduced electric field strength → jet becomes unstable → beads can form
✓ Minimum distance required: enough for complete solvent evaporation before fibers reach collector

The Two Competing Effects of Distance

Increasing distance → thinner fibers

  • Longer flight path → more time for jet to be accelerated and stretched
  • More time for solvent to evaporate (drier, solidified fiber)
  • Overall: longer distance = thinner fibers (up to a point)

But: too far or too close → beads!

  • Too close: Solvent not fully evaporated → wet fibers fuse/bead
  • Too far: Electric field weakens (E = V/d) → jet loses directionality and breaks up → beads
  • Sweet spot must be found experimentally for each system
⭐ The Distance–Electric Field Trade-off

Because E = V/d, increasing distance reduces the electric field strength (for constant voltage). This weakens the driving force for fiber stretching. To compensate when increasing distance, the voltage is often also increased to maintain the same field strength. This parameter interdependency is why electrospinning optimization requires considering all parameters together.

⚠️ Common Exam Mistake

Students often state "longer distance always gives thinner fibers." The correct statement is: "Longer distance generally gives thinner fibers, but too far or too close both produce beaded fibers." There is an optimal range.

All Four Parameters — Combined Summary Table

ParameterIncrease Effect on DiameterDecrease Effect on DiameterDefect Risk
Concentration ↑ Diameter (larger) ↓ Diameter (smaller) Too low → beads/droplets; too high → ribbons
Feed Rate ↑ Diameter (larger) ↓ Diameter (smaller) Too high → beads (wet); too low → unstable cone
Voltage ↓ Diameter (smaller) ↑ Diameter (larger) Too low → no jet; too high → multi-jet / instability
Distance ↓ Diameter (smaller) ↑ Diameter (larger) Too short or too long → beads

Section Summary

  • Distance is the least influential parameter but still important
  • A minimum distance is required for complete solvent evaporation
  • Longer distance → thinner fibers (generally); but too long → field too weak → beads
  • Optimal: neither too close nor too far — sweet spot for each system
