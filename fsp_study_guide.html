What is Flame Spray Pyrolysis?
Definition · Core concept · Why it matters
🔬 Core Definition

Flame Spray Pyrolysis (FSP) is a one-step, continuous, scalable gas-phase synthesis technique for producing nanoparticles. A liquid precursor solution is atomized into fine droplets, dispersed into a self-sustaining flame, and combusted at high temperatures (1000–3000 °C). Metal species undergo pyrolysis, nucleation, growth, and condensation to form ultra-fine nanoparticles (2–100 nm) that are collected on a downstream filter.

Breaking Down the Name

The term "Flame Spray Pyrolysis" is itself a description of the three key elements of the process:

🔥 FLAME

A self-sustaining, high-temperature oxidizing or reducing atmosphere, reaching 1000–3000 °C. This is NOT a furnace — the flame is generated by the combustion of the precursor solution's solvent itself, enabling unique and controllable flame chemistry.

💧 SPRAY

The liquid precursor solution is atomized into a fine mist of small droplets. This aerosol generation enables precise control of the metal delivery rate to the flame and ensures a uniform feed of reactants.

💥 PYROLYSIS

From Greek: pyr (fire) + lysis (decompose). Fire-driven breakdown of the metal precursor compounds → metal oxide vapour → nanocrystals. The heat of the flame decomposes organometallic ligands.

The Complete FSP Process — Step by Step

1
Precursor Preparation
A metal salt or organometallic compound is dissolved in an organic solvent (e.g., ethanol, xylene). This creates a homogeneous liquid precursor solution at the desired molarity.
2
Atomization (Spray)
A syringe pump feeds the solution to a nozzle where a dispersion gas (O₂) breaks the liquid jet into fine droplets (20–100 µm) via Kelvin-Helmholtz instability.
3
Ignition & Combustion (Flame)
A pilot flame ring ignites the spray. The solvent combusts exothermically, creating a self-sustaining flame at 1000–3000 °C. The solvent acts as both carrier and fuel.
4
Pyrolysis & Vapour Formation
At peak flame temperatures, the metal precursor decomposes completely. Organic ligands combust (→ CO₂ + H₂O) and metal oxide vapour forms in the gas phase.
5
Nucleation, Growth & Quench
As gases cool downstream, metal oxide vapour supersaturates → nucleation occurs → nanocrystals grow by coagulation/sintering → rapid quench freezes final particle size.
6
Collection
Nanoparticles are thermophoretically driven toward a glass fiber filter downstream, where they are captured with >99.9% efficiency by Brownian diffusion.

Why FSP Matters — Key Advantages

✅ Technical Advantages
  • One-step, continuous — no post-synthesis calcination required
  • Scalable: mg/hr (lab) to kg/hr (pilot plant)
  • High purity & crystallinity — complete combustion of organics
  • Compositionally flexible — virtually any metal can be incorporated
  • Narrow size distribution (2–100 nm)
  • In-situ doping possible during particle formation
🔑 Key Technical Details
  • Temperature range: 1000–3000 °C
  • Particle size range: 2–100 nm
  • Production rate: mg/hr → kg/hr
  • Surface area: 50–200 m²/g
  • Collection efficiency: >99.9%
  • Process time: milliseconds to seconds

§1 Summary

  • FSP = one-step, continuous, gas-phase nanoparticle synthesis; liquid precursor → flame → nanoparticles (2–100 nm)
  • Name explains mechanism: Flame (high-T combustion) + Spray (aerosol delivery) + Pyrolysis (fire-driven decomposition)
  • Key advantages: continuous, scalable, high purity, compositionally flexible, narrow size distribution
  • Temperature: 1000–3000 °C; operates in milliseconds; self-sustaining (solvent is the fuel)

Historical Development of FSP
Five decades of innovation — from industrial flames to precision nanoengineering
1950s–
60s
Industrial Flame Processes

Degussa's Aerosil® process: SiCl₄ is burned in an H₂/O₂ flame, producing fumed SiO₂ — one of the first industrial gas-phase synthesis methods. The Evonik chloride process produced TiO₂ similarly. These were gas-phase only — no liquid precursors were used. The chemistry was limited to volatile chloride precursors (e.g., TiCl₄, SiCl₄) which could be delivered as gases.

⚠️ Limitation

Only elements with volatile chlorides could be processed. Compositional flexibility was very limited.

1970s–
80s
Spray Flame Processes Emerge

Liquid precursors introduced into flames — a revolutionary step. This enabled vastly greater chemical flexibility: virtually any metal can be dissolved in organic solvent as a salt (e.g., metal nitrate, acetate) or alkoxide (e.g., Ti(OPr)₄). The transition from gas-phase to liquid-phase precursor delivery opened the door to multi-component systems and the synthesis of complex oxide phases.

1990s
Systematic FSP Development — ETH Zürich

Kammler, Mädler, and Pratsinis at ETH Zürich systematically studied and optimized FSP. Their landmark 2002 paper 'Flame Made Nanoparticles' (Mädler et al., J. Aerosol Sci.) established the scientific framework for FSP. This work identified the key process parameters, explained nucleation and growth mechanisms, and showed how to tune particle properties predictably.

2000s–
Now
FSP Matures & Scales

Hundreds of nanomaterials have been synthesized by FSP. Scale-up from lab-scale (g/hr) to pilot-scale (kg/hr) has been demonstrated. FSP has been commercialized for catalysis, energy storage, medicine, and electronics applications. Companies like Evonik and various start-ups now use FSP-derived materials.

§2 Summary

  • 1950s–60s: Industrial gas-phase only (Aerosil® SiO₂, Evonik TiO₂) — limited to volatile chlorides
  • 1970s–80s: Liquid precursors introduced → vastly greater chemical flexibility
  • 1990s: ETH Zürich (Kammler, Mädler, Pratsinis) systematized FSP; landmark 2002 paper
  • 2000s–Now: Commercialization at kg/hr scale across catalysis, energy, medicine, electronics

Core Chemistry: What Happens in the Flame?
Three sequential transformations: metal ions → nanoparticles in milliseconds

Three sequential chemical transformations convert dissolved metal ions into solid nanoparticles in microseconds to milliseconds. Each step is spatially and temporally distinct within the flame.

Step A: Combustion of Solvent

The organic solvent (e.g., ethanol, xylene, 2-EHA) serves a dual role:

  • Carrier: Dissolves and transports the metal precursor to the flame
  • Fuel: Combusts exothermically to provide the thermal energy required for precursor decomposition (pyrolysis)

Without sufficient combustion enthalpy from the solvent, the flame cannot reach the temperatures needed for complete precursor decomposition and nanocrystal formation.

⚗️ Reaction A: Ethanol Combustion (complete)
C₂H₅OH + 3 O₂ → 2 CO₂ + 3 H₂O ΔH° = −1367 kJ/mol (highly exothermic)

This reaction is the primary energy source in ethanol-based FSP. Xylene and 2-EHA provide even more energy per mole (4.33 and 4.85 MJ/mol respectively).

Step B: Precursor Decomposition (Pyrolysis)

At peak flame temperatures (1500–3000 °C), the metal precursor compound decomposes thermally:

  • Organic ligands (carboxylate, alkoxide groups) combust → CO₂ + H₂O
  • Metal atoms are liberated in a highly reactive state
  • Metal atoms react with O₂ in the flame → metal oxide vapour forms
  • At very high T, additional hydrocarbon by-products may form transiently
⚗️ Reaction B: Metal Carboxylate Decomposition (general)
M(OOCR)ₙ → MₓOᵧ + n CO₂ + n H₂O [+ hydrocarbons at high T] Where M = metal, OOCR = carboxylate ligand Example: Fe(C₈H₁₅O₂)₃ → Fe₂O₃ + CO₂ + H₂O
📌 Key Point — No Solid Phase Yet

At the end of Step B, no solid particles exist yet. The metal is entirely in the gas phase as metal oxide vapour (e.g., Fe₂O₃ vapour, TiO₂ vapour). Particle formation only begins in Step C.

Step C: Nucleation & Condensation

As the hot gas stream cools downstream of the combustion zone, the metal oxide vapour becomes supersaturated (i.e., its concentration exceeds the equilibrium solubility). This drives spontaneous nucleation:

  • Nucleation: Metal oxide embryos form when the particle radius exceeds the critical radius r*
  • Growth by Condensation: Vapour molecules deposit onto nuclei surfaces
  • Growth by Coagulation: Particles collide and stick together (coalesce or sinter)
  • Sintering: At high T, colliding particles fuse into a single sphere
💡 Intuitive Analogy — Morning Dew

This is exactly like morning dew forming on a cold glass surface. When warm, humid air contacts a cold surface, water vapour supersaturates and condenses into liquid droplets. In FSP, as the hot metal oxide vapour cools rapidly, it supersaturates and condenses into solid nanocrystals — but in mid-air, within microseconds.

Precursor Solution Components

🧪 Metal Precursor

Source of metal ions. Must be soluble, combustible, and thermally stable in solution.

  • Metal alkoxides: Ti(OPr)₄, Al(OBu)₃
  • Metal carboxylates: Fe(III) 2-EHA, Ce(III) naphthenate
  • Nitrates, acetylacetonates
🔥 Solvent / Fuel

Dissolves precursor and provides combustion energy. High ΔHcomb → hotter flame.

  • Xylene: 4.33 MJ/mol
  • 2-Ethylhexanoic acid: 4.85 MJ/mol
  • Toluene: 3.91 MJ/mol
  • Ethanol: 1.37 MJ/mol
  • Acetonitrile: 1.25 MJ/mol
  • Methanol: 0.73 MJ/mol
🛡️ Stabilizer / Chelator

Prevents premature precipitation of metal precursor in solution. Must leave no non-volatile residue (otherwise contaminates NPs).

  • Acetic acid
  • Acetylacetone (acac)
  • Propionic acid
⚛️ Co-dopant Precursor

Introduces secondary metal atoms for multi-component nanoparticles. Must not react adversely with main precursor in solution.

Example: Pt nitrate co-dissolved with Ce(III) naphthenate → Pt/CeO₂ catalyst NPs

🔑 Solvent Selection Criteria

When selecting a solvent for FSP, consider these factors in order of importance: Enthalpy of combustion (ΔHcomb) · Boiling point (affects droplet evaporation rate) · Solvating power (must dissolve the precursor) · Flash point (safety) · Viscosity (affects atomization)

§3 Summary

  • Step A: Solvent combustion provides thermal energy (e.g., ethanol: ΔH° = −1367 kJ/mol)
  • Step B: Metal precursor decomposes thermally; ligands burn off; metal oxide vapour forms — NO solid yet
  • Step C: Vapour supersaturates as gas cools → nucleation → growth by condensation/coagulation → nanocrystals
  • Precursor solution has 4 components: metal precursor, solvent/fuel, stabilizer, co-dopant
  • Solvent choice controls flame temperature; high ΔHcomb (2-EHA, xylene) → hotter flame → better crystallinity

Thermodynamics of Combustion in FSP
Adiabatic Flame Temperature · Equivalence Ratio · Solvent Data

Adiabatic Flame Temperature (AFT)

📐 Definition

The Adiabatic Flame Temperature (AFT) is the maximum theoretical temperature reached in the combustion products, assuming no heat loss to the surroundings (adiabatic = zero heat exchange). In reality, the actual flame temperature is lower due to radiation losses and mixing with cooler gas.

🔢 AFT Formula
T_ad = T_reactants + ΔH_comb / (n_products × C̄_p) Where: T_ad = adiabatic flame temperature (K) T_reactants = initial temperature of reactants (typically 298 K) ΔH_comb = enthalpy of combustion of solvent (J/mol) n_products = moles of gaseous products C̄_p = mean molar heat capacity of products (J/mol·K)

Typical FSP flames: AFT = 2000–2700 K | Actual temperature is lower due to radiation & mixing with entrained air

💡 Intuition

Think of ΔH_comb as the "energy deposited" by combustion, and (n × C̄_p) as the "thermal mass" that must absorb it. A high-energy solvent (large ΔH_comb) or a small number of product molecules → higher temperature. This is why xylene and 2-EHA produce hotter flames than ethanol or methanol.

Equivalence Ratio (φ) — Flame Stoichiometry

📐 Definition

The equivalence ratio (φ) quantifies how the actual fuel-to-oxidant ratio compares to the stoichiometrically balanced ratio required for complete combustion. It is a dimensionless number that fully characterizes whether a flame is fuel-lean, stoichiometric, or fuel-rich.

🔢 Equivalence Ratio Formula
φ = (F/O)_actual / (F/O)_stoichiometric F/O = Fuel-to-Oxidant mass ratio
φ = 1
Stoichiometric

Complete combustion. Maximum adiabatic temperature. Exact ratio of fuel to oxygen required for full oxidation. C → CO₂, H → H₂O entirely.

φ < 1
Fuel-Lean (Oxidizing)

Excess O₂ present. Oxidizing atmosphere. Preferred for metal oxide nanoparticles — ensures complete metal oxidation (M → MₓOᵧ). Most FSP runs use φ < 1.

φ > 1
Fuel-Rich (Reducing)

Excess fuel, insufficient O₂. Reducing atmosphere. Can produce metallic nanoparticles (e.g., Fe⁰, Ni⁰) or sub-oxides. Used intentionally for certain applications.

⚠️ Critical Point for Oxide Synthesis

The vast majority of FSP synthesis for oxide nanoparticles (catalysts, sensors, pigments) is conducted under fuel-lean conditions (φ < 1). This ensures that all metal atoms are fully oxidized and that no residual carbon from incomplete solvent combustion contaminates the product.

§4 Summary

  • AFT = T_reactants + ΔH_comb/(n × C̄_p); typical FSP AFT = 2000–2700 K
  • φ = actual F/O ÷ stoichiometric F/O; φ=1 complete combustion; φ<1 oxidizing (preferred for oxides); φ>1 reducing
  • Most FSP for oxide NPs uses φ < 1 to ensure complete metal oxidation
  • Solvent energy ranking: 2-EHA (4.85) > Xylene (4.33) > Toluene (3.91) > Ethanol (1.37) > Acetonitrile (1.25) > Methanol (0.73) [MJ/mol]

Nozzle & Atomization System
Gas-assisted pressure atomization · Kelvin-Helmholtz instability · Pilot flame

Atomization Mechanism

🔬 Kelvin-Helmholtz Instability

The atomization in FSP nozzles relies on Kelvin-Helmholtz (KH) shear instability at the liquid-gas interface. When a liquid jet (the precursor solution) flows co-axially with a fast-moving gas stream (O₂), the velocity difference across the interface creates surface waves. These waves grow and amplify, breaking the liquid jet into ligaments and then into fine spherical droplets. This is a purely hydrodynamic breakup mechanism — no external vibration or ultrasound is needed.

📊 Flow Parameters
  • Liquid flow rate: 1–20 mL/min
  • O₂ dispersion gas flow: 5–15 L/min
  • Resulting droplet diameter (d₃₂): 20–100 µm typical
  • d₃₂ = Sauter mean diameter (volume/surface ratio)
📐 Droplet Size Control
  • ↑ Dispersion gas flow → ↓ d₃₂ → smaller droplets → smaller nanoparticles
  • ↑ Liquid flow rate → ↑ d₃₂ → larger droplets
  • Higher gas velocity = more KH instability = finer atomization
  • Nozzle geometry (orifice size) also affects droplet distribution

The Pilot Flame — Critical Distinction

⚠️ Common Misconception — Pilot Flame ≠ Main Flame

The pilot flame is a ring of small CH₄/O₂ or H₂/O₂ flamelets surrounding the nozzle exit. Its roles are purely ignition and stability:

✅ What Pilot Flame DOES
  • Ignites the main spray flame at startup
  • Stabilizes main flame against extinction (especially at low liquid flow rates)
  • Acts as a continuous ignition source
❌ What Pilot Flame does NOT do
  • Does NOT control nanoparticle properties
  • Does NOT significantly contribute to precursor decomposition
  • Is NOT the primary heat source for pyrolysis

The main flame is driven solely by combustion of the precursor solution (solvent + precursor). All particle properties (size, crystallinity, phase) are determined by the main flame chemistry and temperature — not the pilot.

📌 Design Note — Nozzle Materials

The nozzle body and concentric tubes are typically made of quartz or high-temperature stainless steel. Quartz is preferred for the inner parts because it is chemically inert, transparent (allowing optical diagnostics), and withstands the high exit temperatures near the flame base.

§6 Summary

  • Atomization via Kelvin-Helmholtz shear instability at liquid-gas interface → 20–100 µm droplets
  • Higher O₂ dispersion flow → smaller droplets → smaller nanoparticles
  • Liquid: 1–20 mL/min; O₂: 5–15 L/min
  • Pilot flame (CH₄/O₂ or H₂/O₂): only for ignition and stabilization — does NOT control NP properties
  • Main flame (solvent combustion) determines all particle properties

Flame Structure & Reaction Zones
Five spatial zones from nozzle to filter — each with distinct chemistry

The FSP flame has a well-defined spatial structure with five sequential reaction zones. Moving upward from the nozzle to the filter, both temperature and chemistry change dramatically. Knowing which zone produces which effect is critical for particle engineering.

Zone 1 — Evaporation & Ignition
Temperature: Ambient → 1000–1500 K

Immediately above the nozzle, spray droplets enter the flame base. The solvent evaporates from the droplet surface (controlled by heat transfer from the flame and internal temperature). The solvent vapour ignites at the pilot flame. At low temperatures, the precursor remains dissolved in the droplet — no decomposition yet. The goal of this zone is complete droplet evaporation before the high-temperature core.

Zone 2 — Combustion / Pyrolysis Core
Temperature: 1500–3000 K (peak)

This is the highest-temperature zone — the reaction core. Peak temperature depends on solvent choice and feed rate. Here, the solvent combusts completely, and the metal precursor undergoes complete thermal decomposition. Organic ligands combust; metal atoms react with O₂ to form metal oxide vapour. No solid particles exist yet — everything is in the gas phase. This zone occurs 0–30 mm above the nozzle (for typical lab FSP).

Zone 3 — Nucleation
Temperature: 1500–2000 °C | Location: 30–120 mm above nozzle

As the gas stream expands and cools downstream, the metal oxide vapour becomes supersaturated. When supersaturation S > 1, homogeneous nucleation occurs: embryonic clusters form when their radius exceeds the critical radius r*. Primary particles (1–5 nm) appear here for the first time. Nucleation events are nearly simultaneous across the flame cross-section, contributing to the narrow size distribution characteristic of FSP.

Zone 4 — Particle Growth
Temperature: 800–1500 °C

Nuclei grow by two competing mechanisms: coagulation (particles collide and stick) and sintering (at elevated T, colliding particles fuse into a single sphere). The final primary particle size is set in this zone. The ratio of τ_collision (time between collisions) to τ_sintering (time to complete sintering) determines morphology: if τ_sintering < τ_collision → spherical particles; if τ_collision < τ_sintering → fractal chain aggregates.

Zone 5 — Post-Flame / Quench
Temperature: <800 °C

Rapid cooling occurs above the flame tip. No further significant growth occurs here. Thermophoresis (particle migration from hot to cold regions due to gas molecule momentum gradients) drives particles toward the cooler filter. Rapid quench freezes the particle size and crystal phase. A faster quench can preserve metastable phases; slower quench allows thermodynamic equilibrium (more stable, coarser phases).

📌 Thermophoresis Explained

Thermophoresis is the movement of particles in a temperature gradient — from hot to cold. Gas molecules on the hot side of a particle have higher momentum than on the cold side, creating a net force pushing the particle toward lower temperatures. In FSP, this drives nanoparticles from the hot flame zone toward the cooler filter, aiding collection. This is the same principle that makes smoke particles deposit on cold surfaces near fires.

§7 Summary — Five Flame Zones

  • Zone 1 (ambient→1500K): Droplet evaporation and ignition
  • Zone 2 (1500–3000K): Peak combustion; precursor fully decomposes; metal oxide vapour forms; NO solid yet
  • Zone 3 (1500–2000°C, 30–120mm): Nucleation; supersaturation → primary particles 1–5 nm
  • Zone 4 (800–1500°C): Growth by coagulation + sintering; final size established; morphology set by τ_collision vs τ_sintering
  • Zone 5 (<800°C): Quench; no further growth; thermophoresis drives particles to filter

Particle Collection Systems
Four methods compared — glass fiber, ESP, thermophoretic, cyclone

After the quench zone, nanoparticles are suspended in the gas stream and must be separated and collected. The choice of collection method affects product purity, yield, and particle state (dispersed vs. aggregated).

MethodEfficiencyPrincipleProsCons
Glass Fiber Filter ⭐ Most Used >99.9% Brownian diffusion + mechanical interception Simple, inexpensive, standard lab; highest efficiency Batch collection; can clog at high production rates
Electrostatic Precipitator (ESP) >99% Particles charged by corona discharge → collect on grounded plate Continuous operation possible; high efficiency High-voltage power supply required; more complex setup
Thermophoretic Sampling Low (analysis only) Particles migrate toward cooled TEM grid in T gradient Direct TEM grid deposition for structural analysis Not suitable for bulk collection; sample too small
Cyclone Separator Low for <100 nm Centrifugal inertial separation Effective for coarser particles (>1 µm) Very inefficient for sub-100 nm particles — they follow gas streamlines

Why Glass Fiber Filters Work — The Physics

🔬 Brownian Diffusion Capture

For particles <100 nm, the dominant capture mechanism is Brownian diffusion, not sieving. According to the Stokes-Einstein equation, the diffusion coefficient D ∝ 1/d — smaller particles diffuse faster. As nanoparticles flow through the fibrous filter matrix, they undergo rapid random Brownian motion and collide with (and adhere to) fiber surfaces. The fiber pore size can be much larger than the particle, yet capture is still >99.9% efficient.

💡 Analogy — HEPA Filters

This is exactly the same physics as HEPA (High-Efficiency Particulate Air) filters used in clean rooms and medical environments. HEPA filters capture >99.97% of particles >0.3 µm by Brownian diffusion, interception, and impaction — not by acting as a simple sieve.

§8 Summary

  • Glass fiber filter: >99.9% efficiency; most common; works by Brownian diffusion (D ∝ 1/d, Stokes-Einstein)
  • ESP: >99% efficiency; continuous; needs high voltage; good for scale-up
  • Thermophoretic sampling: TEM analysis only; not for bulk collection
  • Cyclone: inefficient for sub-100 nm; good only for coarse particles (>1 µm)
  • Key insight: nanoparticles captured by Brownian diffusion, NOT sieving — filter pore size is irrelevant

FSP Process Parameters & Their Effects
5 tunable handles: concentration · dispersion gas · flame T · residence time · quench rate
🔑 Key Principle

Each FSP parameter is an independent tunable handle — altering one often shifts the balance of others. Precise control over all five parameters enables engineered nanoparticles with predictable properties. These parameters govern particle size, morphology, crystallinity, surface area, and phase composition.

Parameter 1: Precursor Concentration [M]

Definition: Molar concentration of metal precursor in the solvent feed (e.g., 0.05–1.0 M). Governs the metal flux (moles per second) arriving in the flame.

↑ Increasing [M]
  • Larger primary particle size (more nuclei available → more coagulation)
  • Higher production rate (kg/h yield)
  • Increased surface area per gram (at very low [M])
  • >0.5 M risks sintering and hard agglomeration
  • More combustion load → can lower effective flame T if O₂ limited
↓ Decreasing [M]
  • Smaller primary particle size
  • Narrower size distribution (PSD)
  • Better dispersibility in downstream applications
  • Controls stoichiometry in multi-metal systems
  • Lower production rate
📌 Typical Range

Precursor concentration: 0.05–1.0 M. Above 0.5 M, sintering becomes significant. For research-scale FSP, 0.1–0.5 M is most common.

Parameter 2: Dispersion Gas & Flow Rate

Definition: Gas (typically O₂ or air) used to atomize the liquid precursor feed and sustain combustion. Flow rate (L/min) controls droplet size and spray cone angle.

↑ Higher O₂ Flow
  • Finer droplets (smaller d₃₂) → smaller primary particles
  • Wider spray cone angle
  • Better oxidation (more O₂ available → more complete combustion)
  • Better flame mixing, more turbulent
  • Shorter residence time in hot zone (higher gas velocity)
↓ Lower O₂ Flow
  • Larger droplets → larger particles
  • Narrower spray cone
  • Laminar flame → narrower PSD
  • Longer residence time in hot zone
  • Risk of incomplete combustion → carbon contamination
🔢 O₂ Flow Effects Chain
↑ O₂ flow rate → ↑ KH instability → ↓ droplet d₃₂ → ↓ particle size ↑ O₂ flow rate → ↑ O₂:fuel ratio → more oxidizing atmosphere → complete MₓOᵧ formation ↑ O₂ flow rate → ↑ gas velocity → ↓ residence time in hot zone → smaller particles

Parameter 3: Flame Temperature

What controls it?

  • Solvent type and combustion enthalpy (xylene ~42 MJ/kg > ethanol ~27 MJ/kg)
  • Feed rate of precursor solution (mL/min)
  • O₂:fuel equivalence ratio (φ)
  • Sheath gas flow around the flame (dilutes and cools)
↑ High T (>2000 °C)
  • Promotes crystallinity — more complete sintering during growth
  • Enables synthesis of high-melting oxides (ZrO₂, HfO₂)
  • Phase transitions: TiO₂ anatase → rutile above ~1000 °C
  • Risk: sintering and hard agglomeration above NP melting point
↓ Low T (<1500 °C)
  • Amorphous phases dominate
  • Kinetically trapped metastable structures
  • Lower sintering rate → smaller, more porous particles
  • Incomplete precursor decomposition possible
⚠️ Phase Control with Temperature — TiO₂ Example

Temperature directly controls crystal phase. For TiO₂: anatase phase forms at lower temperatures (600–900 °C) and is preferred for photocatalysis; rutile phase forms above ~1000 °C and is preferred for pigments. By tuning flame temperature, both phases can be produced by FSP.

Parameter 4: Residence Time

Definition: The time nanoparticles spend in the high-temperature flame region (milliseconds to seconds).

Controlled by: flame height, feed rate, gas velocity, nozzle geometry.

Effect: Longer residence time → more coarsening via sintering and coalescence → larger, more crystalline primary particles.

🔢 Residence Time Concept
τ_res ≈ L_flame / v_gas Where L_flame = height of high-T zone, v_gas = gas velocity Longer τ_res → more time for sintering → larger, more crystalline NPs Note: ↑ O₂ flow → ↑ v_gas → ↓ τ_res (inverse relationship)

Parameter 5: Quench Rate

Definition: Rate of post-flame cooling (°C/ms). Controls whether kinetically or thermodynamically favored phases are preserved.

Methods to control quench rate:

  • Dilution gas injection above flame tip
  • Quench rings (water-cooled)
  • Water-cooled reactor walls
  • Electrostatic precipitators (also cool and collect simultaneously)
Fast Quench

"Freezes" kinetically favored phases — amorphous or metastable structures. Particles don't have time to crystallize or coarsen. Used when targeting amorphous materials or preserving metastable phases (e.g., metastable γ-Al₂O₃).

Slow Quench

Allows thermodynamic equilibrium — more stable, crystalline, potentially coarser phases form. Used when targeting well-crystallized, stable phases like α-Al₂O₃ or rutile TiO₂.

🔗 Key Interdependencies
  • Precursor Conc. → Flame Temp.: Higher [M] → more combustion load → lower effective T if O₂ is limited
  • Dispersion Gas → Residence Time: ↑ O₂ flow → higher gas velocity → shorter τ_res in hot zone
  • Flame Temp. → Quench Rate: Higher ΔT requires faster quench to preserve metastable phases

§9 Summary

  • [M]: 0.05–1.0 M; higher [M] → bigger NPs, more yield; >0.5 M risks sintering
  • O₂ flow (5–15 L/min): higher flow → smaller droplets → smaller NPs; also increases oxidation
  • Flame T: controlled by solvent (2-EHA > xylene > EtOH), feed rate, φ; high T → crystalline; low T → amorphous
  • Residence time: longer → coarser, more crystalline NPs; controlled by flame height and gas velocity
  • Quench rate: fast → amorphous/metastable; slow → crystalline/stable; determines final phase
  • Parameters are interdependent — changing one affects others

Particle Formation Mechanisms
Gas-to-particle vs. droplet-to-particle · Nucleation physics · Evaporation number

The Two Competing Routes

There are two possible pathways from liquid precursor droplet to solid nanoparticle. The pathway taken depends critically on the Evaporation Number (Ev).

📐 Evaporation Number (Ev)
Ev = τ_evap / τ_residence τ_evap = time required for complete droplet evaporation τ_residence = time available in the high-temperature zone Ev < 1 → Droplet fully evaporates before leaving hot zone → Gas-to-Particle Route (DESIRED) Ev > 1 → Droplet does not fully evaporate → Droplet-to-Particle Route (UNDESIRED)

Route 1: Gas-to-Particle Conversion (Preferred, Ev < 1)

✅ Preferred Pathway

This is the dominant route in well-optimized FSP. It produces the finest, most uniform, most crystalline nanoparticles.

1
Droplet Evaporation
The entire droplet evaporates completely in the flame zone. The metal precursor also vaporizes/decomposes into metal oxide vapour.
2
Metal Oxide Vapour Formation
All metal atoms are now in the gas phase as MₓOᵧ vapour at very high temperature. The vapour is undersaturated at peak T.
3
Supersaturation (S > 1)
As gas cools downstream, the equilibrium vapour pressure of MₓOᵧ decreases. When actual concentration exceeds the new equilibrium → supersaturation S > 1. The driving force for nucleation builds.
4
Nucleation (r > r*)
Spontaneous formation of stable clusters (nuclei) when cluster radius exceeds the critical radius r*. Below r*, clusters dissolve (surface energy dominates); above r*, clusters are stable and grow (bulk energy dominates).
5
Condensation & Growth
Vapour molecules deposit on stable nuclei. Particles also collide and coalesce (coagulation). At high T, they sinter into spheres. Final particle size: 5–20 nm.

Key Physics of Nucleation

🔢 Critical Radius
r* = 2γΩ / (kT ln S) γ = surface energy of the nanoparticle material (J/m²) Ω = molecular volume of the condensing species (m³) k = Boltzmann constant (1.38×10⁻²³ J/K) T = temperature (K) S = supersaturation ratio = P_actual / P_equilibrium High S (rapid cooling) → small r* → easier nucleation → many small nuclei → small particles Low S (slow cooling) → large r* → fewer, larger particles

This is the Kelvin equation for the critical nucleus radius in homogeneous nucleation

🔢 Nucleation Rate
J = A · exp(−ΔG*/kT) J = nucleation rate (nuclei/m³·s) A = pre-exponential frequency factor ΔG* = activation energy for nucleation = (16π/3) · γ³Ω² / (kT ln S)² Higher T or higher S → lower ΔG* → exponentially higher J Near-simultaneous nucleation → narrow particle size distribution
🔢 Growth Rate (Diffusion-Controlled)
dr/dt = D_M (C − C_s) / (ρ_p · r) D_M = diffusion coefficient of metal oxide vapour C = actual vapour concentration C_s = saturation concentration at particle surface ρ_p = particle density r = current particle radius Larger particles grow faster (proportional to C−C_s) but the rate per unit radius decreases This self-limiting growth contributes to narrow size distributions

Route 2: Droplet-to-Particle Route (Undesired, Ev > 1)

⚠️ Problematic Pathway

When the droplet does not fully evaporate before particle formation, this route produces micron-scale, hollow, or porous particles — far too large for nanoscale applications. A bimodal particle size distribution (both nm and µm particles) is the diagnostic signature of droplet-route contamination.

1
Droplet Heating & Surface Evaporation
Outer solvent evaporates from the droplet surface. As it does, the precursor concentration increases toward the droplet interior. A concentration gradient develops (dilute core, concentrated surface).
2
Shell Precipitation
Surface concentration reaches the saturation limit for the metal precursor → a solid or semi-solid crust (shell) precipitates at the droplet surface. A hollow sphere is born with a liquid core.
3
Puffing, Bursting, or Densification
Internal liquid vaporizes → pressure builds inside the shell. Three outcomes: (a) Puffing — shell inflates and remains hollow; (b) Bursting — shell ruptures, releasing fragments; (c) Dense sphere — liquid re-distributes before shell fully solidifies.
4
Final Product: Micron-Scale Hollow/Porous Particle
Particle size ≈ original droplet size (µm range). These contaminate the nanoparticle product and degrade performance. Bimodal PSD (a peak at nm and one at µm) diagnoses this route.
📌 How to Avoid the Droplet Route
  • Use a solvent with a lower boiling point (evaporates faster → reduces τ_evap)
  • Increase dispersion gas flow (smaller droplets → faster complete evaporation)
  • Increase flame temperature (faster heating → faster evaporation)
  • Reduce precursor concentration (less material per droplet → easier evaporation)
  • Goal: always maintain Ev < 1

§10 Summary

  • Ev = τ_evap/τ_residence; Ev<1 → gas route (desired); Ev>1 → droplet route (avoid)
  • Gas-to-particle route: complete evaporation → vapour → supersaturation → nucleation → condensation → 5–20 nm spherical crystalline NPs
  • Critical radius: r* = 2γΩ/(kT ln S); higher S → smaller r* → easier nucleation
  • Nucleation rate: J = A·exp(−ΔG*/kT); near-simultaneous → narrow PSD
  • Droplet route: incomplete evaporation → shell precipitation → puffing/bursting → µm-scale hollow particles; diagnosed by bimodal PSD

Sintering & Agglomeration
Post-nucleation evolution · τ_collision vs τ_sintering · Three particle types

Three Levels of Particle Structure

●
Primary Particle

The smallest individual nanocrystal. A single crystalline domain. Formed by nucleation + growth by condensation. This is the "true" nanoparticle size. Measured by XRD (Scherrer equation) or HRTEM.

◎◎
Aggregate (Hard)

Primary particles fused by solid-state sintering at high temperature. Particles share crystalline necks. Cannot be redispersed — the fusion is permanent. Measured by TEM.

Irreversible

◎◎◎
Agglomerate (Soft)

Groups of aggregates held together by van der Waals forces. Weaker physical attraction. Can be redispersed by ultrasonication or chemical dispersants.

Reversible

Sintering Physics

🔬 What is Sintering?

Sintering is the solid-state diffusion-driven fusion of two touching nanoparticles into a single, larger spherical particle. At high temperatures, atoms diffuse along grain boundaries and free surfaces, eliminating the interface between colliding particles. Sintering requires high temperature because atomic diffusion is thermally activated (Arrhenius-type). For nanoparticles, sintering temperatures are significantly lower than for bulk materials due to the high surface-area-to-volume ratio.

🔢 Sintering Time
τ_s = d⁴kT / (64γΩD_s) d = primary particle diameter k = Boltzmann constant T = temperature γ = surface energy Ω = atomic volume D_s = surface diffusion coefficient (exponentially T-dependent: D_s = D₀ exp(-E_a/kT)) Critical insight: τ_s ∝ d⁴ — larger particles sinter MUCH more slowly! Higher T → much faster sintering (D_s exponentially increases with T)
🔢 Neck Growth Rate
(x/r)² = (56 D_s γ Ω / kT) · (t / r³) x = neck radius between two sintering particles r = particle radius t = time Neck grows as t^(1/2) — initially rapid, then slows as neck grows

The τ_collision vs τ_sintering Race

The final morphology of FSP nanoparticles is determined by the competition between two timescales:

τ_sintering << τ_collision

Sintering completes before the next collision. Each collision event fuses two particles into one sphere, which fully relaxes to a sphere before the next collision arrives.

Result: Spherical primary particles. This occurs at high temperatures where sintering is rapid.

τ_collision << τ_sintering

New collisions occur before sintering is complete. Particles chain together, forming fractal aggregate structures. Sintering creates permanent necks but incomplete coalescence.

Result: Chain-like fractal aggregates. This occurs at lower temperatures (below sintering onset).

🔢 BET Particle Size from Surface Area
d_BET = 6 / (ρ_p · SSA) d_BET = equivalent spherical diameter from BET surface area (m) ρ_p = particle density (kg/m³) SSA = specific surface area measured by N₂ BET adsorption (m²/kg) d_BET represents the primary particle size (assuming non-porous spheres) If d_BET >> d_XRD, particles are porous or agglomerated
📌 Coagulation Rate

The rate of particle collisions (coagulation) scales with the square of particle number concentration: Rate ∝ N². This means that higher precursor concentrations (more nuclei formed → higher N) lead to much more rapid coagulation and therefore larger final particles. This explains why increasing [M] increases particle size.

§11 Summary

  • Three particle types: Primary (single crystal, irreducible) → Aggregate (sintered, hard, irreversible) → Agglomerate (van der Waals, soft, reversible)
  • Sintering time: τ_s = d⁴kT/(64γΩD_s); scales as d⁴; higher T → faster sintering
  • τ_sintering << τ_collision → spherical primaries (high T); τ_collision << τ_sintering → fractal aggregates (low T)
  • Coagulation rate ∝ N² → high [precursor] → rapid coagulation → larger NPs
  • d_BET = 6/(ρ_p·SSA); measures equivalent spherical diameter from surface area measurement

🔧 Polymer Nanocomposites

Challenges & Limitations of FSP
Six major challenge areas · Future research directions
01 — Scalability & Cost
  • High-purity organic precursors are expensive (e.g., metal 2-ethylhexanoates)
  • Energy-intensive: combustion of organic solvents at ~2500 °C
  • Scale-up leads to particle agglomeration — requires dispersants or gas-phase tuning
  • Batch-to-batch reproducibility deteriorates at industrial scale
  • Multiple parallel nozzles needed for kg/hr production → complex engineering
02 — Particle Agglomeration
  • Freshly formed NPs are thermophoretically driven to aggregate
  • Hard agglomerates form above sintering temperature — irreversible
  • Degrades catalytic surface area, drug delivery payload, optical properties
  • Mitigation: in-situ surface functionalization, faster quench cooling, electrostatic dispersion
  • In-situ surface modification with silanes or phosphonates during FSP is possible
03 — Precursor Incompatibility
  • Multi-component systems require matched solubility and volatility
  • Immiscible precursors → phase segregation and stoichiometric deviation
  • Heavy metals (W, Mo, Re) lack suitable organic precursors → limits accessible compositions
  • Side reactions can produce unintended phases (e.g., perovskite vs. spinel)
04 — Toxicology & Safety
  • Ultrafine particles (<10 nm) penetrate lung alveoli and can cross the blood–brain barrier
  • Metal oxide NPs (CuO, ZnO, TiO₂) show dose-dependent cytotoxicity
  • Inhalation risk in open FSP setups → requires HEPA filtration and full PPE
  • Regulatory frameworks (REACH, EPA) still evolving for engineered NPs
  • Fire/explosion risk from flammable organic solvents
05 — Phase & Crystal Control
  • Polymorphic systems (TiO₂: anatase vs. rutile) sensitive to flame temperature
  • Mixed phases can reduce catalytic or electronic performance
  • Quench rate and O₂:fuel ratio must be tightly controlled
  • In-situ XRD monitoring is expensive and technically demanding
  • Predicting phase outcomes in multi-metal systems is complex
06 — Environmental & Life-Cycle Impact
  • CO₂ and CO emissions from solvent combustion → carbon footprint concerns
  • Solvent selection impacts green chemistry metrics (E-factor)
  • NP fate in ecosystems poorly understood — nano-ecotoxicology is an emerging field
  • End-of-life recycling of NP-containing products largely unsolved
  • Water and soil contamination risks from NP release

Future Research Directions

🤖 AI-Assisted Design

Machine learning models predicting FSP process parameters from target NP properties. Bayesian optimization of multi-parameter FSP design space. Autonomous synthesis platforms.

🌿 Green Solvents

Bio-derived, low-emission solvents (e.g., ethyl lactate, limonene) to reduce carbon footprint of FSP. Solvent mixtures optimized for both ΔH_comb and bio-renewability.

🔬 In-Situ Diagnostics

Real-time SAXS/WAXS (X-ray scattering) for particle size and phase monitoring during FSP. Laser-Induced Incandescence (LII) for soot/particle temperature measurement. Closed-loop feedback process control.

§14 Summary — Key Strengths vs. Challenges

  • Strengths: continuous one-step synthesis, precise size/composition control, scalable mg→kg/hr, versatile, in-situ doping
  • Challenge 1 (Cost): expensive precursors, energy-intensive, agglomeration at scale
  • Challenge 2 (Agglomeration): hard agglomerates degrade performance; mitigated by in-situ functionalization or fast quench
  • Challenge 3 (Precursors): heavy metals (W, Mo, Re) incompatible; phase segregation in multi-component systems
  • Challenge 4 (Safety): ultrafine NPs are lung/BBB hazards; flammable solvents; evolving regulations
  • Challenge 5 (Phase control): polymorphic systems sensitive to T; mixed phases degrade performance
  • Challenge 6 (Environment): CO₂ from combustion; NP ecotoxicology poorly understood
  • Future: AI-assisted design, green solvents, in-situ SAXS/WAXS/LII diagnostics
