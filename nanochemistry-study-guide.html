Introduction to Nanochemistry

Definition · Scale · Why nanoscale is special · Examples
Definition

Nanochemistry is the branch of chemistry that deals with the design, synthesis, characterization, and application of materials at the nanoscale — typically between 1 and 100 nanometers (nm).

Scale Reference
1 nm = 10⁻⁹ meters = 1 billionth of a meter
Nanoscale range: 1 nm – 100 nm

Comparison: A human hair ≈ 80,000 nm wide
A DNA strand ≈ 2 nm wide
An atom ≈ 0.1 – 0.5 nm

Why is the Nanoscale Special?

At the nanoscale, matter behaves fundamentally differently from bulk matter. Three key reasons explain this:

⚖️

High Surface Area-to-Volume Ratio

As particle size shrinks, proportionally more atoms sit on the surface. A 1 nm gold particle has ~100% of atoms on its surface. Surface atoms are more reactive — this dramatically changes chemical and physical behavior.

⚛️

Quantum Effects

Below ~10 nm, electrons are confined in tiny spaces. This quantum confinement changes optical, magnetic, and electronic properties. Energy levels become discrete (like atoms) rather than continuous bands.

🔬

Surface-Dominated Behavior

With most atoms on the surface, surface forces (van der Waals, electrostatic) dominate over gravity and inertia. Surface chemistry controls nearly all macroscopic behavior.

Striking Examples of Nanoscale Behavior

MaterialBulk BehaviorNanoscale BehaviorReason
Gold (Au)Metallic yellow, chemically inertAppears red or purple; catalytically active below 5 nmQuantum confinement changes plasmon resonance
CarbonSoft (graphite) or hard (diamond)Carbon nanotubes: ~100× stronger than steelPerfect sp² bonding network; no grain boundaries
Titanium dioxideWhite powder, stableHighly photocatalytic, breaks down organic pollutantsIncreased surface area; bandgap shift
Iron (Fe)Ferromagnetic, silver-greySuperparamagnetic (no permanent magnet)Single-domain magnetic behavior below critical size

Examples of Nanomaterial Morphologies (Shapes)

Nanomaterials come in a remarkable variety of shapes, each with distinct properties:

  • Nanoparticles (Au, Ag): Roughly spherical, 1–100 nm diameter
  • Buckminsterfullerne (C₆₀): Soccer-ball shaped carbon cage; 0.7 nm diameter
  • FePt Nanospheres: Core-shell magnetic structures
  • Titanium Nanoflowers: Branched, high-surface-area structures for catalysis
  • Silver Nanocubes: Cubic geometry with sharp edges; strong plasmonic effects
  • SnO₂ Nanoflowers: Hierarchical structures for gas sensing
Key Insight

Shape matters as much as size. Nanoparticles of the same material but different shapes can have completely different optical, catalytic, and biological properties. This is why morphology control during synthesis is critically important.

Section Summary

  • Nanochemistry covers 1–100 nm scale materials
  • Nanomaterials differ from bulk due to: high surface-to-volume ratio, quantum effects, surface-dominated behavior
  • Gold turns red/purple at nanoscale; carbon nanotubes are 100× stronger than steel
  • Morphology (shape) of nanomaterials critically affects their properties

Classification of Nanomaterials

By Dimension · Origin · Composition · Application

Nanomaterials can be classified in four major ways. Understanding each classification helps predict properties and select the right material for a given application.

Classification 1: Based on Dimensions

This is the most common classification — based on how many dimensions are in the nanoscale range (1–100 nm).

0D

Zero-Dimensional

All 3 dimensions ≤ 100 nm. Acts like a point or atom. Examples: Quantum dots, fullerenes, nanoparticles

1D

One-Dimensional

Two dimensions ≤ 100 nm; one is large. Long and thin. Examples: Nanowires, nanorods, nanotubes

2D

Two-Dimensional

One dimension ≤ 100 nm; forms flat sheets. Examples: Graphene, nanosheets, nanofilms

3D

Three-Dimensional

Bulk structure with nanoscale internal features. Examples: Nanocomposites, nanocrystalline materials

Memory Tip

Think of dimensions as the number of dimensions that are LARGE (above nanoscale): 0D = all nano; 1D = one large dimension; 2D = two large dimensions; 3D = all three large but with nano features inside.

Classification 2: Based on Origin

🌿 Natural Nanomaterials
  • Formed by natural processes (volcanic eruptions, forest fires, biological reactions)
  • Not engineered or designed
  • Examples: volcanic ash, milk proteins (casein micelles ~200 nm), sea spray, viruses
🔬 Engineered (Synthetic) Nanomaterials
  • Intentionally manufactured with controlled size, shape, and surface
  • Designed for specific applications
  • Examples: carbon nanotubes, silver nanoparticles, graphene oxide, quantum dots
⚠️ Incidental Nanomaterials
  • Produced unintentionally as byproducts of human activity
  • Examples: diesel exhaust particles, welding fumes, tobacco smoke particles
  • Often considered environmental/health hazards

Classification 3: Based on Composition

TypeDefinitionKey PropertiesExamples
Carbon-BasedComposed entirely of carbon atomsExtreme strength, high conductivity, unique geometryFullerenes (C₆₀), graphene, CNTs
Metal-BasedPure metals or metal oxides at nanoscaleElectrical, catalytic, antimicrobial, opticalAu, Ag, ZnO, TiO₂
PolymericNanostructures from organic polymersBiodegradable, tailorable for drug releaseDendrimers, micelles, nanogels
Ceramic-BasedInorganic, non-metallic nanomaterialsHigh thermal/chemical stabilitySilica (SiO₂), alumina (Al₂O₃)
CompositeCombination of two or more typesSynergistic propertiesClay-polymer nanocomposites

Classification 4: Based on Application

💡

Electronics-Grade

Graphene, silver nanowires, CNTs — designed for conductivity, magnetic properties, miniaturization

💊

Biomedical

Liposomes, gold nanoshells, dendrimers — for diagnostics, therapy, drug delivery

🌿

Environmental

Iron oxide NPs, TiO₂ photocatalysts — for cleanup, pollution control, water treatment

✨

Cosmetic & Textile

Nano-zinc oxide (sunscreens), nano-silver (antibacterial fabrics)

Section Summary

  • Nanomaterials classified by: Dimension (0D/1D/2D/3D), Origin (natural/engineered/incidental), Composition (carbon/metal/polymer/ceramic), Application
  • 0D = quantum dots, fullerenes | 1D = nanowires, CNTs | 2D = graphene | 3D = nanocomposites
  • Carbon-based NMs: extreme strength + conductivity; Metal-based: catalytic + optical

Physical & Chemical Properties of Nanomaterials

Why nanomaterials are so uniquely powerful

Nanomaterials exhibit fundamentally different properties from their bulk counterparts due to small size (1–100 nm), high surface area-to-volume ratio, and quantum confinement effects.

Chemical Properties

1. High Reactivity

Due to the large surface area-to-volume ratio, more atoms are exposed on the surface and available for reactions. In bulk materials, most atoms are buried inside and contribute nothing to chemical reactions.

Example

Nano-TiO₂ (titanium dioxide) is far more chemically active than bulk TiO₂. A 1 g sample of 5 nm TiO₂ nanoparticles has roughly 600× more surface area than the same mass of 3 µm TiO₂ powder — hence 600× more reactive surface.

2. Surface Functionalization

The surface of nanomaterials can be chemically modified ("functionalized") by attaching molecules or functional groups. This allows tuning of:

  • Solubility (making hydrophobic nanoparticles water-soluble)
  • Biocompatibility (for medical use)
  • Targeting ability (attaching antibodies to find cancer cells)
  • Charge and stability in solution

3. Catalytic Properties

Nanoparticles like gold (Au) and platinum (Pt) act as excellent catalysts. Increased surface atom density reduces activation energy. A startling fact:

Key Fact

In bulk, gold is chemically inert — it doesn't react. But gold nanoparticles below 5 nm diameter become a powerful catalyst for CO oxidation at room temperature. This is purely a nanoscale effect.

4. Chemical Stability (Variable)

  • Stable: Carbon nanotubes are extremely chemically stable
  • Unstable: Iron nanoparticles oxidize/corrode rapidly in air
  • Stability depends on composition and surface chemistry

Physical Properties

1. Large Surface Area-to-Volume Ratio

Mathematical Concept
For a sphere: Surface Area = 4πr² | Volume = (4/3)πr³
SA/V ratio = 3/r

As radius r decreases → SA/V increases sharply
10 nm sphere: SA/V = 3/5 × 10⁶ m⁻¹ = 600,000 m⁻¹
1 cm sphere: SA/V = 3/0.005 = 600 m⁻¹
This exponential increase in surface area at the nanoscale is the foundation of most unique nano properties

2. Quantum Effects (Quantum Confinement)

When a particle's size approaches the de Broglie wavelength of electrons (~1–10 nm), electrons are quantum confined. This means:

  • Energy levels become discrete (like atoms) instead of continuous bands
  • The bandgap becomes size-dependent
  • Optical, electronic, and magnetic properties change with size
Best Example: Quantum Dots

Quantum dots (QDs) are semiconductor nanoparticles. A 2 nm CdSe QD emits blue light; a 6 nm CdSe QD emits red light — same material, different color purely because of size! This is quantum confinement.

3. Mechanical Strength

  • Many nanomaterials are far stronger than their bulk counterparts
  • Carbon nanotubes: ~100× stronger than steel at a fraction of the weight
  • Nanocrystalline metals are harder than coarse-grained metals (Hall-Petch relationship: smaller grains → harder material)

4. Optical Properties

Size-dependent optical behavior is one of the most dramatic nanoscale phenomena:

  • Gold nanoparticles absorb specific wavelengths due to surface plasmon resonance (SPR)
  • 5–20 nm Au NPs appear red; 40+ nm appear blue/grey
  • Quantum dots fluoresce in size-tunable colors

5. Electrical Conductivity

  • Graphene: highest known electrical conductivity (electron mobility ~200,000 cm²/V·s)
  • CNTs: metallic or semiconducting depending on chirality (how they're rolled)
  • Metal nanoparticles: conductivity altered by electron confinement and surface scattering

6. Thermal Conductivity

  • Graphene: ~5000 W/m·K (among highest known for any material)
  • CNTs: ~3000 W/m·K along tube axis
  • Critical for heat management in nanoelectronics
PropertyBulk BehaviorNanoscale BehaviorApplication
OpticalFixed color based on electronic structureSize-tunable color (quantum dots, plasmon resonance)Displays, bioimaging, sensors
MechanicalStandard strength100× stronger (CNTs)Aerospace composites, armor
ElectricalFixed conductivityCan be metallic or semiconductor (CNTs)Nanoelectronics, transistors
MagneticFerromagnetic with domainsSuperparamagnetic (single-domain)MRI contrast agents, data storage
ThermalStandard conductivityUltra-high (graphene)Thermal management in chips
ReactivityLimited surface availableExtremely high surface reactivityCatalysis, drug delivery

Section Summary

  • High SA/V ratio → more reactive surface atoms → higher reactivity and catalytic activity
  • Quantum confinement → size-dependent optical/electronic/magnetic properties
  • CNTs: 100× stronger than steel; graphene: highest thermal & electrical conductivity
  • Gold NPs = red/purple (not yellow) due to surface plasmon resonance
  • Surface functionalization allows tuning of solubility, biocompatibility, and targeting

XRD — X-Ray Diffraction

Structure · Crystallinity · Crystallite size · Lattice parameters
Characterization Technique

X-Ray Diffraction (XRD)

XRD is a powerful, non-destructive analytical technique used in nanochemistry to analyze the structure and crystallinity of nanomaterials. It provides information about atomic arrangement, lattice parameters, phase composition, and crystallite size.

How XRD Works — Step by Step

X-ray beam generated
→
Beam directed at crystalline sample
→
X-rays scatter at atomic planes
→
Constructive interference at specific angles
→
Diffraction pattern recorded
→
Analysis yields structure info
The Core Principle: Bragg's Law

When X-rays hit a crystal, they reflect off parallel atomic planes. Constructive interference occurs only when the path difference equals a whole number of wavelengths.

Bragg's Law (Critical Formula)
nλ = 2d·sinθ

n = order of reflection (integer: 1, 2, 3...)
λ = wavelength of X-rays (~0.154 nm for Cu Kα)
d = interplanar spacing (distance between atomic planes)
θ = angle of incidence/diffraction
By measuring diffraction angles (2θ), we calculate d-spacing and identify crystal structure

Key Applications of XRD in Nanochemistry

1. Phase Identification

Every crystalline material produces a unique XRD pattern (like a fingerprint). By comparing with standard databases (JCPDS/ICDD), researchers identify which crystalline phases are present — whether a single phase or a mixture.

2. Crystallinity Assessment

XRD distinguishes between crystalline (sharp peaks), amorphous (broad humps), and mixed materials. High crystallinity = better structural order = often better electrical properties.

3. Crystallite Size Determination — Scherrer Equation

Scherrer Equation (Critical for Nanochemistry)
D = Kλ / (β·cosθ)

D = average crystallite size (nm)
K = Scherrer constant (~0.9 for spherical particles)
λ = X-ray wavelength
β = full width at half maximum (FWHM) of peak (in radians)
θ = Bragg angle
Broader diffraction peaks = smaller crystallites. This is the most common method to estimate nanoparticle size from XRD data.

4. Lattice Parameter Measurements

XRD precisely measures lattice parameters (a, b, c, α, β, γ) — the dimensions of the unit cell. Changes in lattice parameters reveal doping, strain, or defects in nanomaterials.

5. Strain and Defect Analysis

Peak broadening in XRD has two contributions: crystallite size (Scherrer) AND lattice strain. The Williamson-Hall plot separates these contributions, giving strain information.

6. Interface Characterization

In nanocomposites, XRD reveals atomic arrangements at interfaces between different materials — critical for understanding composite properties.

XRD Limitation

XRD gives average crystallite size over millions of particles — it cannot reveal individual particle morphology or surface features. It must be combined with SEM/TEM for complete characterization.

Section Summary

  • XRD = non-destructive technique using X-ray diffraction patterns to study crystal structure
  • Bragg's law: nλ = 2d·sinθ — relates diffraction angle to atomic plane spacing
  • Scherrer equation: D = Kλ/(β·cosθ) — wider peaks → smaller crystallites
  • Applications: phase ID, crystallinity, crystallite size, lattice parameters, strain/defects, interface characterization

SEM — Scanning Electron Microscopy

Surface morphology · Chemical analysis · High-resolution imaging
Characterization Technique

Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM)

SEM provides high-resolution surface images of nanomaterials using a focused electron beam. It reveals surface morphology, size, and shape at the nanoscale, and can be coupled with EDX for chemical analysis.

How SEM Works

Electron gun generates focused beam
→
Condenser & objective lenses focus beam to <5 nm spot
→
Beam scans across sample surface (raster scan)
→
Electrons interact with sample → secondary and backscattered electrons emitted
→
Detectors collect signals → computer builds 3D-looking image

Types of Signals Detected in SEM

Signal TypeOriginInformation Provided
Secondary Electrons (SE)Outer 1–10 nm of sampleSurface topography, morphology (most common mode)
Backscattered Electrons (BSE)Deeper sample regionsAtomic number contrast — heavier elements appear brighter
X-rays (EDX)Sample atoms excited by beamElemental composition — what elements are present
Auger ElectronsNear-surface (<3 nm)Surface elemental composition (Auger spectroscopy)

Key Roles of SEM in Nanochemistry

1. Imaging Nanomaterials

SEM provides high-resolution images revealing size, shape, and surface features of nanoparticles, nanowires, nanotubes, and other nanostructures. Resolution: typically 1–20 nm depending on instrument.

2. Chemical Analysis (SEM-EDX)

Coupling SEM with Energy Dispersive X-ray Spectroscopy (EDS/EDX) enables elemental analysis of the nanomaterial. This combination is extremely powerful: see morphology AND know what it's made of simultaneously.

3. Studying Nanomaterial Interactions

SEM can image nanomaterials interacting with cells or tissues in the biomedical field — crucial for understanding biocompatibility, cellular uptake, and potential toxicity.

4. Material Science Applications

SEM is used to characterize polymers, metals, ceramics, composites — understanding structure-property relationships across all nanomaterial categories.

Advantages & Limitations of SEM

✅ Advantages
  • High resolution surface imaging (down to ~1 nm)
  • 3D-like appearance of samples
  • Large depth of field
  • Combined elemental analysis (with EDX)
  • Applicable to wide range of materials
⚠️ Limitations
  • Requires vacuum environment
  • Non-conductive samples need coating (gold/platinum)
  • Surface-only information (no internal structure)
  • Sample preparation can alter morphology
SEM vs TEM Key Difference

SEM gives surface images by scanning the electron beam over the sample. TEM transmits electrons through the sample to give internal structure information. SEM = surface; TEM = inside.

Section Summary

  • SEM uses focused electron beam scanning to create high-resolution surface images
  • Secondary electrons → surface morphology; Backscattered electrons → atomic number contrast
  • SEM-EDX combination = morphology + elemental composition simultaneously
  • Used for imaging NMs, studying biomedical interactions, material characterization
  • Limitation: surface only; non-conductors need conductive coating

TEM — Transmission Electron Microscopy

Atomic-level imaging · Internal structure · Lattice fringes
Characterization Technique

Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM)

TEM is a high-resolution technique that transmits electrons through an ultra-thin sample to produce images at atomic resolution. It reveals internal structure, crystal lattice, and composition of nanomaterials at the sub-nanometer scale.

How TEM Works

Electron gun produces high-energy electron beam (60–300 keV)
→
Condenser lenses focus beam onto thin sample (<100 nm thick)
→
Electrons transmit through sample (hence "transmission")
→
Objective lens forms intermediate image; projector lens magnifies
→
Image recorded on CCD camera or fluorescent screen

Key Capabilities of TEM

1. High-Resolution Imaging (HRTEM)

High-Resolution TEM resolves individual atom columns. Lattice fringes (parallel lines visible at high magnification) correspond to rows of atoms in the crystal. Spacing between fringes = interplanar d-spacing. Crystal defects (dislocations, vacancies) are directly visible.

2. Structural and Chemical Information

  • Crystal structure identification via Selected Area Electron Diffraction (SAED)
  • Lattice parameters, crystallographic orientation
  • Phase boundaries and interfaces at atomic scale

3. Morphological Studies

TEM precisely determines size, shape, and size distribution of nanoparticles. More accurate than SEM for particles below 20 nm.

4. Analytical TEM (combined techniques)

Technique Coupled with TEMInformation Obtained
EDX (Energy Dispersive X-ray)Elemental composition with nanometer spatial resolution
EELS (Electron Energy Loss Spectroscopy)Electronic structure, bonding, light element detection (C, N, O)
SAED (Selected Area Electron Diffraction)Crystal structure, orientation, polycrystallinity
STEM (Scanning TEM)Atomic-column imaging; HAADF contrast for heavy atoms

5. In-situ TEM

Advanced TEM allows real-time observation of nanomaterials during synthesis, heating, cooling, or under applied fields. This reveals dynamic processes like crystal growth, phase transitions, and nanoparticle coalescence as they happen.

TEM Applications in Nanochemistry

💊

Drug Delivery

Characterize liposomes, dendrimers, and polymeric NPs — size, shape, and drug encapsulation

⚗️

Catalysis

Image catalyst nanoparticles on supports; see active surface sites at atomic level

🔬

Materials Science

Study thin films, nanowires, nanotubes — internal defects and grain boundaries

🧬

Biological Systems

Analyze NM-cell interactions; Cryo-TEM for biological macromolecules

Sample Preparation for TEM

TEM requires ultra-thin samples (<100 nm). Preparation methods include:

  • Ultramicrotomy: Sectioning embedded samples with diamond knife
  • Focused Ion Beam (FIB): Ion beam milling to precise thickness
  • Dropcast: For nanoparticle suspensions — drop on carbon-coated TEM grid and dry
TEM Limitations

TEM requires high vacuum and ultra-thin samples. Beam damage is possible for sensitive materials. Sample preparation is time-consuming and can alter the material being studied. Not suitable for thick, wet, or beam-sensitive samples in conventional mode.

Section Summary

  • TEM transmits electrons through ultra-thin samples → atomic-level internal structure
  • HRTEM reveals lattice fringes and crystal defects directly
  • Combined with EDX (elemental), EELS (bonding), SAED (crystal structure)
  • In-situ TEM = real-time observation of nanomaterial dynamics
  • Limitations: requires high vacuum, ultra-thin samples, complex preparation

EDX — Energy Dispersive X-Ray Spectroscopy

Elemental identification · Quantification · Mapping
Characterization Technique

Energy Dispersive X-Ray Spectroscopy (EDX / EDS / EDXA)

EDX identifies and quantifies the elemental composition of nanomaterials by detecting characteristic X-rays emitted when the sample is bombarded with an electron beam. It is almost always paired with SEM or TEM.

Physical Principle of EDX

High-energy electrons from SEM/TEM hit sample
→
Inner-shell electrons in atoms are ejected
→
Outer-shell electrons fill the vacancy
→
Energy difference emitted as characteristic X-rays
→
Each element emits unique X-ray energy → element identified
Key Concept

Every element emits X-rays at unique, characteristic energies (like an elemental fingerprint). The intensity of these X-rays tells you how much of each element is present (quantitative analysis).

EDX in Combination with SEM and TEM

SEM-EDX
  • Most common combination
  • SEM provides surface morphology image
  • EDX gives elemental composition of surface regions
  • Can map element distribution across surface area
  • Spatial resolution: ~1 µm (limited by beam-sample interaction volume)
TEM-EDX
  • Higher resolution elemental mapping
  • Probes internal nanostructure composition
  • Spatial resolution: <1 nm (sub-nanometer)
  • Can map individual atomic columns in STEM mode
  • Essential for core-shell nanoparticle characterization

Applications of EDX in Nanochemistry

Application AreaHow EDX Helps
Composite NanoparticlesCharacterizes composition and distribution of elements in hybrid nanostructures
Nanoparticle SynthesisConfirms successful formation and desired elemental presence in product
Material ScienceLinks composition to mechanical, electrical, or optical behavior
Drug DeliveryTracks distribution of nanoparticles within cells or tissues
Environmental StudiesDetects and quantifies heavy metals in environmental nanomaterials
Medical DevicesInvestigates elemental composition of corrosion products (metallosis)

Advantages & Limitations

✅ Advantages
  • Fast and relatively easy to perform
  • Works for metals, ceramics, polymers
  • Provides both qualitative (identification) and quantitative (concentration) data
  • Can create element distribution maps (EDX mapping)
⚠️ Limitations
  • Limited spatial resolution in SEM-EDX (~1 µm)
  • Non-conductors need conductive coating (may introduce artifacts)
  • Poor detection of very light elements (H, He, Li)
  • Overlapping X-ray peaks for some elements can cause confusion

Section Summary

  • EDX detects characteristic X-rays → identifies and quantifies elements in nanomaterials
  • Always paired with SEM (surface analysis) or TEM (internal analysis)
  • SEM-EDX: spatial resolution ~1 µm; TEM-EDX: sub-nanometer resolution
  • Applications: nanoparticle synthesis confirmation, drug delivery tracking, environmental heavy metal detection
  • Limitation: poor for light elements (H, He, Li); non-conductors need coating

Electro-Optical Characterization

How electric fields change optical properties · Techniques · Applications
Definition

Electro-optical characterization in nanochemistry studies how nanomaterials interact with electric and magnetic fields, and how these interactions affect their optical properties (absorption, emission, scattering, refractive index).

Spectroscopic Techniques

1. UV-Vis Spectroscopy

Measures absorption and transmission of UV and visible light by nanomaterials. Provides information about:

  • Electronic structure and bandgap
  • Particle size (via plasmon resonance peak position for metal NPs)
  • Concentration of nanomaterial in solution (Beer-Lambert law)
Beer-Lambert Law (UV-Vis)
A = εcl

A = absorbance | ε = molar extinction coefficient | c = concentration | l = path length
Used to determine nanoparticle concentration in solution

2. Photoluminescence (PL) Spectroscopy

Measures light emitted by nanomaterials after absorbing photons. Especially important for:

  • Quantum dots: PL reveals exact bandgap and size distribution
  • Detecting defects (trap states cause broader emission)
  • Energy level analysis

3. Raman Spectroscopy

Analyzes scattered light from nanomaterials. When monochromatic laser light hits the material, most photons scatter elastically (Rayleigh) but a tiny fraction scatter inelastically (Raman). Raman shift reveals vibrational modes and molecular structure.

  • Graphene: characteristic G band (~1580 cm⁻¹) and 2D band (~2700 cm⁻¹)
  • CNTs: radial breathing mode (RBM) identifies nanotube diameter
  • Identifies crystalline vs. amorphous phases

Electro-Optical Measurement Methods

Electro-optic Modulation

Using applied electric fields to change optical properties of nanomaterials. The refractive index or birefringence changes with field — used in optical switches and modulators.

Electro-optic Kerr Effect

The change in refractive index of a material proportional to the square of the electric field (E²). Important for nanomaterial-based optical devices.

Kerr Effect
Δn = λ·K·E²

Δn = change in refractive index | λ = wavelength | K = Kerr constant | E = electric field

Dielectric Spectroscopy

Measures how nanomaterials polarize in response to oscillating electric fields across a wide range of frequencies. Reveals polarization mechanisms, charge transport, and interfacial effects.

Near-field Scanning Optical Microscopy (NSOM/SNOM)

Uses a nanoscale optical probe (aperture <100 nm) to image optical properties at spatial resolutions beyond the diffraction limit. Enables nanoscale optical imaging — impossible with conventional optics.

Applications of Electro-optical Characterization

💡

Nanophotonics

Design materials for optical sensors, switches, data storage

🧫

Biosensors

Optical response linked to electrical changes when biomolecules bind

📺

Displays & Lighting

Tunable optical properties for advanced display tech and efficient LEDs

☀️

Photovoltaics

Light-nanomaterial interaction for enhanced solar energy conversion

Section Summary

  • Electro-optical characterization = studying how electric/magnetic fields affect optical properties of NMs
  • UV-Vis: bandgap, concentration; PL: energy levels, defects; Raman: vibrational modes, crystal structure
  • Kerr effect: electric field changes refractive index (Δn = λKE²)
  • Dielectric spectroscopy: polarization and charge behavior vs frequency
  • Applications: nanophotonics, biosensors, displays, solar cells, catalysis, drug delivery

Current Trends in Micro to Nanoscale Analysis

Advanced imaging · AI in nano analysis · Emerging methods

Modern nanoscale analysis is rapidly evolving. Key trends focus on higher resolution, multimodal approaches (combining multiple techniques), and AI-assisted analysis.

Advanced Imaging Techniques (Ultra-Resolution Visualization)

TechniqueHow it WorksWhat it RevealsSpecial Feature
AFM (Atomic Force Microscopy)Sharp probe tip physically "feels" the surface; measures force between tip and sample3D surface topography, roughness, mechanical properties, particle sizeWorks in air, vacuum, or liquid; no vacuum needed
STM (Scanning Tunneling Microscopy)Measures quantum tunneling current between conducting tip and sampleElectronic properties, individual atom positions, local density of statesCan image and move individual atoms; only for conductors
Cryo-EM (Cryo-Electron Microscopy)Samples flash-frozen in vitreous ice; TEM used to image3D structure of biological macromolecules (proteins, viruses) in near-native stateNo staining needed; preserves biological structure; won Nobel Prize 2017
HRTEM (High-Resolution TEM)Phase-contrast imaging of transmitted electronsInternal structure, lattice fringes, crystal defects at atomic scaleSub-Ångström resolution; visualizes individual atomic columns

AI & Machine Learning in Nano Analysis

The massive datasets generated by modern nano instruments are increasingly analyzed by artificial intelligence:

  • Automatic defect detection: AI identifies defects in TEM/SEM images faster and more accurately than humans
  • Property prediction: Machine learning predicts material properties from composition/structure data
  • Image analysis: Deep learning segments nanoparticles, measures size distributions automatically
  • Spectroscopy: AI identifies phases and impurities in XRD/Raman patterns
  • Autonomous synthesis: AI-controlled robotic synthesis for nanoparticle optimization
Trend: Multimodal Analysis

Modern nanochemistry increasingly combines multiple techniques on the same sample — e.g., XRD for phase + TEM for morphology + EDX for composition + Raman for bonding — giving a complete, multi-dimensional picture of the nanomaterial.

Section Summary

  • AFM: physical surface probing → 3D topography and mechanical properties (works in liquid)
  • STM: quantum tunneling → atomic-scale electronic mapping (conductors only)
  • Cryo-EM: flash-frozen biological samples → 3D protein/virus structure (Nobel Prize 2017)
  • HRTEM: sub-Ångström lattice fringe imaging → crystal defects at atomic scale
  • AI: automated defect detection, pattern recognition, property prediction in nano datasets

Green Nanomaterials & Nanotechnology

Eco-conscious synthesis · Biosynthesis · Sustainable applications
Definition

Green Nanotechnology merges green chemistry principles with nanoscience to create sustainable, non-toxic, and environmentally friendly nanomaterials using renewable resources, non-toxic chemicals, and energy-efficient processes.

Key Principles of Green Nanomaterials

☘️

Low/No Toxicity

Non-toxic to humans and ecosystems throughout life cycle

♻️

Biodegradable/Recyclable

Breaks down naturally or can be reclaimed after use

🌱

Sustainable Raw Materials

Plant extracts, biopolymers, and renewable biological sources

⚡

Energy-Efficient Synthesis

Minimal energy input; avoids harsh conditions (high temp/pressure)

🗑️

Minimal Waste

Atom-efficient reactions; avoids hazardous byproducts

Green Synthesis Methods

1. Biological Methods (Biosynthesis) — Most Important

Using living organisms or their extracts to reduce metal ions into nanoparticles. This is called "green synthesis" or "biogenic synthesis".

Biological SourceMechanismExample NM Produced
Plant extracts (neem, aloe vera, tulsi, green tea)Phytochemicals (flavonoids, terpenoids) act as reducing and capping agentsAg NPs, Au NPs, ZnO NPs
Bacteria (e.g., Pseudomonas, Bacillus)Enzymatic reduction of metal ionsAg NPs, Fe₃O₄ NPs
Fungi (e.g., Aspergillus)Extracellular enzyme secretion reduces metal ionsAg NPs, Au NPs
AlgaePhotosynthetic pigments act as reductantsAu NPs, Ag NPs

2. Microwave-Assisted Synthesis

Microwave irradiation provides uniform, rapid heating throughout the reaction mixture. Benefits: fast reaction times (minutes vs hours), uniform particle size, reduced energy consumption, and less solvent needed.

3. Ultrasound (Sonochemistry)

High-frequency ultrasound creates cavitation — tiny bubbles that collapse violently, generating intense local pressure (>1000 atm) and temperature (>5000°C) for microseconds. This drives nanoparticle formation without bulk heating.

4. Supercritical Fluids

CO₂ above its critical point (31°C, 73 atm) behaves as both liquid (good solvent) and gas (easy removal). Replaces harmful organic solvents. Produces pure nanoparticles with no solvent residue.

Fabrication of Nanomaterials

Top-down vs Bottom-up approaches · Techniques · Comparison

Fabrication means creating nanomaterials with structures between 1–100 nm. There are two fundamentally opposite philosophies:

⬇️ Top-Down Approach
  • "Cutting from big to small"
  • Bulk material is physically or chemically broken down into nanoscale structures
  • Like sculpting — removing material to reveal the nanostructure
  • Useful for large-area nanostructures (semiconductors, chips)
⬆️ Bottom-Up Approach
  • "Building from atoms to nano"
  • Nanoparticles assembled from individual atoms or molecules
  • Like building a brick wall — atomic precision
  • High purity, precise size control, uniform products

Top-Down Techniques

1. Mechanical Milling (Ball Milling)

High-energy balls grind bulk material into nanoparticles inside a rotating drum. The repeated fracturing, cold-welding, and re-fracturing reduces particle size to the nanoscale.

  • Product: Metallic and ceramic nanoparticles
  • Advantage: Simple, scalable, no chemicals needed
  • Limitation: Wide size distribution, possible contamination from mill

2. Lithography

Used primarily in semiconductor manufacturing to create nanocircuits and nanopatterns on substrates.

  • Photolithography: UV light through patterned mask exposes photoresist → etched to create patterns (resolution ~100 nm)
  • Electron-Beam Lithography (EBL): Focused electron beam writes patterns directly → resolution down to ~2 nm
  • Nanoimprint Lithography: Hard mold pressed into polymer → transfers nanopattern

3. Chemical/Plasma Etching

Removing layers of material using reactive chemicals or plasma. Allows precise depth control. Used in semiconductor fabrication to create nanoscale trenches, pillars, and features.

Bottom-Up Techniques

1. Sol-Gel Process

Metal alkoxide precursor in solution
→
Hydrolysis & condensation
→
Sol (colloidal solution)
→
Gel formation
→
Drying/Calcination
→
Nanoparticles/nanoporous materials

Produces silica, titania, alumina nanomaterials. Excellent for controlling composition and pore structure.

2. Chemical Vapor Deposition (CVD)

Gaseous precursor molecules flow into a reactor, react at the hot substrate surface, and deposit as a solid thin film or nanostructure.

  • Used to grow carbon nanotubes, graphene, and semiconductor nanostructures
  • Extremely precise thickness control (monolayer precision)
  • Used in electronics industry for chip manufacturing

3. Self-Assembly

Molecules spontaneously organize themselves into ordered nanostructures driven by non-covalent forces (hydrogen bonding, van der Waals, electrostatic). No external direction needed — the molecular structure programs the assembly.

  • Examples: DNA origami, lipid bilayers, block copolymer nanostructures
  • Used to create complex 3D nanoarchitectures

4. Precipitation (Chemical Reduction)

Metal ions in solution are chemically reduced to form zero-valent metal nanoparticles. A capping agent (stabilizer) is added to prevent agglomeration and control size.

Example Reaction: Silver NPs
AgNO₃ + NaBH₄ → Ag⁰ (nanoparticles) + NaNO₃ + B compounds

Ag⁺ ions + reducing agent → Ag⁰ atoms → nucleation → growth → Ag NPs

  • Bottom-up: atoms → nano (sol-gel, CVD, self-assembly, precipitation) — better size control and purity
  • Sol-gel: metal alkoxide → hydrolysis → gel → nanoparticles
  • CVD: gaseous precursors deposit as solid nanostructures; used for CNTs and graphene
  • Self-assembly: molecules spontaneously organize via non-covalent forces

Nanocomposites

Matrix + Nanofiller · Types · Properties · Applications
Definition

Nanocomposites are materials made by combining nanoparticles (nanofiller) with bulk materials (matrix) — such as polymers, metals, or ceramics — to create a material with improved or entirely new properties that neither component possesses alone.

Basic Structure

Matrix (Base Material)
  • Provides the bulk structure and holds everything together
  • Can be: Polymer (plastic), Metal, or Ceramic
  • Properties: lightweight (polymer), strong (metal), heat-resistant (ceramic)
Nanofiller (Reinforcement)
  • Nanoparticles dispersed throughout matrix
  • Can be: CNTs, graphene, nanoclay, nano-silica, quantum dots
  • Very small amounts (1–5 wt%) create dramatic property improvements

Types of Nanocomposites

1. Polymer Matrix Nanocomposites (PMNCs)

Most widely studied and commercially used. A polymer matrix reinforced with nanofillers.

  • Matrix: plastics (nylon, epoxy, polyethylene)
  • Fillers: nanoclay, carbon nanotubes, graphene, nano-silica
  • Uses: packaging, automotive parts, flexible electronics, aerospace
  • Even 1–3 wt% nanoclay dramatically improves gas barrier properties

2. Metal Matrix Nanocomposites (MMNCs)

Metal matrix with ceramic or carbon nanoparticle reinforcement.

  • Matrix: aluminum, titanium, copper
  • Fillers: SiC NPs, CNTs, Al₂O₃ NPs
  • Uses: aerospace, automotive, defense (high strength, wear resistance)

3. Ceramic Matrix Nanocomposites (CMNCs)

Ceramic matrix with nano-reinforcements to overcome brittleness.

  • Matrix: Al₂O₃, Si₃N₄, ZrO₂
  • Fillers: CNTs, SiC whiskers, ZrO₂ NPs
  • Uses: cutting tools, thermal barrier coatings, biomedical implants

4. Carbon-Based Nanocomposites

Graphene, CNTs, or fullerenes as fillers in any matrix type.

  • Exceptional electrical and thermal conductivity enhancement
  • Uses: sensors, flexible electronics, EMI shielding, batteries

5. Hybrid Nanocomposites

Two or more different types of nanofillers in one matrix.

  • Example: CNTs + nanoclay in polymer → combines electrical conductivity with gas barrier
  • Uses: advanced aerospace, biomedical tools

Properties of Nanocomposites

💪

High Strength-to-Weight

CNT-reinforced composites can be stronger than steel at a fraction of the weight

🌡️

Thermal Conductivity

Graphene fillers dramatically improve heat dissipation in polymer matrices

⚡

Electrical Properties

Carbon-based fillers create conductive pathways in insulating polymers (percolation threshold)

🛡️

Barrier Properties

Nanoclay platelets create tortuous path for gases → dramatically reduced permeability

🦠

Antibacterial

Ag NP-loaded composites inhibit bacterial growth on surfaces

🔄

Chemical Resistance

Nano-silica reinforcement improves resistance to solvents and corrosion

Why Such Small Amounts Work So Well

At 1–5 wt% filler, nanoparticles create enormous interfacial area between filler and matrix. This large interface is where all the property enhancement happens. With nano-sized fillers, you get orders of magnitude more interface than with micro-sized fillers at the same weight fraction.

  • Applications: automotive, aerospace, electronics, biomedical, food packaging

Separation Science in Nanochemistry

Isolating and purifying nanomaterials from complex mixtures
Definition

Separation Science involves techniques and strategies to separate, isolate, purify, or characterize nanomaterials — like nanoparticles, nanotubes, quantum dots, and nanoclusters — from mixtures, unreacted precursors, or unwanted byproducts.

Why Separation is Critical in Nanochemistry

Nanomaterials often exist in complex mixtures after synthesis. Size, shape, surface properties, and composition critically affect their performance. Without precise separation:

  • Products may be impure or inconsistent in properties
  • Toxic or unstable byproducts might remain
  • Applications in medicine, electronics, or catalysis may fail due to heterogeneity
  • Biological studies require mono-disperse (uniform size) nanoparticles

Separation Techniques in Nanochemistry

1. Centrifugation

Exploits differences in density and size. Sample placed in centrifuge tube and spun at high speed. Larger/denser particles sediment faster.

  • Standard centrifugation: separates large aggregates and unbound reagents
  • Ultracentrifugation (100,000–500,000 × g): separates particles differing by just a few nm
  • Density gradient centrifugation: particles band at their isopycnic point in a sucrose/CsCl gradient — separates by size AND density simultaneously

2. Membrane Filtration / Ultrafiltration

Semipermeable membranes with defined pore sizes allow selective passage of molecules based on size exclusion.

  • Nanoporous membranes (0.1–100 nm pores) enable size-selective separation
  • Removes small molecules, free ligands, ions from nanoparticle suspensions
  • Dialysis bags use this principle for gentle, prolonged purification

3. Chromatography

TypeSeparation PrincipleApplication
Size Exclusion Chromatography (SEC)Separates by hydrodynamic volume — larger particles elute firstSize-sorting nanoparticles, polymer-NP conjugates
Affinity ChromatographySeparates based on specific surface ligand-receptor interactionsAntibody-functionalized NPs, targeted drug carriers
Thin Layer Chromatography (TLC)Surface-bound molecules travel different distances on plateQuality control of surface-modified NPs

4. Electrophoresis

Uses electric field to separate charged particles. Charged nanoparticles move toward oppositely charged electrode at rates depending on charge-to-size ratio.

  • Gel electrophoresis (agarose/polyacrylamide): separates nanoparticles and nanoclusters by size and charge — like DNA electrophoresis
  • Can resolve gold nanoclusters differing by just one atom!

5. Dialysis

Sample placed inside semipermeable membrane tube, submerged in large volume of buffer. Small molecules (free ligands, ions, solvents) diffuse out while nanoparticles (too large to pass membrane) remain inside. Slow but gentle — preserves nanoparticle stability.

Real Example: Gold Nanoparticle Purification

After synthesis of Au NPs: (1) Centrifugation removes large aggregates and precipitates them. (2) Dialysis removes free citrate ligands and unreacted HAuCl₄. (3) Size-exclusion chromatography refines into narrow size fractions. Result: pure, mono-disperse Au NPs ready for application.

Section Summary

  • Separation science purifies and isolates NMs from complex post-synthesis mixtures
  • Centrifugation: size/density-based sedimentation; ultracentrifugation resolves nm differences
  • Membrane filtration/dialysis: size exclusion removes small molecules from NP suspensions
  • SEC chromatography: separates by hydrodynamic volume (size); affinity chromatography: by surface chemistry
  • Gel electrophoresis: separates charged NPs by size/charge — can resolve single-atom differences in nanoclusters

Catalysis in Nanochemistry

Nanocatalysts · Efficiency · Types · Examples
Definition

Catalysis in nanochemistry refers to the use of nanomaterials (nanoparticles) to accelerate chemical reactions, often with greater efficiency, selectivity, and reusability than traditional bulk catalysts.

Why Nanoparticles are Such Excellent Catalysts

Three nanoscale properties make nanoparticles exceptional catalysts:

  • High surface area-to-volume ratio: More active surface sites per gram than bulk material
  • Tunable surface chemistry: Surface can be modified to be selective for specific reactions
  • Quantum size effects: Electronic structure of NPs differs from bulk — different (often better) catalytic activity
The Gold Paradox — Most Stunning Nano Example

Bulk gold is completely inert — that's why gold jewelry doesn't tarnish. But gold nanoparticles below 5 nm become extremely powerful catalysts for CO oxidation even at room temperature (bulk gold requires 600°C+). This dramatic property change is purely a nanoscale phenomenon.

Types of Nanocatalysis

TypeDescriptionExampleAdvantage
HomogeneousNanocatalyst in same phase as reactants (usually solution)Colloidal gold NPs in solution for oxidation reactionsBetter contact between catalyst and reactants; easy to control
HeterogeneousNanocatalyst in different phase from reactants (solid catalyst + liquid/gas reactants)Pt NPs on carbon for hydrogenation; TiO₂ NPs for photocatalysisEasy catalyst separation and reuse; industrially practical

Important Nanocatalysts and Their Reactions

Gold Nanoparticles (Au NPs)

  • CO oxidation at room temperature (critical for car catalytic converters)
  • Selective hydrogenation of unsaturated compounds
  • Must be below 5 nm and supported on metal oxide (TiO₂, Fe₂O₃)

Titanium Dioxide Nanoparticles (TiO₂)

  • Photocatalysis: Absorbs UV light → generates electron-hole pairs → degrade organic pollutants
  • Used for dye degradation in wastewater treatment
  • Self-cleaning surfaces and glass coatings
  • Water splitting for hydrogen production (future clean energy)
TiO₂ Photocatalysis Mechanism

e⁻ + O₂ → •O₂⁻ (superoxide) — another oxidant
•OH + organic pollutant → CO₂ + H₂O (degradation)

Platinum Nanoparticles (Pt NPs)

  • Hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) in water electrolysis
  • Oxygen reduction reaction (ORR) in fuel cells
  • Hydrogenation of alkenes and alkynes

Applications of Nanocatalysis

🏭

Industrial

Chemical synthesis, petroleum refining, ammonia production (Haber process improvements)

🌍

Environmental

TiO₂ photocatalysis for wastewater treatment and air purification

🔋

Energy

Pt NPs in fuel cells; photocatalytic water splitting for H₂ generation

💊

Selective synthesis of drug molecules with high enantiomeric purity

Section Summary

  • Nanocatalysts: high SA/V ratio + tunable surface + quantum effects = superior catalytic activity
  • Homogeneous catalysis: same phase; Heterogeneous: different phase (solid NP + liquid/gas reactants)
  • Au NPs <5 nm: catalyze CO oxidation at room temp (inert in bulk)
  • TiO₂ NPs: UV light → •OH radical → destroys organic pollutants (photocatalysis)
  • Pt NPs: hydrogen evolution, oxygen reduction in fuel cells

Nano Optics (Nanophotonics)

Light-matter interaction at the nanoscale · Surface plasmons · Applications
Definition

Nano optics (nanophotonics) deals with how light interacts with matter at the nanometer scale. At this level, light behaves differently due to quantum effects, confinement, and surface plasmon resonance.

Key Phenomena in Nano Optics

1. Surface Plasmon Resonance (SPR)

When light hits metal nanoparticles, it excites collective oscillations of free electrons called plasmons. The resonance condition depends on particle size, shape, and surrounding medium.

  • Gold NPs (~20 nm): absorb at ~520 nm → appear red in solution
  • Silver NPs: absorb at ~400 nm → appear yellow
  • Shape change shifts resonance: nanorods have two resonance peaks (transverse and longitudinal)

2. Quantum Confinement Effects

In quantum dots, electrons are confined in all three dimensions. Energy levels are discrete — photons absorbed/emitted at specific, size-dependent wavelengths. Quantum dots have near-perfect, tunable fluorescence.

3. Near-Field Enhancement

Metal nanoparticles concentrate electromagnetic field intensity in tiny "hot spots" at their surface. This enhances spectroscopy signals by 10⁶–10¹⁴× in techniques like Surface-Enhanced Raman Spectroscopy (SERS).

Applications of Nano Optics

ApplicationMechanismExample
High-res biomedical imagingQD fluorescence, plasmonic enhancementQuantum dot-labeled cells and tissues
Quantum communicationSingle-photon emission from QDsQuantum cryptography devices
Energy-efficient LEDsQD narrow emission bandQLED displays (Samsung, LG)
Data storage using lightNear-field optical writing below diffraction limitNano-optical data storage
Optical computersLight (photons) replace electrons for signal processingFuture optical computing chips
SERS sensingNear-field electromagnetic enhancementSingle-molecule detection

Section Summary

  • Nano optics = light-matter interaction at the nanoscale; phenomena include SPR, quantum confinement, near-field enhancement
  • SPR: electron oscillations in metal NPs → size/shape-dependent color (Au NPs = red)
  • Quantum dots: size-tunable fluorescence → QLED displays, bioimaging
  • SERS: plasmonic near-field enhancement → single-molecule Raman detection
  • Applications: bioimaging, LEDs, quantum communication, optical computing

Drug Discovery & Delivery in Nanochemistry

Nanosensors · Targeted delivery · Nanocarriers · Real examples

Nanochemistry is transforming medicine by enabling smarter, safer, and more effective medications through nanoscale design.

Part 1: Drug Discovery via Nanochemistry

In drug discovery, nanochemistry helps scientists design, identify, and test new drug candidates more efficiently at the molecular level.

Nanosensors for Drug Screening

Detect biomolecules and screen thousands of drug candidates rapidly. Nanowire biosensors can detect single molecules of disease biomarkers, enabling ultra-early diagnosis.

Nanoparticles as Probes

Quantum dots and fluorescent NPs used to study drug-target interactions in real time inside living cells. Track where a drug goes inside the body, how it binds to its target, and what causes side effects.

Nanostructured Drug Platforms

Aid in understanding how drugs behave inside cells — membrane penetration, intracellular distribution, degradation pathways.

Lab-on-a-Chip Systems

Miniature nanotech devices that perform hundreds of drug tests simultaneously on tiny samples (~nanoliters). Dramatically accelerates drug screening — from years to weeks.

Part 2: Drug Delivery via Nanochemistry

Nanochemistry enables targeted and controlled delivery of drugs to specific tissues or cells, dramatically improving treatment and reducing side effects.

Key Advantages of Nanoscale Drug Delivery

🎯

Targeted Delivery

Nanoparticles functionalized with antibodies, peptides, or aptamers that recognize and bind only diseased cells (e.g., cancer cells), sparing healthy tissue

⏱️

Controlled Release

Drug released slowly over hours/days/weeks. Reduces dosing frequency. Can be triggered by pH, temperature, or light

💧

Improved Solubility

~40% of new drugs are poorly water-soluble. Nanocarriers encapsulate and deliver them effectively

🛡️

Reduced Toxicity

Less drug reaches healthy tissues → dramatically reduced side effects (critical for chemotherapy)

Real-World Examples

Real Examples — Products on Market

Doxil (1995): Liposome-encapsulated doxorubicin for cancer treatment. The PEGylated liposomes avoid immune clearance (stealth liposomes) and accumulate in tumors via EPR (Enhanced Permeability and Retention) effect.

Abraxane: Albumin-bound paclitaxel nanoparticles (~130 nm). Albumin naturally concentrates in tumors. Better than conventional Taxol (toxic solvent eliminated).

Pfizer & Moderna COVID-19 Vaccines: Use lipid nanoparticles (LNPs) to deliver mRNA encoding the spike protein. LNPs protect mRNA from degradation, enable cellular uptake, and release mRNA inside cells. Revolutionary platform technology.

The EPR Effect — Why Nanoparticles Accumulate in Tumors

Enhanced Permeability and Retention (EPR) Effect

Tumor blood vessels are abnormally leaky (large pores, 100–800 nm) compared to normal blood vessels (<10 nm pores). Nanoparticles (20–200 nm) selectively leak into tumors through these large pores. Once inside, poor lymphatic drainage in tumors causes NPs to be retained. This passive targeting is the foundation of most cancer nanomedicine.

  • Nanocarriers: liposomes, polymeric NPs, dendrimers, gold nanoshells, LNPs, mesoporous silica
  • Real examples: Doxil (liposome cancer drug), Abraxane (albumin NP), Pfizer/Moderna COVID vaccines (LNP-mRNA)
  • EPR effect: tumor leaky vessels (100–800 nm pores) allow selective NP accumulation

📡 Advanced Imaging Trends

  • AFM: tip feels surface → 3D topography + mechanical properties

  • HRTEM: atomic-column imaging, lattice fringes, crystal defects
  • AI: automated defect detection + property prediction from nano data
